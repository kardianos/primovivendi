# Models: wrong vs incomplete — astronomy chain (writer analysis)

**Use:** Part 1 section M.  
**Claim:** Predictive success does not certify full ontological truth. Incomplete models can progress.

---

## 1. Definitions (operational)

### Wrong model
- Makes claims that fail under appropriate, repeatable tests.
- No expanding domain of reliable success under honest scrutiny.
- **Class example:** Alchemical recipes that promise gold from base metals under stated conditions and never deliver under controlled trial.

### Incomplete model
- Delivers reliable success inside a stated domain (range of speeds, masses, precisions, timescales).
- Fails or needs correction outside that domain.
- Successor theories typically **recover** the old successes as a limiting case.

### Useful calculation scheme
- Produces numbers good enough for a purpose (calendar, navigation, aiming).
- May rest on a physical story that is false or strained.
- **Must not** be equated with “the full true account of nature.”

---

## 2. Example A — Pre-Kepler positional astronomy vs Kepler

### What older schemes could do
- Babylonian and later Greek/medieval/Renaissance table methods, and the **Ptolemaic** geometric toolkit (deferent, epicycle, equant, etc.), were refined to predict **planetary positions** and lunar phenomena with accuracy useful for calendars and astrology/astronomy of their eras.
- Adding parameters could reduce residual error for observers of that period.

### What was wrong or strained in the *story*
- Dominant physical pictures included Earth-centered cosmos, crystalline spheres, and epicycle machinery treated as how the heavens *are*, not merely as calculation devices.
- Saving the appearances was prioritized; the true orbital dynamics (sun-focused Keplerian motion later) were not the ontology.

### Kepler (early 17th c.)
- Using Tycho Brahe’s observations, Kepler formulated laws including:
  - Planets move in **ellipses** with the sun at one focus.
  - Equal areas in equal times.
  - Period–distance relation (harmonic law).
- These improved **correspondence** between mathematical description and what bodies do, with fewer ad hoc patches for the same class of phenomena.

### Logical point for the book
1. Older methods could be **relatively accurate in results**.  
2. That accuracy did **not** validate the full epicycle ontology.  
3. Therefore: “X produces good Y” ≠ “X is a fully valid model of reality.”  
4. Prefer theories that survive harder data and unify more cases with less special pleading.

### Fairness
- Do not mock pre-Kepler astronomers; they were rigorous within their tools.
- The virtue is **correction under better evidence**, not contempt for ancestors.

## 3. Example B — Kepler → Newton → special relativity

### Kepler
- Empirical regularities of planetary motion.
- Not yet a general dynamics of all terrestrial and celestial motion.

### Newton (*Principia*, 1687)
- Laws of motion + universal gravitation (inverse square).
- **Builds on** Kepler: elliptical orbits emerge as consequences under Newtonian assumptions (idealized two-body, etc.).
- Wider domain: falling bodies, moons, approximate tides, new predictions.

### Special relativity (Einstein, 1905) and gravity beyond Newton
- Newtonian mechanics is **incomplete** for speeds comparable to light and for frames where absolute time fails.
- Full gravitational theory requires more than Newton (general relativity, 1915) for strong fields / precision tests (e.g. Mercury perihelion historically; modern GPS corrections involve relativistic physics).
- Newton is **not** alchemy-wrong: engineers use Newtonian models daily inside the envelope.

### Progressive structure
```
empirical regularities (Kepler)
    → dynamical unification (Newton)
        → deeper kinematics/dynamics (relativity)
```
Each step: keep earned successes where they still hold; extend domain; do not hermetically freeze the prior ontology.

---

## 4. Tie to procedural symmetry

1. Same tests for favored and unfavored theories.  
2. Prestige, sacredness, or political utility do not exempt a model from failed predictions.  
3. “Our side’s model predicted something once” is not a one-way valve against disconfirming data.  
4. Humility: your best model may be incomplete.  
5. Non-relativism: incomplete ≠ equal to wrong; ranking by correspondence remains possible.

---

## 5. Social/policy transfer (careful)

- A policy can show short-run “good numbers” (like a wrong astronomical story that still tables positions) while the underlying model of humans or incentives is false.  
- Hermetic politics: redefine every failure as “not real implementation.”  
- Scale and generation (Part 1 H10): numbers that work for a pilot fail when a whole population adopts the premise.  
- Do **not** claim physics proves a party program. Claim only: **the same epistemic virtues apply**.
