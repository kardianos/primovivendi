## Existing text (as of 2026-07-17)

### Prefix (¶2a + ¶2b opening)

> A common mistake starts from a true point (no one chose their birth) and slides away from *you, your family, your lineage, your situation*. Unchosen facts are treated as morally weightless. Care for your own is treated as prejudice. The self is redescribed as a generic person who could have held any slot, with no real parents and no real people. Justice is redescribed as what that generic person would pick without knowing their place—and often, in practice, as if many would simply pick candyland: goods without burden, claims without the household and line that make goods possible.
>
> Reject that slide **from the positive**: if there is no generic person—only you, your family, lineage, and situation—then a morality built for a generic person is built for nobody real.

### Sentence under review

> People also differ: genetic potential for intelligence is largely set; environment and self-determination shape how far it is reached; all three vary. Capacity to track second- and third-order effects (what keeps prosperity and freedom in place) varies.

### Suffix (rest of ¶2b)

> Design that pretends everyone is the same foresighted cooperator—or that "choose any arrangement" would not yield mass demand for free stuff without responsibility—will be gamed and will hollow the platform. Thrival needs people who form, produce, and transmit *in their actual chains*, not only claim as tokens. Same rules in a shared scope remain necessary among those who share a public order. The fiction that you are a generic person does not.

## Facts and logic (raw material, no packaging)

### Material basis

- Mind is what a brain does; no testable evidence of cognition without a working brain.
- Genes encode part of the mind: the species-typical architecture is gene-guided. Across every culture humans recognize faces and expressions, acquire language, fear the same classes of things, feel the same basic emotions. Shared human nature across cultures is itself evidence of genetic shaping of the mind.
- Genes do not encode the mind's contents: which language, which beliefs, which skills. Those come from formation and experience.
- Brain development runs through regulation, developmental noise, and experience-driven plasticity (synaptic pruning, myelination). DNA biases and bounds; it does not print contents.

### Variation

- Every biological trait in a population varies. Variance is the default state and the raw material of selection; uniformity is the anomaly that would need explaining.
- Cognitive potential varies between individuals within any population — same structure as variation in height, build, or hair color.
- Variation exists even within one family: siblings of the same parents differ measurably. This is not a claim about lineages or peoples.
- Foresight — the capacity to track second- and third-order effects — varies between people as well.

### What the evidence says

- Twin/family studies estimate within-population heritability of adult cognitive ability at roughly half or more (0.5–0.8) in wealthy countries.
- DNA-based estimates are lower; the gap ("missing heritability") is openly debated. Polygenic scores currently predict ~10–15% of variance.
- Verdict of mainstream behavioral genetics: not pseudoscience, not settled to precision either.

### What heritability means and does not mean

- Heritability is a population statistic, not an individual measure. It bounds no named person's future.
- It shifts with environment; deprivation lowers it.
- Heritable ≠ unchangeable: heritable traits still respond to conditions (nutrition moves height; lenses fix myopia).
- Within-group heritability licenses no inference about the causes of between-group differences. The claim at issue is individual-level.
- Environment, formation, and self-determination decide how much of the potential is reached. Skills transmit only by teaching, every generation, regardless of potential.

### Logic and entailments

- If minds are material, brains gene-guided, and all traits vary, then inborn cognitive variation follows — unless brains alone are exempt. The blank slate is the extraordinary claim; the burden of proof sits on it.
- "People differ" without "some difference is inborn" is absorbable by the blank slate ("yes — all formation; remake the institutions"). The inborn clause is what forecloses that move.
- The thesis the book needs is non-interchangeability: no two persons are fungible units. Genetics is evidence for that thesis, not the thesis itself.
- Institutions designed for fungible units get gamed by the variance they deny; equal procedure cannot deliver equal outcomes without bending procedure (ties B5, E10).
- Non-interchangeability of ability is not non-interchangeability of standing: same rules still bind within scope. This is a design input, not a moral rank.
- Fully compatible with the book's formation emphasis: potential is inborn; achievement is formed. Both, neither alone.
