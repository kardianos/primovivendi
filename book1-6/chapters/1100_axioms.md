## The axioms

The five questions are on the table; here are the answers this book holds. Rival answers exist and are held by serious people, so each answer below is stated next to its rivals rather than presented as the only option. The first two answers describe the world and what makes a claim true. The third names the good. The fourth and fifth order duty and justice under that good. One more answer, about the humans these answers are for, closes the set.

**Reality is real.** There is a world that exists whether anyone likes it, names it, or votes on it. Rival answers say otherwise: one holds that a person's experience is the world, another that agreement among people settles what is. Both fail at the same place. Bodies need food, shelter, and heat, and those needs are met or not met by what actually happens, never by what is declared.

The test is physical. A village can vote that the old bridge is sound; the vote does not pull the rot from the timbers. The cart rolls onto the span, and the timbers carry the weight or drop the cart into the river. A harvest comes in or fails, whatever was said at the planting feast. A knife cuts the rope or does not, whatever the seller promised. Wish, story, and power can move people to act; none of the three rewrites a load-bearing fact. Public life must treat the world as independent of wish.

**Truth is correspondence to reality.** A claim is true when the claim matches what is. Rivals name other makers of truth: decree, power, the consensus of the group, what serves the cause, a story that agrees with itself. Those tests serve the holder of the decree or the cause; cooperation needs a test that serves no one in particular. People must plant, build, heal, and defend together. Shared work needs shared knowledge, shared knowledge needs a shared test, and correspondence is that test: does the claim track the world? Models are provisional, and the world is the judge. When a model and the world disagree, keep the world. Even a model that predicts well can carry a wrong or incomplete story inside it; the next section takes that up.

**Life is good.** Human life should continue and continue well: survival, and then order, skill, health, building, and transmission past bare continuation. This answer is affirmed, not deduced. No chain of pure description can force a person to value life, so this book states the value openly and gives its motivations. First: without living people, no value, justice, beauty, or kindness has a holder. Second: a rival ethic recognizes duties only to existing subjects who can consent or be wronged. On that ranking a dead earth and a living earth come out equal, because no subject is wronged by emptiness. That result is coherent only if the existence of life is not itself a good, and this book rejects the result. Third: every living person is the end of a chain of parents who continued life.

Three layers stay distinct under this axiom. The axiom itself: life continuing and continuing well is good. The corollaries: institutions must be able to persist; no one should celebrate a culture's own extinction; honor the chain of parents by transmitting language, skill, law, and formation, not by living off the work of parents while despising that work. Participation: have children or raise them up by real means, and structure life so that continuation is honorable and common. Participation is duty and honor under the affirmed axiom, not a syllogism squeezed out of the bare fact "I exist," and not a draft into every profession.

**Tiered priority is mandatory.** Duties are ranked: strongest at the self and the household, then family, neighbors, community, polity, and the wider world. The reason is limit. Time, knowledge, and strength are finite, and a parent knows the child's cough better than a stranger's need on another continent. A person who declares unlimited duty ends by performing none. So this book ranks duty: outer claims stay real but weaker, and this book does not reverse the order as a fashion virtue. Ranking is not hatred of strangers; no duty vanishes past the door, and the nation is not the only real unit by metaphysics. The tiers mark practical reach under limits. Scopes are the working form of this answer: a role, a membership, a jurisdiction inside which procedure can actually run. Borders, household authority, and professional roles are scopes of that kind. The rival answer declares equal duty to everyone; declared insistently and performed nowhere, that declaration becomes pretense while its holders neglect the near.

**Justice is procedural symmetry.** Within an honest scope, the same rules and methods of judgment apply to each person in the same position. Two rivals answer otherwise. Outcome symmetry holds that justice is the same end-state for everyone and treats disparity as proof of injustice. Value symmetry holds that justice is equal return in kind, a ledger in which injury answers injury. The chain runs: limited, self-interested people must cooperate; cooperation needs stable expectations; stable expectations need the same rules within a defined set of people and roles. Outcome symmetry must apply unequal procedure to reach its equal end-states, and the work of equalizing breeds a class of officials who live by it. Value symmetry faces backward and has no end, because each injury answered becomes the next injury to answer. The same form holds across domains: in law, one rule for the same case; in inquiry, one critical standard for every model; in conduct, the reversal test (would you still accept the rule if the positions were swapped); between generations, the procedure you wish had been used toward you (life given, formation given, a platform to stand on). What this answer rejects is one-way rules for favored groups, and a mercy that never names the wrong, which is not mercy but permissiveness.

**The answers work together.** Truth without life as the good becomes trivia or a means to death. Life as the good without truth becomes wish passed off as policy. Tiers without procedure become clan favoritism and exemption. Procedure without tiers becomes a universal claim no limited person can keep. The set needs no product name; the working together is the point.

**The humans are limited, self-interested, and not naturally good.** This is the working premise under the five answers, this book's answer to the second question. People are limited in time, knowledge, and reach. People are self-interested in the descriptive sense: oriented to their own survival and near kin, not commanded to be egoists. People are not naturally good: virtue is trained and kept by parents, teachers, and the person who practices it, not shipped as factory settings. The rival answers hold that a person is a blank slate to be written, or naturally good and restorable by remaking the whole social order. This book builds for the humans who exist: habits and rules that channel self-interest, with no waiting for people to become good on their own. The humans these axioms are for are also particular, and this book opens that claim fully later. There is no generic person, only you, tied to your family, their lineage, and your situation; the axioms are for that creature, not for an interchangeable token.

**Charge.** These are the answers held here: reality is real; truth is correspondence to reality; life should continue and continue well; duty is tiered; justice is the same procedure inside an honest scope; the humans are limited, self-interested, and not naturally good.
