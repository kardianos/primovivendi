## The practice close

Everything argued in this book has to fit in one hand. The axioms, the tests, the history: they earn their keep only at the moment a person decides something that costs. This last chapter is that moment, repeated. First a short list that every serious decision must pass. Then a few decisions run through it, at rising stakes. Then the smallest unit of any people, the reader, and one act. Then the charge that closes the book.

### The precepts matrix

A serious decision needs a fixed short list, not a mood. Write the list once, before the decision arrives, because the wanting that comes with a live decision edits any list it can reach. Deciding what to plant is beneath the list. Deciding whether to sell the field is not. Six filters, and a seventh for decisions that outlive you. Fail any one, and you stop or you revise. You do not argue a failed filter into a pass.

The filters:

1. **Reality and correspondence.** The decision rests on claims about the world. Is the load-bearing claim testable, and does it pass? Suppose the plan assumes that sales will recover by spring; that is testable, so wait for the evidence, or stake less. Suppose instead the plan assumes that the policy is on the right side of history; that is not testable, so it buys nothing and should carry nothing.

2. **Wrong versus incomplete.** Is a useful wrong story being treated as final? A story that fails everywhere it is tried has earned no domain. A story that works inside limits may keep those limits and its successes at the edges. Build on the second kind. Never mortgage a decade to the first, however well the first story flatters you.

3. **Form versus substance.** Is the decision a redesign of costume, or a denial of constraint? Forms may change while the problem remains; the test is whether the problem reappears when the official form is banned. Denying the constraint is not redesign. Denial is refusal dressed as design.

4. **Life and continuing well.** Does the decision serve continuing, and continuing well? And which is it building, a cushion or a free-ride? A cushion is a time-bound bridge over a short shock. The road has a gap where the flood took it; someone lays planks across; people walk who can see the far side; when the road crew fills the gap, the planks come up. A bridge with no far side and no date for the planks to come up is not a cushion. It is a way of life, billed to whoever maintains the planks.

5. **Tiers.** Who is owed first? Duties run outward: self and household, then family, neighbors, community, polity, world. A decision that starves the near rings to feed the far ones has put the far ahead of the near, and the near rings will pay for it, in silence, for years.

6. **Procedural symmetry.** Same rules inside the honest scope? If the decision needs a quiet exemption for your own side, your own child, your own ministry, it fails. The exemption is the decision confessing.

7. **Long horizon and scale, for the large decisions.** What if many people hold this for decades? Some failures hide at small scale and short time and stand up only at the size of populations and generations. Ask the question before the generations answer it.

All must pass. That is the whole discipline. And when you catch yourself stretching a fact to force a pass, treating a maybe as a yes at filter one, or renaming a free-ride as a cushion at filter four, take the stretch itself as the answer. The desire is driving, and the list is doing its job by refusing.

Keep the tool light. The list fits on a card in a pocket, and an honest run of it takes minutes, not a committee. The list does not make decisions; it stops and revises them. The choosing, and the cost of the choosing, remain yours. A list that requires a ceremony has become one more ceremony.

### The matrix at work

Four decisions, stakes rising from a kitchen table to the survival of a people. In each, the matrix does the work; the drama is incidental. Where a package walks into the room, the package gets named. Where a decision is made, someone owns the maintenance of it.

**A kitchen budget.** The household income fell in the spring; by autumn the question is whether to keep the old table on credit or cut it. Filter one carries the load: the claim that the shortfall is temporary is testable against six months of payslips and bills, and the payslips answer no. Filter four names the honest tool: a cushion, savings drawn for two named months while a second income is found, a bridge with a far side. Restaurant meals on a card with no end date fail the same filter, a free-ride billed to the household's own future. Filter five settles the shame: the household eats before the neighbors' opinion of the table. The parents choose the cut, keep the dated cushion, and tell the children the true reason. The maintenance: the card statement, read aloud, every month, until the planks come up.

<!-- arc: G2 | point: school discipline: the matrix separates mercy from permissiveness; the head teacher owns the path back -->
**A school discipline case.** A boy hits classmates twice a week. The advocate for keeping him in the room says that a child marked early is a child lost, and she is right to fear the marking. Run the list anyway. Filter one: the claim that patience is working is testable, and the incident log and the reading scores of the other twenty-nine answer it. Filter five: the school owes the twenty-nine who came to learn before it owes the one who came to hit; the one is owed a path back, not the room. Filter six: the rule that violence removes the violent must hold for the mayor's nephew as for the stranger's son, or the school has no rule. Filter four names the package in the room: permissiveness, judgment skipped and the skip renamed compassion, with the harm billed to children who did not choose to pay. The head teacher suspends the boy with named conditions, a named teacher, and a named date for return. The maintenance: the conditions kept, the return made real, and the same rule applied to the next hard case without checking the surname first.

<!-- arc: G2 | point: border and membership: compassion is real but budgeted by tier; the assembly owns the count -->
**A border and a membership.** A polity votes on how many to admit. The honest advocate of wide admission stands at the gate and sees real misery outside and real surplus inside; her compassion is not counterfeit, and the matrix does not ask her to discard it. The matrix asks her to aim it. Filter one: the claim that the numbers absorb themselves is testable against housing lists, wage lines, and school places. Filter five: the polity owes its own households first; compassion beyond that is real, but spent from surplus, never from the children's keep. Filter four: refuge from a short shock is a cushion, time-bound, ending in return or in full membership; an open-ended claim on what the members built and maintain is the free-ride again, this time described as generosity. Filter three: the fence is form; the duties of membership are substance; argue about the fence only after the duties are settled. Filter six: one entry law, applied to the minister's cousin and the unknown man alike. Filter seven: what do the schools, the courts, and the common tongue look like if this rate holds for thirty years? The assembly sets a rate the platform can carry, names the duties of membership in plain law, and publishes the count every year. The maintenance: the count, the enforcement, and the revision, all three, or the decision was only a speech.

**A temporary coalition.** Now the stakes are survival. Your people face an enemy that means their end, and the only strong ally on offer holds a package whose premises deny your axioms: in that package your survival is negotiable, and truth is an instrument rather than a master. Say first what the ally's own side would say: they share your enemy, they need your front, and some among them mean the treaty honestly. Then run the list. Filter one: the claim that the ally will keep the treaty is testable against the ally's texts and the ally's practice; read both. Filter two: decide whether the ally's story of the world is incomplete, working inside a domain you can bound, or wrong, failing everywhere it is tried and asking to be treated as final. Filter five settles the choice: the survival of your own comes first, so a coalition may be signed. Filter seven shapes the signature: what happens when the shared enemy falls? This book has already walked through the alliance that sorted its partners by purge once the common target was gone, and that record is not a rumor. The council signs a bounded treaty for a bounded purpose, says the ally's package name aloud in its own minutes, keeps its own procedure and its own arms, and plans the exit before the victory. The maintenance: watch the ally's practice against the ally's word, month by month, and when the enemy falls, sort honestly. Temporary allies are not shared axioms.

### Begin with yourself

Society is individuals under procedures. That is the whole anatomy. A procedure is kept or dropped by persons, one decision at a time, and the person most available to you for this work is the one reading this page.

So the book ends with an order that sounds small and is not: before the next indictment of the world, create one thing and maintain it. Not a theory of the thing. The thing. A garden bed kept through a season. A sum saved and not raided. A class taught weekly to children not your own. A roof repaired so well it does not leak in the second winter. A trade learned well enough that a stranger pays for the work twice. The size is not the point; the creating and the maintaining are. The creating teaches you what reality costs, and the maintaining teaches you what procedures are for. A person who has kept one small thing alive under constraint reads the news differently.

Allow failure in training; do not court failure. The apprentice planes the board crooked, and the crooked board is the budgeted price of the skill. A household or a school that forbids that failure produces adults who can do nothing untested. Courting failure is a different act: betting the rent, wiring the house untrained, signing a child up for a debt the child did not choose. Filter five draws the line. You may spend your own splinters on your own learning. You may not spend your children's footing on it.

### The closing charge

Hold the spine. The world is consequential and has natural constraints. Wishes do not move the world; names do not move it; votes do not move it. Work moves it, inside limits, at a cost.

Keep your maps in correspondence with that world, and keep your procedure honest. A map stays honest when its claims are tested and the same tests judge success and failure. A procedure stays honest when the same rules run inside an honest scope, for your side and for the other side, on the good day and on the bad.

Keep life, continuing and continuing well, as the aim. Every test in this book bends back to that aim: does this help a person, a household, a people survive, and then do more than survive, in order, with skill, building and transmitting?

Refuse the counterfeits. Justice is procedural symmetry; when someone demands fairness, ask which of the three things called fair is being demanded, and refuse the two rivals: outcome symmetry, same end-states enforced regardless of conduct, and value symmetry, equal return in kind kept as a feud ledger. Compassion is real only after judgment has happened; refuse the skip that calls itself mercy and sends the bill to the next victim. Refuse the compassion that is aimed at you as a weapon by those who need not practice it.

Learn from the peoples who thrived under limits, and from the ruptures that emptied the stalls. A market stall is a chain kept daily: the miller's cart came through the rain, the baker woke in the dark, the road stayed safe, the price named yesterday is honored today. Break the procedure at any link and the stall can empty in a season, and no decree fills it. You have read the harbor state that treated the world as owing it nothing and built order under scarcity. You have read the empire that chose rupture over repair and graded out in famine and terror. Grade every proposal you meet by one question: which of those two does it walk toward?

Name packages; do not hunt blood. A package is a set of premises, texts, and institutions; a package can be named, tested, and refused. Persons are not packages. Judge the man in front of you by his commitments and his conduct, never by his surname. This discipline is not politeness. It is accuracy. Blood cannot hold a premise, and any blood can carry one.

Then do the work. Repair what is broken in your reach. Teach what you know to whoever will hold it. Raise the children in order and in skill. Defend your own, in the order the tiers set. Tell the truth, at the price truth costs. The world answers. Make your answer a maintained thing.
