# Foundations

## The five questions

Every society argues about policies while the deeper split sits underneath, in a small set of questions most people never say out loud. Two citizens can attend the same church, vote for the same party, and still give opposite answers about what truth is, what a human being is, and what justice requires. The shared label hides the split until a hard case (an inheritance, a hiring decision, a war) forces the answers into the open.

<!-- arc: Q6 | point: unspoken answers already govern conduct; actions reveal them -->
Whether or not a person states answers, that person's actions already contain them. A father who pays for his own daughter's schooling before funding any distant cause has answered the question of priority. A voter who reads unequal pay as automatic injustice has answered the question of justice. A colleague who shrugs "true for you" at a factual dispute has answered the question of truth. A couple that treats children as an optional luxury, and a people that treats raising children as its main work, have each answered the question of the good. The only choice on offer is between answers examined and answers absorbed without notice.

Here are the five questions, stated in full:

1. What is truth? Is reality real and independent of wish? Can we know it with usable confidence? Is knowledge mainly discovery that answers to the world, or only a product of power, will, or pure dialectic? What makes a claim true?

2. Are humans limited and self-interested by nature, a blank slate waiting to be written, or naturally good / perfectible / restorable by remaking the whole social (or sacral-political) order?

3. What is the standard of the good: what ought human life and society aim at?

4. What deserves priority when obligations conflict?

5. What does justice require: equal procedures within a scope, equal outcomes for everyone, or equal value returned in kind?

Read the five in order and a structure appears. The first two questions are descriptive: they ask what the world is and what the creature living in it is. The third question makes the turn from what is to what ought to be, and this book leaves it open at this point on purpose. Rival standards of the good exist, and loading one of them into the question would rig the asking before any reader could answer. The fourth and fifth questions ask how duty and justice should be ordered under whatever standard the third question selects, given what the first two said about the world and the creature.

<!-- arc: Q7 | point: answers to the five predict cooperation; labels do not -->
When a decision starts to cost something, watch who actually cooperates. The splits that matter run along answers to these five questions, not along the labels on the church door or the party card. A neighbor who shares your answers on truth, on human nature, on the good, on priority, and on justice can plan with you across a decade; a colleague who differs on all five cannot, whatever organizations you both belong to.

So name your answers. Ask these five questions of yourself, and prefer these questions to any label you or anyone else wears. State your answers plainly, or admit that you hold answers other than the ones this book is about to give. The next chapter states this book's five answers as axioms; the rest of the book is how to live under them.
