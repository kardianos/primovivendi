%+ Societies argue about policies; the deeper division sits beneath the arguments, in a small set of questions which few men ever pronounce aloud, and it is with these questions that this book begins. Two citizens may attend the same church and vote for the same party, and yet return opposite answers upon the nature of truth, the nature of man, and the demands of justice. The shared label conceals the division until some hard case (an inheritance, a hiring decision, a war) compels the answers into the open.

<!-- arc: Q6 | point: unspoken answers already govern conduct; actions reveal them -->
Whether or not a person states answers, that person's actions already contain them. A father who pays for his own daughter's schooling before funding any distant cause has answered the question of priority. A voter who reads unequal pay as automatic injustice has answered the question of justice. A colleague who shrugs "true for you" at a factual dispute has answered the question of truth. A couple that treats children as an optional luxury, and a people that treats raising children as its main work, have each answered the question of the good. The only choice on offer is between answers examined and answers absorbed without notice.
