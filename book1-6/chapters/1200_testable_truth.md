## Testable truth

Some claims can be tested in reality, and for those the rule is simple: if a claim can be tested in reality, test the claim in reality. A bridge design is tested by the load the bridge carries. A planting rule is tested by the harvest. Claims about birth rates, crime counts, and the strength of beams yield to counting and measuring, and the counts do not adjust themselves to our hopes.

For claims that cannot be tested in this reality, this book settles nothing. The ultimate will of the divine, and any final account of all things that leaves no empirical trail, cannot be confronted with observation or measurement. Let the divine speak there, for those who hold a divine, or leave the question open. This section offers nothing for or against the words of the divine as such: no catechism, and no anti-catechism.

People who share streets and shared work still need shared tests for shared empirical claims. A commitment that cannot be tested may guide the person who holds it; the commitment may not cancel a failed test of a testable claim. When the bridge falls, no conviction above measurement makes the fall a success.

This division of claims follows directly from the opening axioms, and this section states the resulting method once. Reality is real, and reality judges: the failed harvest and the broken bridge grade our claims without appeal. Truth is correspondence: a claim is true when the claim matches what is, so when model and world disagree, the world wins. And we are limited: finite time, finite knowledge, finite reach, so every account we hold is held by minds that can be wrong. From these three the method follows. Confront claims with what is, prefer better correspondence, and stay correctable. The rest of the book can then spend its pages on living rather than on re-arguing method.

A model that produces good numbers has passed one test and only one. Truth is correspondence, not usefulness, and a procedure can output right answers from a wrong or incomplete model. Useful prediction is therefore a clue and a filter: a clue about where to look, a filter that removes what fails outright. Useful prediction is not a certificate that the full account has been secured, and no prediction closes a favored model against further scrutiny, whatever political or sacred favor that model enjoys.

A practice can keep people alive while the story told about the practice is garbled, and the two must be graded separately. Consider a farmer who rotates his crops because the field spirits, he says, demand their seasons of rest. He moves the barley off the tired field and plants clover where the barley stood. The field lies a year under green cover, and the next planting of grain comes up heavy in the hand. The granary fills; the practice works. The story about spirits may still be wrong. Grade the practice by the harvest and the story by the evidence, and do not let a full granary certify the spirits.

The method rests on a distinction that must not be blurred: the difference between a wrong model and an incomplete one. A model is wrong when the model fails systematically under proper tests, across places and trials, and keeps no domain where the model earns its keep. The alchemist's recipes are the standing example: base metal into gold by the stated steps, and the steps fail every controlled trial. A model is incomplete when the model succeeds inside a domain of conditions (a range of speeds, sizes, accuracies, timescales) and fails or needs extension outside that domain. A successor then keeps the old successes as a limiting case and covers more. Incomplete is not an insult. Incomplete is the normal state of limited knowers.

Do not confuse the two, in either direction. The first error dresses a wrong model as incomplete: defenders redefine every failure as outside the domain and never state an independent rule for where the domain ends. The second error treats an incomplete model as wrong: people discard a tool that still builds sound bridges because a newer theory handles the extremes. The first error protects a story from the world. The second error discards earned correspondence.

Start with the older astronomical calculators, because their accuracy was earned and real. For calendars, for navigation, and for predicting where the planets would stand, the older schemes (Babylonian tables, then the Greek and later geometric methods of deferent, epicycle, and equant) delivered results accurate enough for the needs of their eras. The calculators were serious people doing exacting work, and their corrections accumulated because the corrections genuinely reduced the error an observer would see.

The accuracy did not make the story true. The dominant physical story placed Earth fixed at the center and treated the spheres and the epicycle machinery as the real arrangement of the heavens, while the calculators aimed their mathematics at matching what observers saw, whatever the machinery had to do. Kepler, working from Tycho Brahe's observations, stated laws that match what the bodies do with far fewer patches. Each planet moves in an ellipse with the sun at one focus. The line from sun to planet sweeps equal areas in equal times. The period of an orbit stands in a fixed relation to the orbit's size. The lesson is exact: accuracy of results is not truth of the full model. The step from the tables to Kepler deserves praise, not contempt; the virtue on display is correction under better evidence, and the ancestors were not fools.

The chain from Kepler to Newton to Einstein shows the second category: incomplete models that expand. Kepler's laws were empirical regularities, deep, but not yet a general account of all falling and orbiting bodies. Newton built on that base with his laws of motion and inverse-square gravitation, deriving the elliptical orbits as consequences; the same laws cover falling apples, the moon, the tides, and predictions the old tables never made. Then Einstein's relativity showed the limits of Newton's domain. At speeds near the speed of light, and wherever absolute time fails, Newton's model no longer matches, and full gravity needs more than Newton. None of this makes Newton an alchemist. Engineers put Newtonian models to work every day inside ordinary speeds and sizes, and relativity itself keeps Newton's successes as the limiting case where they still hold. Nor is relativity confined to laboratories: the satellite clocks behind everyday position finding need relativistic correction to keep their accuracy.

A chain of incomplete models is not the failure of truth-seeking; a chain of incomplete models is what truth-seeking looks like among limited knowers. Each step keeps the successes the earlier step earned, holds them as the special case where conditions are ordinary, and then covers ground the earlier step could not. Kepler is not discarded by Newton, and Newton is not discarded by Einstein. Each later account corresponds more widely and more precisely than the one before.

The method demands one more discipline, and the axioms already named the discipline: procedural symmetry in inquiry. The same critical standard applies to favored and disfavored models alike. Whatever test kills a disfavored model must also be run on the favored one, by the same people who love it. The tests run in both directions: a model that predicted something once is not exempt from later failure. When the failures of a favored model are excused while the failures of a rival are counted as fatal, the people running the tests have stopped doing inquiry and started protecting a story. Kepler's laws won on the data, not on court fashion. Newton's laws won on unified success, not on pedigree. Correspondence outranks prestige.

This standard permits humility without collapse. Every model we hold may be incomplete; that is the condition of limited minds. It does not follow that all models are equal. Wrong still fails, better correspondence still ranks higher, and tests that anyone may run do the ranking. The same honesty applies past the sciences, wherever a policy or a moral rule claims empirical effects (this law will reduce harm; that custom will help people continue and continue well). Short-run good numbers can hide a wrong story about people and costs postponed to others, exactly as the old tables hid a wrong story about the heavens. Nothing in this method picks out a party program. The claim is only that the same discipline of testing applies.

So the charge of this section is short. Section off the divine and the non-testable, and offer nothing for or against the words of the divine as such. Test what can be tested, in reality, and prefer the world to the preferred story. Do not mistake useful output for a true full account. Keep the tools that still earn their keep inside their domains, and discard what reality refuses. Run on your own models every test you run on the models of your opponents. Then live. Survival and thriving need claims that correspond well enough to build on, and people honest enough to correct those claims when the world corrects them. The next sections take up the living.
