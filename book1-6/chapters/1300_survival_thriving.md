## Survival and thriving

Survival is continuing to exist: as a person, as a household, as a people. The contents are plain: food on the table, safety from force and from fraud, health enough to act, and the means to defend all three. Dead people do not thrive. Dead peoples do not keep justice, skill, or art. Survival is the floor that everything else in this book stands on. A floor does not argue for itself; a floor holds, and everything above the floor (the meals, the books, the children at the table) exists only while the floor holds.

Survival is not a guarantee, and survival is not comfort. No tribunal grants a right to exist that holds without work and defense. A declaration can name a claim; only growing, building, and guarding keep the claimant alive. Each person and each people earns the next year, and the earning is never finished.

Thriving is the direction past bare continuation: order where there was chaos, skill where there was fumbling, health, prosperity enough to act freely, beauty in what is made, the formation of the next generation, and the capacity to repair and enlarge what was inherited. The measure is a slope between two times, never an arrival: a dirtier floor made cleaner, a weaker skill made stronger, a chaos brought into usable order. Whoever can point at that slope between two honest times is thriving.

Thriving is not a finished paradise at the top of the slope; the slope has no top. Thriving is not wealth as the whole good. Thriving is not feeling good as the test: a household can feel fine while the roof rots and the skills die untaught, and the good feeling measures nothing.

A household can live for years on what its members did not build. The grandfather built the house and filled the storehouse. The father kept the accounts and repaired what the weather broke. The son eats the grain, sells the tools he never learned to use, and teaches his one daughter nothing of planting, accounts, or roofs. Every year the family is still alive. Every year there is less grain, less roof, less skill, and fewer hands to carry the name. Survival without thriving is this: a person, a household, or a people consuming a platform that earlier people built, living in ruins no one can repair, letting skills die untaught, ending the line that carried the name. Alive this year; failing across decades.

The second failure is thriving talk without survival: the vocabulary of the good life spoken over a floor that nobody maintains. The talk itself is not the error. Justice, art, and compassion are real goods, and the people who speak for them often mean what they say. The error is the neglect underneath: the speakers leave defense to unnamed others, assume food arrives, do not have or raise children, and treat honest work as beneath them. A choir can sing well about the beauty of the hall while the floor gives way beneath the singers. The song is real, the voices are trained, and the crack in the boards does not care. When defense, food, and children go, the talk goes too; what remains is performance on a collapsing floor.

Between the two failures stands the positive task: build and keep the social forms that serve survival and thriving, and grade those forms only by that service. The list is short and old: a family that raises competent adults; property that ties care to consequence, so the owner who neglects the roof is the one who gets wet; honest trade under scarcity; the rule of law as the same process for everyone inside the polity; schools that transmit skill and judgment, since a school forms character whether or not the school admits to forming; borders that keep the set of payers and the set of protected roughly the same set; speech free enough that error can be found.

The order of that list follows the tiered priority stated among the axioms: duties strongest near self and household, outer claims real but weaker.

Grade each form by results for life under the axioms, never by the sacredness of a name. The question is whether the people under a form continue and continue well, and the question has no favorite regime. Democracy, republic, monarchy, premier, party: each is a tool, kept while the tool serves life, repaired or replaced when the tool stops serving. The form is never the sacred object; the life of the people the form serves is.

This standard is the load-bearing middle of the book, and the virtues hang on it. Survival and thriving do not happen by accident for creatures like us: limited in time, knowledge, and reach, self-interested, not naturally good. The virtues are how such people meet the standard on purpose. The next part takes the virtues of the person: the trained habits by which one limited man or woman earns the next year and bends the slope upward. The part after takes the virtues of the society: the kept forms by which households and polities let those habits scale.
