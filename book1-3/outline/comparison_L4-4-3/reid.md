# Provenance regarding Thomas Reid
Primary sources include *An Inquiry into the Human Mind on the Principles of Common Sense* (1764) and *Essays on the Active Powers of Man* (1788). Reid, the founder of the Scottish School of Common Sense, argues for the reliability of the senses and the inherent agency of the human will against Humean skepticism.

## Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential
"If there are certain principles, as I think there are, which the constitution of our nature leads us to believe, and which we are under a necessity to take for granted in the common concerns of life, without being able to give a reason for them—these are what we call the principles of common sense; and what is manifestly contrary to them, is what we call absurd." (*Inquiry*, Ch 2, §6).
"I resolve not to believe my senses. I break my nose against a post that comes in my way; I step into a dirty kennel; and, after twenty such wise and rational actions, I am taken up and clapped into a madhouse." (*Inquiry*, Ch 6, §20).
"The evidence of sense, the evidence of memory, and the evidence of the necessary relations of things, are all distinct and original kinds of evidence, equally grounded on our constitution." (*Intellectual Powers*, Essay 2, Ch 20).
"To reason against any of these kinds of evidence is absurd; nay, to reason for them is absurd. They are first principles; and such fall not within the province of reasoning, but of common sense." (*Intellectual Powers*, Essay 2, Ch 20).
"We find it impossible to doubt of things which we clearly perceive." (*Intellectual Powers*, Essay 6, Ch 5).

## Humans are Limited and Selfish
"It is the want of strength and resolution that makes us yield to passion." (*Active Powers*, Essay 3, Ch 8).
"A man may be led by passion or by interest to do what is wrong; but it is impossible he should think it right, unless his judgment be perverted." (*Active Powers*, Essay 5, Ch 7).
"[Power] is a trust, and we are accountable for the use of it." (*Active Powers*, Essay 1, Ch 7).
"Men are not so much disposed to seek the truth as to support their own opinions." (*Active Powers*, Essay 3, Ch 6).
"In all ages, men have been prone to imagine that they see what they wish to see." (*Inquiry*, Ch 1, §1).

## Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility
"The power to produce any effect, implies power not to produce it, and high probabilities are not certainties... moral responsibility implies, in its very nature, this power." (*Active Powers*, Essay 1, Ch 5).
"Every man is conscious of a power to determine, in things which he conceives to depend upon his determination." (*Active Powers*, Essay 4, Ch 1).
"To call a man to account for his conduct, who has no power to regulate it, is a solecism in government." (*Active Powers*, Essay 4, Ch 1).
"If a man receives a trust, he is accountable for the discharge of it." (*Active Powers*, Essay 3, Ch 8).
"Our power is the measure of our duty." (*Active Powers*, Essay 3, Ch 8).

## Goodness is to Live and Live Well
"I thank the Author of my being... and now I yield to the direction of my senses, not from instinct only, but from confidence and trust in a faithful and beneficent Monitor, grounded upon the experience of his paternal care and goodness." (*Inquiry*, Ch 6, §20).
"The benevolent affections are naturally agreeable... they diffuse a joy and serenity through the mind." (*Active Powers*, Essay 3, Ch 4).
"Self-love... dictates to us to avoid pain and misery, and to pursue happiness and enjoyment." (*Active Powers*, Essay 3, Ch 2).
"Nature has given us a desire of society... and the happiness of the individual is inseparable from the public good." (*Active Powers*, Essay 5, Ch 5).

## Procedural Symmetry
"In every case, we ought to act that part toward another, which we would judge to be right in him to act toward us, if we were in his circumstances and he in ours." (*Active Powers*, Essay 5, Ch 1).
"This is the law of the prophets, and it comprises every rule of justice." (*Active Powers*, Essay 5, Ch 1).
"Justice is infinite... it is the same to all men, and in all cases." (*Active Powers*, Essay 5, Ch 3).
"To define justice... it is a fixed and constant purpose to give to every man his due." (*Active Powers*, Essay 5, Ch 2).

## Humility (The Criticism Test)
"Philosophy... has no other root but the principles of Common Sense; it grows out of them, and draws its nourishment from them." (*Inquiry*, Ch 1, §1).
"If [philosophy] attempts to act independently, and to set up for herself, she produces only monsters and chimeras." (*Inquiry*, Ch 1, §1).
"Reason must be subject to the dictates of common sense." (*Intellectual Powers*, Essay 6, Ch 2).
"We ought to be always ready to give a reason for our faith." (*Intellectual Powers*, Essay 6, Ch 4).
"Error is the common lot of humanity." (*Intellectual Powers*, Essay 6, Ch 8).
