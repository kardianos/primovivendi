# Scratchpad for Worldview Audits Drafts (Expanded)

## 1. John Locke (The Architect of Liberalism)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Compatible
Locke builds his entire political philosophy on the foundation of Natural Law, which he argues is discovered by reason, not invented by will. In the *Second Treatise*, he describes the State of Nature as having a "law of nature to govern it," which is binding on everyone. This law is not a social construct or a decree of a monarch. It is a fixed moral reality inherent in the fabric of the world and human existence. For Locke, reason is the "candle of the Lord," which is a tool given to men to discern this pre-existing order. This aligns perfectly with our first principle: Reality is Objective and Knowable. A society cannot be free if truth is arbitrary; Locke ensures freedom by anchoring it in the unchangeable reality of Natural Rights. Without this objective grounding, rights would be mere permissions granted by the state and revocable at whim.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Compatible
Locke strikes a delicate balance that perfectly matches the "Constrained Vision." He asserts that men are "by nature, all free, equal, and independent," acknowledging our agency and dignity. However, he explicitly rejects the utopian idea that men are angels who can live without governance. He argues that in the State of Nature, the enjoyment of rights is "very unsafe, very unsecure" because men are biased in their own cases and driven by passion to over-punish others. We are not naturally depraved monsters, but we are flawed enough that we cannot be trusted to be judges in our own cause. This recognition of inherent fallibility is the very reason he proposes limited government: to act as a neutral arbiter. This aligns with our second principle: Human Nature is Flawed.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Compatible
Locke defines the individual as the ultimate unit of moral and political account. He famously argues, "Every man has a property in his own person: this no body has any right to but himself." This self-ownership implies that we are responsible for our own actions, labor, and survival. He does not place the burden of the individual's existence on the tribe or the state. Rather, "the labour of his body, and the work of his hands" are the means by which he sustains himself. By linking rights to responsibilities, specifically the responsibility to respect the rights of others, Locke creates a system of Tiered Responsibility where the individual is primary. The state exists only to protect this sphere of responsibility, not to usurp it.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Compatible
The entire purpose of Locke's social contract is the "preservation of their property," which he defines broadly as "Life, Liberty, and Estate." This is a direct political expression of the Axiom of Goodness: "Life should continue and continue well." Locke does not see the goal of society as glory, conquest, or spiritual purity, but as the practical flourishing of the individual. He establishes that the government has no other end but the peace, safety, and public good of the people. This focus on earthly preservation and the security to build a life aligns perfectly with our definition of Goodness. It rejects the death cults and nihilistic philosophies that view life as a burden or a resource to be spent by the state.

### Procedural Symmetry: Compatible
Locke is the great champion of Procedural Symmetry in governance. He insists that freedom is not autonomy from all law, but "to have a standing rule to live by, common to every one of that society." This rule must apply equally to the peasant and the magistrate. He explicitly argues against arbitrary power, stating that the law must not vary in particular cases but must remain the "same rule" for the favorite at court and the countryman at the plough. This is the definition of the Rule of Law. It is the symmetrical application of procedure regardless of status. By stripping the monarch of divine right and subjecting him to the same law as the citizen, Locke enshrined the principle of Symmetry at the heart of the West.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Allowed
Locke's *Letter Concerning Toleration* is the foundational text for the Humility Test. He argues that "Ill will... ought not to be found in the debates of men who have truth for their aim." He recognized that because human knowledge is limited (fallible), no magistrate has the authority to enforce a monopoly on truth. In the Hegemony of Liberalism (Western Democracies), this principle holds: one can publish books viciously attacking Locke, Liberalism, or the Prime Minister without fear of the dungeon. The system possesses the structural humility to allow for its own critique and correction. It does not demand worship, only adherence to the civil law.

### Summary
John Locke provides the most direct architectural blueprint for a society based on these five principles. His system is not merely compatible. It is the native operating system of a free society. By grounding rights in Objective Reality (Natural Law), acknowledging the Flaws of Human Nature (Bias), centering Responsibility on the individual (Self-ownership), aiming for Goodness (Preservation), and insisting on Symmetry (Rule of Law), he satisfies every condition of the audit. While modern iterations may drift, the core Lockean framework is the primary example for how these 5 principles translate into political machinery.

## 2. Christianity (The Separation of Spheres)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Compatible
Christianity posits a Logos-centered universe: "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" (Genesis 1:1). This means that reality is intelligible, ordered, and objectively true. Paul confirms that knowledge of reality is accessible to all: "For since the creation of the world His invisible attributes, that is, His eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly perceived, being understood by what has been made, so that they are without excuse" (Romans 1:20). This aligns with "Reality is Knowable." Furthermore, because God is Truth, reality is not malleable by human will; it is a fixed territory that we must adapt to.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Compatible
The doctrine of Original Sin is perhaps the most empirically verifiable assertion of Christian theology. It states that "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" (Romans 3:23), meaning no human is perfectible or naturally good. Jesus Himself held this constrained view: "But Jesus, on His part, was not entrusting Himself to them, because He knew all people, and because He did not need anyone to testify about mankind, for He Himself knew what was in mankind" (John 2:24-25). This aligns perfectly with the "Flawed" or "Constrained Vision." Because man is fallen, he cannot be trusted with absolute power. This humility regarding human nature is the essential protection against totalitarianism.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Compatible
Christianity emphasizes Tiered Responsibility through the lens of stewardship. Paul is explicit about the priority of the family: "But if anyone does not provide for his own, and especially for those of his household, he has denied the faith and is worse than an unbeliever" (1 Timothy 5:8). One cannot bypass the duty to family to save the world; the circles of concern start at home. Yet, it does not stop there; the call to "love your neighbor" extends this responsibility outward in concentric rings.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Compatible
Adam was given the command: "Be fruitful and multiply, and fill the earth" (Genesis 1:28). To Noah, it was repeated: "Be fruitful and multiply, and fill the earth" (Genesis 9:1). Jesus framed his mission around flourishing: "I came so that they would have life, and have it abundantly" (John 10:10). Paul instructs the rich to fix their hope "on God, who richly supplies us with all things to enjoy" (1 Timothy 6:17). This aligns with the simple axiom that life should continue well.

### Procedural Symmetry: Compatible
The Old Testament Law demanded equality for the stranger: "As for the assembly, there shall be one statute for you and for the stranger who resides among you... as you are, so shall the stranger be before the Lord" (Numbers 15:15). Leviticus commands: "You shall not take vengeance... but you shall love your neighbor as yourself" (Leviticus 19:18). Jesus amplified this: "Treat people the same way you want them to treat you" (Matthew 7:12). This establishes a radical symmetry where every individual is equal before the law and God.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Allowed
The defining feature of Western Christian Hegemony is the separation of Church and State, grounded in the words of Jesus: "Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar’s; and to God the things that are God’s." This creates a structural "secular" space where the sword of the state cannot enforce the dogmas of the church. While historical aberrations occurred (Inquisitions), the developed Christian West allows for the fiercest critique of its own foundations. One can publish "The God Delusion" in a Christian nation without legal repercussion. The distinction between "Sin" and "Crime" allows specific theological claims to be debated without the force of law, passing the Humility Test.

### Summary
Christianity offers a robust metaphysical grounding that reinforces every aspect of these principles. Its insistence on an ordered Reality, a flawed Human Nature, and the infinite value of the Individual provides the "Why" behind the "What" of liberal governance. Critically, its doctrinal separation of the spiritual and political spheres allows it to be a safe dominant partner. It provides the moral capital necessary for a free society, specifically self-restraint, charity, and duty, without demanding the theocratic subjugation of those outside the faith. It is the foundational support of this framework.

## 3. Thomas Reid (Common Sense Realism)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Compatible
Reid anchors his philosophy in the direct reliability of perception, countering the skepticism of his day. 
> "Every man feels that perception gives him an invincible belief of the existence of that which he perceives; and that this belief is not the effect of reasoning, but the immediate consequence of perception." (*Inquiry into the Human Mind*, Chapter 5, Section 2)
 
This assertion of an "invincible belief" in the external world provides the necessary "Shared Reality" for a functional society. It rejects the solipsism that claims we only know our own minds, establishing that the table, the crime scene, and the contract are real objects known directly by all.
 
### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Compatible
Reid defines human nature by "Active Power"—the capacity to choose.
> "The power to produce any effect, implies power not to produce it, and high probabilities are not certainties... moral responsibility implies, in its very nature, this power." (*Essays on the Active Powers*, Essay 1, Chapter 5)
 
This acknowledges the "Constrained Vision" by rooting agency in the individual's will rather than environmental determinism. Because we have power, we are liable to error, but we are also capable of self-governance.
 
### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Compatible
For Reid, responsibility is the natural corollary of Active Power.
> "Moral laws are the laws which as the supreme Lawgiver he prescribes to his reasonable creatures for their conduct, which, indeed ought always to be obeyed, but in fact are often transgressed." (*Essays on the Active Powers*, Essay 3, Chapter 8)
 
By distinguishing moral laws (which can be broken) from physical laws (which cannot), Reid places the burden of action squarely on the individual. This is "Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility": we are "reasonable creatures" who must own our transgressions.
 
### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Compatible
Reid sees the design of the universe as fundamentally benevolent, meant for our preservation.
> "I thank the Author of my being... and now I yield to the direction of my senses, not from instinct only, but from confidence and trust in a faithful and beneficent Monitor, grounded upon the experience of his paternal care and goodness." (*Inquiry into the Human Mind*, Chapter 6, Section 20)
 
This gratitude for "paternal care" aligns with the axiom that life is good and should continue. It is not an indifferent universe, but one structured for human flourishing.
 
### Procedural Symmetry: Compatible
Reid articulates the principle of Procedural Symmetry through his definition of Justice and the Golden Rule.
> "In every case, we ought to act that part toward another, which we would judge to be right in him to act toward us, if we were in his circumstances and he in ours..." (*Essays on the Active Powers*, Essay 5, Chapter 1)
 
He calls this "the most comprehensive" rule of justice. It demands that the specific identity of the actor (me vs. him) be irrelevant to the moral judgment of the action, which is the definition of Procedural Symmetry.
 
### Humility (The Criticism Test): Allowed
Reid's "Common Sense" is inherently democratic and anti-elitist. It subjects every philosophical theory to the judgment of the ordinary faculties of man.
> "Philosophy... has no other root but the principles of Common Sense; it grows out of them, and draws its nourishment from them." (*Inquiry into the Human Mind*, Chapter 1, Section 1)
 
By placing the philosopher's theories below the "authority of these principles," Reid ensures a society where even the highest intellectual systems can be critiqued by the plain reality experienced by the common citizen.
 
### Summary
Thomas Reid provides the epistemological bedrock for Real Reality. His "Common Sense Realism" ensures that the courtroom and the laboratory operate in the same world, rejecting the skepticism that dissolves facts into mere opinions. By anchoring Justice in the Golden Rule and Agency in Active Power, he is a fully compatible partner who provides the "sanity check" against high-theory utopianism.

## 4. Stoicism

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Compatible
Stoicism is grounded in the belief that the universe is governed by *Logos*, which is rational, divine order. It is the opposite of chaos. "If you are going to be a philosopher, you must adjust your will to nature." This alignment with Natural Law puts Stoicism squarely in the Compatible camp for Reality. They believe that facts are immovable and that wisdom consists of aligning one's internal map with the external territory. A Stoic citizen does not try to wish away gravity or economic scarcity. They accept reality as it is and work within it.
 
### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Compatible
Stoicism teaches that human beings are capable of reason but are constantly besieged by false judgments and unruly passions. We are not born perfect; we must train ourselves (askesis) to become virtuous. This acknowledges the "Flawed" aspect of human nature, as our default state is often anxiety and error. However, it upholds human dignity by asserting that we have the capacity to choose our response. This "Constrained Vision" holds that we cannot control the world (the outcome), but we can control our will. This fits the need for citizens who are self-governing and resilient, rather than fragile and demanding of total state protection.
 
### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Compatible
Hierocles, a Stoic philosopher, described the "Circles of Concern," which perfectly maps to "Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility." The self is the center, then the immediate family, then extended family, then neighbors, then citizens, then humanity. The Stoic practice is to "draw the circles inward," treating the distant with more care, but never denying the structure itself. You cannot skip the inner rings to serve the outer ones. This structural alignment ensures that a Stoic society would prioritize local duties, such as feeding one's own children, before attempting to save the world, preventing the dissipation of responsibility.
 
### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Compatible
Here there is a slight nuance. For a strict Stoic, "Virtue is the only Good." Health, wealth, and long life are "preferred indifferents," nice to have, but not essential for happiness. However, in practice, Stoics like Marcus Aurelius worked tirelessly for the stability and flourishing of the Empire. They recognize that while the *sage* might be happy on the rack, the *citizen* needs security and bread. Therefore, Stoicism supports the Axiom of Goodness politically (creating a functional state) even if strictly philosophically it sets a higher, internal standard for "Good." It is compatible because it promotes the conditions for flourishing.
 
### Procedural Symmetry: Compatible
Stoicism is the origin of "Cosmopolitanism," the idea that we are citizens of the world. "There is but one law for all, namely that common reason which affirms what should be done," wrote Cicero (heavily influenced by Stoicism). This concept of Natural Law applies to the Emperor and the slave alike. It is the ultimate Symmetrical standard. In a time of strict tribalism, Stoicism declared that all men share in the Divine Reason, laying the groundwork for universal human rights. A Stoic judge would apply the law based on reason, not status, fulfilling the requirement of Procedural Symmetry.
 
### Humility (The Criticism Test): Allowed
The Roman Empire, under Stoic influence (like Marcus Aurelius), was remarkably tolerant of diverse cults and philosophies. One could stand in the Forum and argue against Stoicism without being arrested, provided one did not commit treason against the state. Stoicism itself encourages self-criticism; the "Meditations" of Marcus Aurelius are essentially a private audit of his own failings. A worldview that demands constant self-correction and indifference to the opinion of the mob is naturally resilient to critique. It does not need to silence dissenters to feel secure.
 
### Summary
Stoicism serves as a foundational code for the guardians of these principles. While it may be too austere for the masses, its emphasis on duty, resilience, and Natural Law makes it an ideal personal philosophy for leaders and soldiers. It aligns with every one of these principles, reinforcing the shared reality, the need for personal responsibility, and the universal application of law. It is a philosophy that strengthens the resolve of a free society against decadence and entitlement.

## 5. Scripturalism and the Denial of the Real

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Incompatible
Gordon Clark's Scripturalism explicitly rejects the "Correspondence Theory of Truth" (that truth is the agreement of the mind with reality) and replaces it with the "Coherence Theory" (that truth is the internal consistency of propositions revealed by God). He claims, "Scripturalism denies the correspondence theory of truth... a proposition is true because God thinks it to be true." This is "Divine Constructivism." It posits that reality itself is malleable and dependent on the momentary will of the Deity, rather than being a fixed "nature" we can study independently of Revelation. This shatters the "Shared Reality." If the Bible is the *only* source of knowledge, then there is no common ground of "General Revelation" where the Christian and the Atheist can agree on the laws of physics or evidence in a courtroom.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Compatible
Scripturalism holds a high view of Total Depravity, aligning with the "Flawed" axiom. It asserts that sin has corrupted not just the will but the intellect itself (the Noetic effects of sin). This prevents the utopianism of human perfectibility. However, it tends towards "Intellectualism" such as defining sin primarily as logical error or ignorance of Scripture. While it respects the constraints of human nature, its solution (deriving correct propositions from the Bible) can lead to a rigid rationalism that undervalues the non-propositional aspects of human existence, like habit, affection, and tacit knowledge.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Compatible
As a branch of Reformed Theology, Scripturalism upholds a strong Covenantal model of responsibility. It emphasizes the duties of the individual, the family, and the church as distinct spheres. It rejects the socialist dilution of responsibility, insisting that a man is accountable to God for his specific station. The "Tiered Responsibility" model fits well here, as the duties are clearly defined by Scripture (e.g., parents must train children, children must honor parents) rather than vague appeals to "social justice."

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Mixed
The Scripturalist definition of Goodness is "The Glory of God." While this is the highest theological end, in political practice, it can create friction with "Survival and Flourishing." If a specific interpretation of Scripture demands policies that lead to famine or war (hypothetically), the Scripturalist must choose the proposition over the outcome. There is no "Natural Law" feedback loop where bad outcomes (starvation) prove bad policy; only Scripture judges policy. This lack of an empirical check can lead to a form of Theonomy that ignores the practical "Axiom of Goodness" required for a pluralistic society to flourish.

### Procedural Symmetry: Incompatible
Imposing the Bible as the *axiom* of the state violates Epistemic Symmetry. It creates two classes of citizens: those who have access to Truth (Believers) and those who have only "Opinion" (Unbelievers). Procedural Symmetry relies on the idea that the rules of evidence are accessible to all rational creatures (Common Sense). Scripturalism denies this, arguing that without the Bible, one cannot truly "know" anything. A court run on these principles could potentially throw out forensic evidence if it conflicted with a deduction from Scripture, destroying the neutral ground required for the Rule of Law.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Inconclusive
Scripturalism is an "Axiomatic System." Clark argues that you cannot prove the Bible; you must presuppose it as the detached axiom of all thought. To critique the Axiom is to step outside the system into irrationality. In a theoretical Scripturalist Hegemony, denying the Bible would be akin to denying the laws of logic. Criticism of the foundational text would likely be treated not just as error, but as insanity or heresy (treason against the truth). There is no structural humility because there is no authority outside the text (like Reality or Nature) to correct the text's interpreters.

### Summary
Scripturalism presents significant friction. While ethically conservative and responsible, its rejection of independent natural reality makes it deeply problematic for a society based on Real Reality. By denying the "Correspondence Theory of Truth," it destroys the foundation of "Shared Reality" upon which a free, pluralistic society sits. It retreats into a "Closed System of Propositions" that is internally consistent but externally unassailable and uncheckable. It fails the Reality and Humility tests, making it a worldview that can exist *within* this framework but cannot *ground* it.

## 6. The Jesuit Order (Modern: Certeau & Tilliette)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Incompatible
The Modern Jesuit approach is a syncretism of Catholic Dogma and Continental Critical Theory. Xavier Tilliette represents the "Orthodox" anchor, arguing that the "Fact of Christ" is the ultimate reality. However, Michel de Certeau adopts the language of Postmodernism/Marxism, viewing social reality as a "Simulacrum" constructed by power. This creates a dangerous friction: while they believe in a *Divine* Reality, their analysis of *Social* Reality often mirrors the "Deconstructionist" view that truth is just a mask for power ("The Scriptural Economy"). This makes them unreliable allies in defending the "Shared Reality" of the West.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Incompatible
Implicitly, they agree that humans are "weak" and "constrained." Certeau's "Tactician" is a weak operator trying to survive in a massive, alien system. This rejects the "Utopian" Marxist view that the Proletariat will become Gods/Rulers. However, it retains the "Oppressor/Oppressed" binary of Marxist thought, framing human relations primarily as a power struggle between the "Strategy" of the Strong and the "Tactics" of the Weak, rather than a cooperative high trust society.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Incompatible
While focusing on the "Agency" of the common man, the Modern Jesuit tendency (Liberation Theology influence) is to locate "Sin" in "Structures" (Systemic Injustice) rather than the Individual. By focusing on "Strategies of Institutions" as the source of evil, they risk eroding "Self-Anchored Responsibility." If the system is rigged (a Simulacrum), the individual is a victim, not a responsible moral agent. This shifts them away from the Lockean/Christian focus on individual duty toward a Collectivist critique.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Incompatible
Tilliette's "Incarnational" view is life-affirming. But the critical/political wing often drifts into "Revolutionary" goodness—the idea that "Good" is found in resisting/overturning the current Hegemony. This can lead to a "Burn it Down" mentality (Utopianism) that conflicts with the "Preservation" axiom ("Continue and Continue Well").

### Procedural Symmetry: Incompatible
This is the widest gap. By adopting the "Critical Vanguard" role, Modern Jesuits often adopt the "Repressive Tolerance" of the New Left. They critique the "Center" (Western/Capitalist/Hegemonic) norms relentlessly while advocating for the "Margins." While this mimics "Justice," in practice it violates Procedural Symmetry by creating a "Preferential Option" that is not just charitable, but *structural*. They judge the "Strong" and the "Weak" by different standards, justifying "Tactical" dishonesty or resistance from the weak that they would condemn in the strong.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Incompatible
Intellectually, Tilliette is humble before Revelation. Politically, the "Critical Theorist" persona is often devoid of humility. It assumes a "Gnostic" insight—that the Jesuit knows the "Hidden Structures" of oppression that the common man is too blinded to see. This "Vanguard" mentality is inherently elitist and resistant to critique from the "False Consciousness" of the masses.

### Summary
The Modern Jesuits (1920s-1980s) represent a "Syncretic" worldview: Christian Metaphysics overlaid with Marxist/Critical Analysis. Their **Methods and Language** (Deconstruction, Power Analysis, Vanguardism) are heavily borrowed from marxism. This makes them a "Trojan Horse" in a Liberty-based framework: they dismantle the Cultural/Institutional defenses of the West using Critical Theory, even if their ultimate goal (The Kingdom of God) differs from the Marxist goal (The Communist State). They are a source of significant **Civilizational Mixed**.

## 7. Immanuel Kant (Ethical Match, Metaphysical Mixed)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Incompatible
Kant introduces a split in reality with his distinction between the *Noumenal* (the thing-in-itself, which is unknowable) and the *Phenomenal* (the world as it appears to our senses). By arguing that "we can know nothing of these [noumena] as they are in themselves" (*Critique of Pure Reason*), he philosophically undermines the claim that "Reality is Knowable." However, for all civilizational purposes, Kant treats the *Phenomenal* world (the world of science and law) as governed by objective, universal laws. He is a "Transcendental Idealist" but an "Empirical Realist." This creates a friction: his metaphysics doubts the bedrock, but his ethics act as if the bedrock is solid.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Compatible
Kant is the source of the ultimate "Constrained Vision" quote: "Out of the crooked timber of humanity, no straight thing was ever made" (*Idea for a Universal History*, Proposition 6). He rejects the idea that humans are naturally good. In *Religion within the Bounds of Bare Reason*, he argues for "Radical Evil"—a natural propensity to subordinate the moral law to self-love. He constructs his political philosophy not on the hope of angelic behavior, but on the necessity of laws for a race of "rational devils." This aligns perfectly with the acknowledgement of human flaw.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Compatible (with nuance)
Kant distinguishes between "Perfect Duties" (universal rules of respect, e.g., do not lie, do not steal) and "Imperfect Duties" (duties of love, e.g., be benevolent). While Perfect Duties are symmetrical and universal, Kant acknowledges latitude in Imperfect Duties, noting one can "vary the degree greatly in accordance with the different objects of my love" (*Metaphysics of Morals*). This allows for Tiered Responsibility: we owe *Respect* (Rights) to everyone equally, but we owe *Care* (Resources) to our own sphere first. The anchor is the autonomous Self, responsible to the Moral Law within, not to an external despot.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Incompatible
This is the area of greatest conflict. Kant explicitly argues that the highest good is not *Happiness* (Flourishing) but *Moral Worth* (Duty). He rejects Utilitarianism (doing what ensures survival/happiness) in favor of strict Deontology. He writes, "Establishment of the moral law... differs largely from the principle of happiness" (*Critique of Practical Reason*). If the "Axiom of Goodness" is purely about biological and material flourishing ("Life should continue well"), Kant considers this secondary to the purity of the will. He would let the world perish rather than commit one injustice (*Fiat justitia, pereat mundus*).

### Procedural Symmetry: Strongest Match
While the *Categorical Imperative* is a moral test, Kant's political application in *The Metaphysics of Morals* (The Doctrine of Right) is the definition of Procedural Symmetry. He defines the "Civil State" by three principles, the second being "The equality of each with every other as a subject" (Ak 8:290). He insists that "no member of the commonwealth can have a hereditary privilege against his fellow subjects." The law must be "blind" to status. Thus, the *moral* requirement to universalize one's maxims becomes the *political* requirement for a Symmetry of Law. He provides the secular, logical steelframe for this principle.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Allowed
Kant's motto for the Enlightenment was *Sapere Aude*—"Dare to know!" He argued passionately for the "public use of one's reason" in *What is Enlightenment?*. For Kant, the ability to critique authorities, dogmas, and the state itself was the very engine of social maturity. He envisioned a "Kingdom of Ends" where every rational being is a legislator. Consequently, a Kantian society is inherently open to debate. To silence critique would be to treat humans as "immature," violating their dignity.

### Summary
Immanuel Kant provides the "Ethical Engine" for Procedural Symmetry. Despite his metaphysical over-complications regarding Reality, his political philosophy allows strict Equality before the Law ("No Hereditary Privilege") and a robust defense of Free Speech ("Public Use of Reason"). His "Crooked Timber" view of human nature aligns with the specific humility required by this framework. While his rigid prioritization of Duty over Happiness creates proper friction with the "Goodness" axiom, he remains an essential scaffold for a secular, rights-based republic.

## 8. David Hume (Political Match, Epistemological Incompatibility)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Incompatible
David Hume is the radical skeptic of the Enlightenment. His skepticism attacks the very foundations of knowledge. He argues that we cannot know "Cause and Effect" (we only see reliance, not necessary connection) and that the "Self" is just a bundle of perceptions. This is a direct assault on "Reality is Objective and Knowable." If we cannot be certain that the sun will rise tomorrow, or that we even exist as continuous selves, the foundation for a "Shared Reality" dissolves into mere habit and custom. A society built strictly on Humean epistemology would have no defense against a strongman who claimed to create his own reality, as Hume admits reason is helpless to refute him.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Compatible
Hume famously declared, "Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions." He rejects the rationalist idea that humans are ruled by logic. He sees us as emotional creatures driven by desire, pride, and self-interest. This is a robust "Constrained Vision" approach. He advises building political structures that channel these passions rather than trying to eradicate them. He warns against "Enthusiasm" (ideological or religious zealotry) which ignores human limitations. His conservatism is born of a low estimate of human rationality, making him a safe, cautious political thinker.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Compatible
Despite his skepticism, Hume defends "Artificial Virtues," rules like Justice and Property that we invent because they work. He argues that while we may not have a "natural" instinct for justice toward strangers, we develop the convention of Promise-Keeping and Property rights because we learn that society collapses without them. This leads to a functional responsibility. We keep our word not because of a cosmic categorical imperative, but because we want to be trusted in the future. It is a responsibility based on enlightened self-interest and social utility, which supports the Compact's tiered structure effectively.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Compatible
Hume is a proto-Utilitarian. He argues that "Public Utility" is the sole origin of justice. This aligns perfectly with the "Axiom of Goodness." We have laws not because they are written in the stars, but because they enable life to "continue and continue well." If a law leads to misery, Hume would argue it should be changed. His focus is entirely on the empirical well-being of society. He advocates for commerce, refinement, and the "peaceful enjoyment" of life, making him a strong ally of human flourishing against ascetic or martial ideologies.

### Procedural Symmetry: Compatible
Hume was a historian as well as a philosopher, and he championed the "Rule of Law" as the great achievement of civilized liberty. "The law always limits every power it gives." He argued that general, inflexible rules are necessary to check the "passions" of rulers and citizens alike. While he doesn't ground this in a "Divine Right" or "Natural Law," he grounds it in the practical observation that arbitrary power destroys prosperity. Therefore, for the sake of utility, the law must be applied symmetrically. It is a pragmatic, rather than principled, symmetry, but the outcome is the same.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Allowed
Skepticism, by its nature, is humble. A Humean knows that he cannot be certain of his own truths, so he is hesitant to persecute others for their errors. Hume was the great critic of his age, dismantling arguments for miracles and religious dogma. A society based on Humean skepticism is inherently tolerant because it lacks the "Absolute Certainty" required to fuel an Inquisition or a Gulag. If all beliefs are just "custom and habit," there is no justification for killing someone over a difference of opinion. The Humility Test is passed with flying colors.

### Summary
David Hume offers mixed support. Politically, he is excellent: he defends the Rule of Law, property, and caution against radical change. He is a check against utopianism. However, his Epistemological roots are dangerous to Real Reality. By denying the objective grounding of Reality and Causation, he leaves the door open for "Truth is Power." If we accept his politics but adopt his metaphysics, we lose the ability to say that freedom is "True" or "Right," we can only say it is "Pleasant for now." He is useful for the audit, but cannot be the foundation.

## 9. Confucianism (Order Match, Symmetry Mixed)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Compatible
Confucianism assumes a stable, objective moral order called the "Mandate of Heaven" (Tian Ming) and the "Way" (Dao). It is not a constructivist philosophy; it believes that proper relationships and rituals reflect a cosmic reality. The "Rectification of Names" (Zhengming) argues that language must correspond to reality; a father must actually *be* a father in conduct, not just in name. This alignment with an objective, discoverable moral order matches the "Reality is Knowable" principle. It is a philosophy grounded in the observation of nature and society, seeking to harmonize human conduct with the order of the universe.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Mixed
There is a significant debate within Confucianism between Mencius (Human nature is good) and Xunzi (Human nature is bad/unformed). However, the dominant Neo-Confucian strain tends toward the idea that humans are perfectible through education and ritual (`Li`). This belief in the potential for distinct moral perfection, particularly of the "Sage King" or the "Junzi" (Gentleman), can friction with the Western "Constrained Vision" which doubts that any human can be trusted with absolute power. The Confucian hope for a "Benevolent Ruler" who needs no checks and balances because of his virtue is a dangerous utopianism that the principle of Limited Humanity rejects.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Strong Match
Confucianism is the world's premier philosophy of "Tiered Responsibility." The "Five Bonds" (Ruler-Subject, Father-Son, Husband-Wife, Elder-Younger Brother, Friend-Friend) explicitly define duty based on relationship distance and hierarchy. A son has a specific, overriding duty to his father (Filial Piety) that he does not have to a stranger. This perfectly aligns with the rejection of "Universal Responsibility." By building society on the strength of the family unit and local obligations, Confucianism ensures that every individual is anchored in a web of specific, concrete duties rather than vague abstractions.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Compatible
The goal of Confucianism is *Ren* (Benevolence/Humaneness) and social Harmony. It is deeply concerned with the flourishing of the populace. A ruler who fails to provide for the people loses the Mandate of Heaven and can be overthrown. The focus is on stability, prosperity, and the generational continuity of the family line, which aligns with "Life should continue and continue well." It is not an ascetic faith; it values culture, music, food, and family life as the proper expressions of the Way.

### Procedural Symmetry: Incompatible
Confucian justice is fundamentally hierarchical, not symmetrical. "Let the ruler be a ruler, the subject a subject." Justice means acting according to one's station, not treating everyone as identical units. A son covering up for his father's crime is considered virtuous in Confucian ethics, whereas in a Symmetrical system, it might be obstruction of justice. The "Rule of Law" (Legalism) was historically the enemy of Confucianism, which preferred the "Rule of Man" (Virtue). This reliance on status and hierarchy violates "Procedural Symmetry," which demands that the law is blind to rank.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Persecuted (If Disruptive)
In Imperial China, the concept of "Remonstrance" existed, the duty of an official to critique the Emperor. However, this was a dangerous duty often met with execution or exile. The overarching value of Confucianism is "Harmony" and "Order." Public, raucous critique that threatens social stability is viewed as unvirtuous. In a Confucian Hegemony, the "Humility Test" often fails because the preservation of the hierarchy's dignity is considered more important than the exposure of error. The "face" of the father/ruler must be maintained, suppressing the open feedback loop required for correction.

### Summary
Confucianism is a hierarchical alternative to these principles. It is a highly developed system of "Tiered Responsibility" and social stability, offering a civilization that has lasted millennia. However, its fundamental incompatibility with "Procedural Symmetry" and its reliance on the "Perfectible Ruler" rather than the "Rule of Law" make it distinct from the specific operational parameters of this framework. It builds a stable Family-State, but not a Free Society of equal citizens. It can coexist as a private practice, but as a hegemonic system, it would dismantle the legal equality this framework requires.

## 10. Shinto (Nature Alignment, Ethical Mixed)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Compatible
Shinto is deeply "World-Affirming." It views Nature itself as the manifestation of the Divine (*Kami*). Rocks, trees, rivers, and mountains are spirited. This rejects the Gnostic idea that the world is an illusion or a prison. For the Shintoist, the physical territory of Japan is sacred and real. This supports an environmental and physical realism. It is not an abstract theology but a concrete engagement with the land and the seasons, aligning with "Reality is Objective (and Sacred)."

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Mixed
Shinto lacks a concept of "Original Sin." Humans are viewed as "Children of the Kami," born pure. Evil is understood as *Kegare* (Pollution) or *Tsumi* (Offense), external impurities that "stick" to a person and must be washed away through ritual purification (*Harae*). This suggests that evil is not an internal condition of the heart but an external accident. This friction makes Shinto vulnerable to nationalist utopianism; if we just purify the nation of foreign elements, we return to the "Age of the Gods." It underestimates the internal capacity for evil within the "Pure" citizen.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Mixed
Shinto responsibility is often collective rather than individual. The village or the nation is the unit of account. If a member of the community offends the Kami, the whole community may suffer a calamity. This creates a "Collective Responsibility" that overrides the individual. In a system of Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility, the individual is the primary unit of moral agency. While Shinto promotes strong community bonds (`Wa` - Harmony), it risks submerging the individual conscience into the collective will of the "Volk," blurring the tiered lines of personal accountability.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Compatible
The concept of *Musubi* (Creative connection/Growth) is central to Shinto. It values fertility, growth, vitality, and the continuation of life. It is an explicitly life-affirming religion, celebrating births and harvests. Death is seen as pollution. This aligns aggressively with "Life should continue and continue well." Shinto aims for the vitality of the land and the people, rejecting any philosophy that seeks the cessation of existence.

### Procedural Symmetry: Mixed (Particularist)
Shinto is an "Ethnic Faith." It is historically bound to the land of Japan and the Japanese people. It does not claim to be a universal truth for all mankind in the way Christianity or Science does. Therefore, its "Symmetry" is internal to the tribe (all Japanese are children of the Emperor) but not external (Japan is the Land of the Gods, others are less divine). This "Particularism" violates the universal application of Procedural Symmetry. You cannot easily build a universal law on a faith that privileges a specific bloodline and soil.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Persecuted (Imperial)
Historically, State Shinto was the engine of Imperial Hegemony. The Emperor was considered a "Living Kami" (Arahitogami). To critique the Emperor or the national essence (*Kokutai*) was not just treason; it was blasphemy: *Lese-majeste*. There was zero room for the Humility Test. The system demanded absolute spiritual and political submission to the divine hierarchy. While modern "Shrine Shinto" is peaceful, the "State Shinto" model proves that at its hegemonic peak, it tolerates no rivals and no critique.

### Summary
Shinto is a culturally specific system. Its reverence for nature and life-affirming ethos (*Musubi*) are beautiful and compatible with the flourishing component of these principles. While it works as a cultural underpinning for a specific people, its ethno-centric particularism, denial of internal human flaw (evil is just external pollution), and historical fusion with divine absolutism make it difficult to universalize as the software for a diverse society based on Procedural Symmetry.

## 11. John Rawls (Justice as Fairness)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Incompatible
Rawls implements a "Method of Avoidance" regarding deep metaphysical truths. To achieve an "Overlapping Consensus," he argues we should set aside our deep beliefs about reality. This is not just "friction." It is a fundamental failure of perspective. By refusing to anchor rights in a deeper "Order of Being" (like Natural Law), Rawls leaves rights suspended in the void. The principle of Real Reality asserts that truth is not just whatever we agree on (Consensus); it is what actually exists (Reality). Ignoring the foundation is not a valid strategy for building a house.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Incompatible
Rawls fails to account for the reality of human difference. By treating "natural talents" (intelligence, strength, drive) as "arbitrary" and thus a "common asset," he denies the biological reality of the individual. Genetics are real; humans are different. These differences are not "mistakes" to be corrected by the state, but features of reality. His system attempts to engineer a "Fairness" that contradicts the natural, genetic reality of human diversity, treating people as interchangeable units to be leveled rather than distinct agents to be respected.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Incompatible
Here Rawls fundamentally breaks with Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility. His "Difference Principle" asserts that inequalities are only justified if they benefit the least advantaged. This effectively makes the productive individual responsible for the non-productive by default. It transforms "Responsibility" from a tiered duty (I start with myself and my family) into a universal lien on my effort. If my success is not "allowed" unless it benefits a stranger, then I am not an owner of my own effort, but a steward for the collective. This collectivizes responsibility and undermines the local autonomy of the family unit.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Compatible
Rawls bases his system on "Primary Goods," the basic rights, liberties, and opportunities that any rational person would want to pursue their plan of life. This is a functional definition of "Flourishing." He does not define the "Good Life" for you (teleologically), but secures the means for you to define it yourself. This aligns with "Life should continue and continue well" by prioritizing the material and legal basics required for survival and agency. He rejects nihilism and seeks a society that supports the rational life-plans of its citizens.

### Procedural Symmetry: Incompatible
While Rawls claims his system is "Fairness," it introduces a deep "Procedural Asymmetry" regarding talent. He explicitly states that "the distribution of natural talents is a common asset." This means your intelligence or strength is not yours; it belongs to the collective. This creates an asymmetry where the State has a claim on the internal attributes of the Individual. In a truly Symmetrical system (Locke/Kant), the State treats everyone's person as inviolable. In Rawls, the "Talented" are legally encumbered in a way the "Untalented" are not, violating the principle of equal treatment under the law.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Persecuted (Soft Totalitarianism)
Rawlsian Hegemony (the modern Managerial State) uses the concept of "Public Reason" to exclude certain types of critique. If your argument rests on a "Comprehensive Doctrine" (like religion), it is deemed "Unreasonable" and inadmissible in the public square. You must translate your beliefs into secular, liberal language to be heard. This functions as a "Soft Totalitarianism." While you aren't jailed, you are structurally silenced. The system claims humility ("we don't judge your private beliefs") while aggressively policing the boundaries of "Reasonable" discourse to exclude anything that challenges the liberal consensus.

### Summary
John Rawls presents a deceptively similar alternative to the modern state. He mimics the language of these principles (fairness and rights), making him seem compatible. However, he inverts the principles of Responsibility and Symmetry. By treating individual talent as a "common asset," he provides the philosophical justification for the unlimited redistributionist state, turning citizens into resources for the collective. His concept of "Public Reason" acts as a filter that slowly suffocates deep metaphysical critique, replacing the open "battle of ideas" with a managed consensus that excludes the sacred.

## 12. Postmodernism

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Incompatible
Postmodernism begins with the rejection of "Metanarratives," and the first metanarrative it rejects is Objective Reality itself. Thinkers like Derrida ("Il n'y a pas de hors-texte") and Foucault argue that "Truth" is not a correspondence with reality, but a product of power relations and language games. This is a "Social Constructivism" that is antithetical to Real Reality. If there is no objective territory, then there is no "Shared Reality," only warring interpretations. Science is just "Western ways of knowing," and logic is "Logocentrism." This dissolves the very ground upon which these principles stand.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Incompatible
Postmodernism rejects the idea of a fixed "Human Nature." We are blank slates inscribed by culture, language, and power structures. There is no "essential self." This denies the "Flawed/Constrained" axiom because it suggests that if we change the language and the structures, we can fundamentally change the human being. This leads to the "Unconstrained Vision," the belief that we can engineer a new type of human by policing speech and deconstructing norms. It ignores the biological and psychological hard-wiring that Limited Humanity respects.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Incompatible
If the self is a social construct, then "Individual Responsibility" is a myth. Postmodernism systematically shifts the locus of agency from the Individual to the Structure (Systemic Racism, Patriarchy, Capitalism). If I commit a crime, it is not my moral failure; it is the result of my "positionality" within the matrix of power. This "Structural Determinism" destroys Tiered Responsibility. It relieves the individual of the burden of their actions and places infinite guilt on the "System," leading to a society of permanent grievance and no personal accountability.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Incompatible
Postmodernism is fundamentally "Critical" rather than "Constructive." Its tool is Deconstruction, tearing things apart to reveal power dynamics. It views the concept of "The Good" or "Flourishing" with suspicion, seeing them as masks for oppression (e.g., "Family Values" is just code for heteronormativity). It lacks a positive definition of Goodness. It knows what it is *against* (Hierarchy, Power usually), but it struggles to articulate what it is *for*, other than an abstract liberation that tends toward entropy. It cannot build; it can only dismantle.

### Procedural Symmetry: Incompatible
The goal of Postmodern "Social Justice" is not Procedural Symmetry (Equality of Opportunity), but "Equity" (Equality of Outcome). Because it sees all existing structures as infected by power differentials, it argues that treating everyone the same (Colorblindness) preserves the oppression. Therefore, it demands "Asymmetrical" remedies: reverse discrimination, quotas, and speech codes that punish the "Oppressor" groups and privilege the "Oppressed" groups. This is the explicit rejection of the Rule of Law in favor of Identity Politics.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Persecuted (Repressive Tolerance)
Herbert Marcuse's concept of "Repressive Tolerance" is the operating manual here. It argues that to achieve true liberation, we must be intolerant of invalid (conservative) ideas and tolerant of valid (leftist) ideas. In the Postmodern University (Hegemony), this means that critique of the "Woke" consensus is labeled as "Violence" or "Hate Speech." Speakers are shouted down, de-platformed, or fired. There is absolute intolerance for any view that challenges the Critical Theory narrative. The Humility Test result is a failing grade: Totalitarianism.

### Summary
Postmodernism is the "Inverse Framework." It consciously inverts every single one of the 5 Principles. It denies Reality (Power is Truth), dissolves the Individual (Social Construct), erases Responsibility (Structural Determinism), mocks Goodness (Normativity is Oppression), and scorns Symmetry (Equity over Equality). It is not just a rival worldview. It is an ideology designed to dismantle civilizational cohesion. It cannot coexist with these principles because its explicit goal is to deconstruct the very categories (Reason, Truth, Nature) that this framework requires to function.

## 13. Islam (Total Submission)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Mixed (The Occasionalist Split)
Islam is centered on the absolute Reality of Allah (Al-Haqq, The Truth), which ostensibly aligns with "Real Reality." However, the dominant Ash'arite theology promotes **Occasionalism**, which denies secondary causation. It posits that fire does not burn cotton; God creates burning *at the occasion* of contact. This denial of cause-and-effect creates a form of "Ontological Nihilism" regarding the physical world. It implies that there is no fixed "Book of Nature," only the momentary habits of God's will. This friction destabilizes the scientific and legal reliability required for a "Knowable Reality," as nothing has intrinsic nature or predictable causality.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Incompatible
Islam rejects "Original Sin" in favor of *Fitra* (innate purity). It teaches that humans are born pure and only sin due to "forgetfulness" (*Ghaflah*). This high view of human nature suggests that if we just implement the perfect Law (Sharia), we can create a perfect society. It lacks the "Tragic Vision" that acknowledges the inherent corruption of the human heart. This mimics the Postmodern/Utopian error of believing that better structures can perfect the human being, ignoring our inherent flaws.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Incompatible
Islamic responsibility is tribal, not tiered. The obligations of a Muslim end at the *Ummah* (Community of Believers). Ethical duties are bifurcated: one set of rules for fellow Muslims, another for the *Kafir* (unbeliever). In the "Abode of War" (*Dar al-Harb*), established norms of truth-telling and fair dealing can be suspended for the benefit of the faith (*Taqiyya* or war ethics). This violates the universal requirement of Tiered Responsibility, where basic duties (like not lying) apply to all humans. By allowing deceit or betrayal of outsiders, it destroys the high-trust environment required by Procedural Symmetry.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Incompatible
The definition of "Good" varies depending on power. We must look at the "Two Muhammads." The Muhammad of Mecca was a persecuted prophet preaching tolerance. The Muhammad of Medina was a warlord and statesman who spread the faith by the sword. The Goodness of Medina is "Succession and Dominance," often expressing a "Value Symmetry" (Eye for an Eye) rather than a life-affirming flourishing for all. It justifies the domination of the other as a "Good," which directly conflicts with the definition of universal flourishing.

### Procedural Symmetry: Incompatible
Traditional Sharia Law is fundamentally Asymmetrical. It establishes distinct legal categories based on religion and gender. A Muslim has different rights than a *Dhimmi* (protected non-Muslim). A man has different testimony weight and inheritance rights than a woman. While *Dhimmis* are protected, they are not *equal* citizens; they pay a special tax (Jizya) and are barred from political power. This violates "Procedural Symmetry" which demands that the law be blind to identity.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Persecuted (Capital)
Which Islam holds hegemony? If it is the Islam of Mecca, it is humble. But historical Islamic Hegemony is the Islam of Medina. In this state, criticizing the Prophet or the Quran is not debate; it is Blasphemy, often a capital offense. The system demands submission. There is no distinction between "Church and State" to allow for a secular space of critique. To critique the Leader (Prophet) is to critique God.

### Summary
Islam offers a comprehensive political system (Sharia) that is structurally incompatible with Procedural Symmetry and the requirement for Humility. It fundamentally rejects the separation of the sacred and the political, demanding submission rather than open critique. Far from being a reliable defense against modern dissolution, there is a documented practical alliance between Islamist movements and Postmodern critical theories (the "Red-Green Alliance"). Additionally, theological strands that deny secondary causes (Occasionalism) create a form of ontological nihilism that denies the stability of the "Book of Nature." It fails the compatibility test.

## 14. Buddhism (The Way of Cessation)

### Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential: Incompatible
Buddhism teaches that the world of form is *Maya* (Illusion) or at least characterized by *Anicca* (Impermanence) and *Anatta* (No-Self). The goal is not to build on this reality, but to see *through* it. This clashes with "Real Reality." Western science and law are built on the assumption that "Things are Real." Buddhism is built on the assumption that "attachment to things as real is the source of suffering." This metaphysical mismatch makes it difficult to ground a society of "Rights" and "Property" which require solid ontological ground.

### Humans are Limited and Selfish: Compatible
The First Noble Truth is *Dukkha* (Suffering/Unsatisfactoriness). Buddhism has a deep, realistic view of the human condition. It knows that humans are driven by *Tanha* (Craving/Thirst) which leads to suffering. This aligns with the "Flawed" axiom. We are not naturally happy or good; we are engines of desire that need to be tamed.

### Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility: Incompatible
Karma is not Tiered Responsibility. Tiered Responsibility implies concrete duties to specific people based on relationship (Family to Neighbor to Stranger). Karma is a "Cosmic Individualism," I am solely responsible for my own spiritual state. It often encourages a detachment from social bonds (*renunciation*) to pursue enlightenment. Furthermore, the concept of "Skillful Means" or the Bodhisattva ideal can sometimes imply "sowing where you do not reap" (taking on others' burdens) or reaping where you do not sow, which violates the strict accountability the Compact relies on.

### Goodness is to Live and Live Well: Incompatible
The goal of Buddhism is *Nirvana* (Blowing Out/Cessation). The axiom of Goodness is "Life should continue." The Buddhist Axiom is "Suffering should cease (by ending the cycle of rebirth)." While compassionate, its ultimate focus points toward the Exit. A civilization built on the premise that "existence is suffering" will struggle to justify the energetic expansion and material flourishing ("Be fruitful and multiply") that the axiom of Goodness seeks. It is "Anti-Life" in the specific sense of wanting to end the cycle of *Samsara*.

### Procedural Symmetry: Incompatible
Karma represents "Value Symmetry" (Cosmic Debt/Retribution), not "Procedural Symmetry." Procedural Symmetry says "Treat X and Y the same in court." Karma says "X is suffering because he sinned in a past life." This can classify suffering as "Just" in a way that prevents intervention or legal equality. It applies a cosmic scale to human interactions that confuses the clear, procedural justice required for a state. It is a symmetry of moral physics, not a symmetry of civil rights.

### Humility (The Criticism Test): Mixed
Zen masters famously say, "If you meet the Buddha on the road, kill him." There is a tradition of radical iconoclasm and non-attachment to names and forms. However, in practice, Buddhist polities (like Tibet or Thailand) have often been Theocratic Monarchies with strict Lese-majeste laws protecting the Dharma-King (*Dharmaraja*). While the philosophy is humble (rejecting the self), the institutions can be rigid. But compared to the Abrahamic Theocracies, there is generally more room for debate and syncretism.

### Summary
Buddhism is the "Quiet Neighbor." Its emphasis on Radical Responsibility (Karma) and Egalitarianism (Dharma) makes it a peaceful and orderly presence within this framework. However, its ultimate denial of the "Real" world and the "Self" prevents it from being the *foundation* of a Western-style civilization of Rights and Property. It works best as a personal ethic rather than a foundation for the state. It passes the Humility test better than most religions but fails the Reality test required for a scientific, industrial society.
