# Hegemony Test Development Scrapbook

## The Core Question
**The Hegemony Test:** If this worldview held absolute power (Hegemony), and you were a compliant citizen but a member of a different (compatible) worldview, how would you be treated?
**Outcome:** Allowed | Persecuted | N/A (Pure Philosophy)

## Analysis by Worldview

### 1. John Locke (Liberalism)
*   **Status:** Religion/Philosophy with state implications.
*   **Hegemony Example:** Western Liberal Democracies (UK, USA).
*   **Observation:** historically, these states have protected the rights of minorities (religious and political) to exist and practice, provided they obey civil law.
*   **Quote:** "Ill will... ought not to be found in the debates of men who have truth for their aim."
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 2. Christianity (Separation of Spheres)
*   **Status:** Religion.
*   **Hegemony Example:** High Middle Ages / Christendom (at times), but specifically the *doctrinal* application in the US/West.
*   **Observation:** The doctrine "Render to Caesar" creates a structural separation. While historical persecution existed (Inquisition), the *ideal* executed in the most developed Christian nations (post-Reformation) allowed for the "Secular" space.
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 3. Thomas Reid (Common Sense)
*   **Status:** Pure Philosophy.
*   **Hegemony Example:** N/A as a state religion, but foundational to US Jurisprudence.
*   **Observation:** Defines the "Shared Reality" of the court system. It doesn't have a "purity test" for membership, just a requirement for sanity/rationality in court.
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 4. Stoicism
*   **Status:** Philosophy / Elite Code.
*   **Hegemony Example:** Roman Empire (Marcus Aurelius).
*   **Observation:** Rome was syncretic and cosmopolitan. You could worship Isis or Mithras as long as you paid taxes and didn't start rebellions. Christians were persecuted *politically* (refusing to swear loyalty), not explicitly for their theology (Rome didn't care about theology).
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 5. Scripturalism (Gordon Clark)
*   **Status:** Pure Philosophy / Theology.
*   **Hegemony Example:** None (Theoretical). Maybe Puritan Colonies?
*   **Analysis:** It denies the validity of "knowledge" from senses. If it were the State Epistemology, science courts might reject empirical evidence in favor of Scriptural deduction.
*   **Purity Test:** High. "Natural science is opinion."
*   **Result:** **N/A (Philosophy)** OR **Persecuted (Low confidence)**. *User suggests N/A is appropriate for pure philosophies.*
*   **Draft Verdict:** **N/A.**

### 6. The Jesuit Order
*   **Status:** Religious Order / Quasi-State Actor.
*   **Hegemony Example:** Paraguay Reductions (Jesuit Republics).
*   **Observation:** They created a communal theocracy protecting indigenous people from slavers, but it was a closed, paternalistic society. Rival sovereignty to the King.
*   **Purity Test:** Absolute loyalty to the Order/Pope.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted (Political Unavailability).** You couldn't really be a "dissenter" in a Reduction; you were a ward of the Order.

### 7. Immanuel Kant
*   **Status:** Pure Philosophy.
*   **Hegemony Example:** International Human Rights / UN (Philosophical ancestor).
*   **Observation:** "Perpetual Peace" requires a federation of free states. It pushes for a universal legal order.
*   **Purity Test:** "Reason." If you rely on "Revelation" or "Tradition" against "Reason," you are "immature."
*   **Result:** **Allowed (with bureaucratic friction).**

### 8. David Hume
*   **Status:** Pure Philosophy.
*   **Hegemony Example:** Modern Secular/Skeptical State.
*   **Observation:** Skepticism of absolute truth claims.
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 9. Confucianism
*   **Status:** Social Philosophy / State Cult.
*   **Hegemony Example:** Imperial China.
*   **Observation:** Strict hierarchy. "Let the ruler be ruler." Dissent that disrupts harmony is punished.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted (if disruptive).** / **Allowed (if compliant).** The test is Order, not Truth.

### 10. Shinto
*   **Status:** National Religion (Ethno-religion).
*   **Hegemony Example:** Imperial Japan (State Shinto).
*   **Observation:** Syncretic (Buddhist/Shinto mix worked for centuries). But State Shinto (1868-1945) enforced emperor worship.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted (if refusing national rites).**

### 11. John Rawls (Justice as Fairness)
*   **Status:** Political Philosophy (functions as State Religion in managerial states).
*   **Hegemony Example:** The Managerial State / HR Departments / Civil Service.
*   **Context:** Rawls argues for "Public Reason." Views that are "comprehensive doctrines" (religious/metaphysical) are PRIVATE.
*   **The Trap:** To participate in the Public Square, you must translate your views into "Public Reason" (secular, liberal language). If you cannot (e.g., "Abortion is wrong because God says so"), you are "Unreasonable" and excluded.
*   **Purity Test:** You must accept the "Political Conception of Justice."
*   **Observation:** In hegemony (University hiring, Corporate HR), if you hold "Unreasonable" views (traditional marriage, biological essentialism), you are ousted not by law, but by "policy" and "unsuitability."
*   **Result:** **Persecuted (Soft Totalitarianism).**

### 12. Postmodernism
*   **Status:** Anti-Philosophy / Ideology.
*   **Hegemony Example:** USA Liberal Universities / Activist spaces.
*   **Theory:** "Repressive Tolerance" (Marcuse). Tolerating the Intolerant (Right wing) reinforces their oppression. Therefore, "Liberating Tolerance" means suppressing the Right and tolerating the disruptive Left.
*   **Observation:** Cancellation, de-platforming, mandatory DEI statements.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted.**

### 13. Islam (Political/Doctrine)
*   **Status:** Religion + Political System.
*   **Hegemony Example:** Caliphates (Umayyad, Abbasid, Ottoman) or Modern "True" States (ISIS, Iran - though Iran is Shia, ISIS is Salafi).
*   **Observation:** *Dhimma* contract. Non-Muslims are second-class citizens (cannot build new churches, pay Jizya, testimony worth less).
*   **Result:** **Persecuted (Structural Asymmetry).**

### 14. Buddhism
*   **Status:** Religion.
*   **Hegemony Example:** Tibet (Theocracy), Thailand (Monarchy).
*   **Observation:** Generally non-proselytizing. Tibet was a feudal theocracy (serfdom), but religious persecution of *other* groups was rare compared to West/MidEast.
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

## The Criticism Test
**The Criticism Test:** Can you publicly, orderly, and non-defamatorily critique the primary tenets or founder of this worldview while living under its hegemony?
**Outcome:** Allowed | Persecuted

### 1. John Locke (Liberalism)
*   **Test:** Can you critique Liberalism/Locke in a Liberal Democracy?
*   **Observation:** Yes. You can write books attacking Liberalism (e.g., Patrick Deneen, Schmitt) without state punishment.
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 2. Christianity
*   **Test:** Can you critique Jesus or the Bible in Christendom?
*   **Observation (Historical):** Pre-modern: No (Blasphemy laws).
*   **Observation (Modern Hegemony):** Yes. In the US (Christian foundation), you can publish "The God Delusion" or "The Da Vinci Code" freely. Doctrinal support for "Apologetics" (defense) implies the existence of attack.
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 3. Thomas Reid
*   **Test:** Can you critique Common Sense Realism?
*   **Observation:** Yes, it's academic debate.
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 4. Stoicism
*   **Test:** Can you critique Marcus Aurelius or Zeno in Rome?
*   **Observation:** Yes. Philosophical schools (Cynics, Epicureans) debated openly. Mockery was common (Lucian).
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 5. Scripturalism
*   **Test:** Can you critique Gordon Clark or the axiom "The Bible is the only source of truth"?
*   **Observation:** In a theoretical Scripturalist State (Theonomy), this might be Blasphemy/Heresy.
*   **Result:** **Likely Persecuted.**

### 6. The Jesuit Order
*   **Test:** Can you critique the Pope/Ignatius in a Reduction?
*   **Observation:** No. The structure was military/theocratic obedience.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted.**

### 7. Immanuel Kant
*   **Test:** Can you critique the Categorical Imperative?
*   **Observation:** Yes. Academic philosophy thrives on this.
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 8. David Hume
*   **Test:** Can you critique Skepticism?
*   **Observation:** Yes. Reid did exactly this.
*   **Result:** **Allowed.**

### 9. Confucianism
*   **Test:** Can you critique Confucius or the Emperor?
*   **Observation:** "Remonstrance" is a formal duty, but highly dangerous. Open public critique of the Emperor's virtue usually resulted in death or exile. Critique of Confucius himself was historically suppressed.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted.**

### 10. Shinto
*   **Test:** Can you critique the Emperor's divinity?
*   **Observation:** Absolutely not in Imperial Japan. Lese-majeste laws.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted.**

### 11. John Rawls (The Managerial State)
*   **Test:** Can you critique "Identity Politics" or "Social Justice" in the modern University/HR regime?
*   **Observation:** You will be labeled "Racist," "Sexist," or "Creating an Unsafe Environment." You will face social sanctions, firing, or de-platforming. It is not "illegal," but it is effectively punished by the institutions holding hegemony.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted (Socially/Professionally).**

### 12. Postmodernism
*   **Test:** Can you critique "The Science" or Gender Theory in a Postmodern Hegemony?
*   **Observation:** No. Dissent is framed as "Violence" or "Hate Speech." As Marcuse argued, "Liberating Tolerance" requires *not* tolerating the critique from the Right.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted.**

### 13. Islam
*   **Test:** Can you critique the Quran or Muhammad?
*   **Observation:** No. Blasphemy is a capital offense in Sharia. Even in "moderate" states, it leads to imprisonment or mob violence.
*   **Result:** **Persecuted (Death/Imprisonment).**

### 14. Buddhism
*   **Test:** Can you critique the Buddha?
*   **Observation:** Generally yes. "If you meet the Buddha on the road, kill him" (Koan). The tradition invites inquiry ("Ehipassiko"). However, critiquing the *King* in Buddhist Thailand is a crime (Lese-majeste).
*   **Result:** **Allowed (Doctrinally) / Mixed (Politically).**
