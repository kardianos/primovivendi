### 1.1 Chapter 3.1: Freedom of Speech (Expanded)

**Current State**: 1 line
**Required State**: Full chapter with philosophical grounding, applications, and examples

```
{3.1|chapter} Align With Truth: Freedom of Speech
{3.1.1|idea:C045} The procedure of public discourse must remain available to all.
{3.1.1.1|explanation} Freedom of speech is not a mere policy preference; it is the procedural mechanism by which truth is sought in the public square.
{3.1.1.2|tie:C015} If truth corresponds to reality, and reality is discoverable, then we must allow the process of discovery to occur.
{3.1.1.3|tie:P001} Procedural Symmetry requires that the same access to discourse be granted to all positions, for the truth cannot be known in advance.

{3.1.2|idea:speech_epistemic} Speech is the epistemic tool of a free society.
{3.1.2.1|explanation} Ideas must be tested against reality and against competing ideas. This testing requires expression.
{3.1.2.2|tie:C018} Because knowledge is provisional, we cannot grant any authority the power to declare inquiry complete.
{3.1.2.3|tie:C024} The same scrutiny applied to heretical ideas must be applied to orthodox ones.

{3.1.3|idea:speech_limits} Freedom of speech is not absolute, but its limits must be procedurally defined.
{3.1.3.1|explanation} Defamation (false statements of fact that damage), incitement to imminent violence, and fraud are not protected because they are not truth seeking; they are harm causing.
{3.1.3.2|tie:P001} The key test: Are the limits applied symmetrically? A rule against incitement must apply equally to all factions.
{3.1.3.3|distinction} The distinction is between speech (expression of ideas) and action (direct harm). Words that ARE actions (contract, threat, fraud) can be regulated; words that DESCRIBE ideas cannot.

{3.1.4|idea:speech_uncomfortable} Uncomfortable speech is the speech that matters most.
{3.1.4.1|explanation} Speech that everyone agrees with needs no protection. The test of commitment to free speech is whether we protect speech we find offensive.
{3.1.4.2|tie:C023} Truth is indifferent to our feelings. A society that silences uncomfortable truths will drift from reality.
{3.1.4.3|example:E056} The heliocentric model was offensive to established authorities. Silencing Galileo did not make geocentrism true.

{3.1.5|application_example} Historical Examples: Speech Suppression and Consequences.
{3.1.5.1|example:E057} Soviet Lysenkoism: Suppressing genetic science led to agricultural catastrophe and famine.
{3.1.5.2|example:E058} Medieval Index of Forbidden Books: Delayed scientific progress by centuries in Catholic Europe.
{3.1.5.3|example:E059} Modern social media censorship: Suppression of lab leak hypothesis delayed investigation into COVID origins.
{3.1.5.4|tie:C010} In each case, reality did not change because speech was suppressed. The consequences merely arrived later and with greater severity.

{3.1.6|idea:speech_institutional} Institutional capture is the modern threat to free speech.
{3.1.6.1|explanation} When institutions (universities, media, platforms) enforce ideological conformity, speech is suppressed without government action.
{3.1.6.2|tie:C127} This is selective application: viewpoints favored by institutional gatekeepers pass, while disfavored viewpoints are labeled misinformation.
{3.1.6.3|tie:P001} The remedy is Procedural Symmetry: rules about content must apply equally regardless of viewpoint.

{3.1.7|insight} Key Insight: A society that cannot speak cannot think. A society that cannot think cannot align with reality.
{3.1.7.1|tie:C007} Reality is real. Speech is how we discover it together.
{3.1.7.2|tie:C013} Reason untethered to open discourse is groupthink, which is a form of collective hallucination.
```

### 1.2 Chapter 3.3: Responsibility and Ownership Extended (Expanded)

**Current State**: 4 name tags
**Required State**: Full treatment of each concept

```
{3.3|chapter} Responsibility and Ownership Extended
{3.3.1|section} Property as an Extension of Individual Responsibility
{3.3.1.1|idea:C200} Property is not arbitrary convention; it is the material extension of personal agency.
{3.3.1.2|tie:C033} If responsibility begins with the self, then the fruits of one's labor must belong to the self.
{3.3.1.3|tie:C108} The CONSEQUENCE that property solves: Without secure ownership, no one invests in improvement. Why plant a tree if another can take the fruit?
{3.3.1.4|ref:Locke} "Every man has a property in his own person... The labour of his body, and the work of his hands, we may say, are properly his."
{3.3.1.5|tie:P001} Property rights must be procedurally symmetric: the same rules of ownership apply to the peasant and the prince.
{3.3.1.6|example:E060} The tragedy of the commons: When no one owns a resource, everyone depletes it. Ownership creates stewardship.

{3.3.2|section} Markets and Specialization
{3.3.2.1|idea:C201} Markets are the coordination mechanism for specialized knowledge.
{3.3.2.2|tie:C025} Humans are limited. No central planner can know what millions of individuals know about their local circumstances.
{3.3.2.3|tie:C011} Markets are DISCOVERED, not invented. They emerge wherever humans specialize and trade.
{3.3.2.4|idea:C109} Money solves the double coincidence of wants: I need not find someone who both has what I want AND wants what I have.
{3.3.2.5|example:E061} The Soviet calculation problem: Without market prices, planners could not know what to produce or in what quantities. Result: shortages and surpluses simultaneously.
{3.3.2.6|tie:C010} Markets are reality's feedback mechanism. Prices signal scarcity and abundance. Ignore them and reality punishes.

{3.3.3|section} Sound Money
{3.3.3.1|idea:C202} Money is stored responsibility; sound money preserves that store.
{3.3.3.2|explanation} When you work and save, you defer consumption. You trust that your stored labor will be redeemable later.
{3.3.3.3|tie:C038} Sound money enables generational planning. You can save for your children's future.
{3.3.3.4|idea:C203} Inflation is a hidden tax that violates Procedural Symmetry.
{3.3.3.5|explanation} Those closest to new money (banks, government) benefit; those furthest (savers, workers) lose. This is asymmetric extraction.
{3.3.3.6|tie:P001} Sound money applies the same purchasing power to all holders, regardless of their proximity to the money printer.
{3.3.3.7|example:E062} Weimar Germany: Hyperinflation destroyed the middle class savings, destabilizing society and enabling extremism.

{3.3.4|section} Power to Defend Yourself
{3.3.4.1|idea:C204} The right to self defense is the right to exist.
{3.3.4.2|tie:C035} Responsibility must recognize enemies. If enemies exist, defense must be possible.
{3.3.4.3|tie:C033} If responsibility begins with the self, the self must have the means to fulfill that responsibility.
{3.3.4.4|explanation} This does not mean vigilantism. The Rule of Law provides systematic defense. But when seconds count, the police are minutes away.
{3.3.4.5|tie:C034} Defense radiates outward: self, then family, then community. A father who cannot protect his children has failed his primary responsibility.
{3.3.4.6|example:E011} The Moriori: Their pacifist covenant left them defenseless. When enemies arrived, they were massacred and enslaved.
{3.3.4.7|tie:P001} The right to self defense must be procedurally symmetric: available to all law abiding citizens, not just the powerful who can afford private security.

{3.3.5|insight} Key Insight: Property, markets, money, and defense are not luxuries; they are the material infrastructure of responsibility.
{3.3.5.1|tie:C032} Responsibility is behavior capable of creating and maintaining. These institutions make creation and maintenance possible.
```

### 1.3 Chapter 3.5: The State's Role in Human Goodness (Expanded)

**Current State**: 4 name tags
**Required State**: Full treatment with constraints

```
{3.5|chapter} The State's Role in Human Goodness
{3.5.0|idea:state_limited} The state's role is to enable, not to provide, goodness.
{3.5.0.1|explanation} The state cannot make people good. It can only create conditions where good choices are possible and bad choices have consequences.
{3.5.0.2|tie:C025} Humans are limited. A state that tries to do everything will do nothing well.
{3.5.0.3|tie:C034} Tiered Responsibility means most goodness comes from individuals, families, and communities. The state is the outermost ring, not the center.

{3.5.1|section} Enabling Families Through Housing Policy
{3.5.1.1|idea:C205} Family formation requires physical space. Zoning policy either enables or prevents this.
{3.5.1.2|tie:C038} If life should continue, families must be able to form. If families need homes, homes must be buildable.
{3.5.1.3|application} Zoning that prohibits multi family housing in most areas creates artificial scarcity, driving up costs and preventing family formation.
{3.5.1.4|application} Excessive regulation (parking minimums, setback requirements, height limits) increases construction costs beyond what young families can afford.
{3.5.1.5|tie:P001} Procedural Symmetry: Existing homeowners should not have veto power over new construction that new families would use.
{3.5.1.6|example:E063} Japan's national zoning: By preventing local NIMBY vetoes, Japan kept housing affordable despite urbanization.
{3.5.1.7|example:E064} California's housing crisis: Restrictive zoning created the highest housing costs in America, driving out young families.

{3.5.2|section} Safety Policies
{3.5.2.1|idea:C206} Physical safety is the precondition for all other goods.
{3.5.2.2|tie:C038} Life must continue before it can continue well. The first duty of the state is to prevent murder, assault, and theft.
{3.5.2.3|tie:C059} The Rule of Law is the formalized system of judgment. It must be enforced consistently.
{3.5.2.4|application} Selective enforcement (ignoring crimes in certain areas or by certain groups) violates Procedural Symmetry and abandons citizens.
{3.5.2.5|tie:P012} When societies govern behavior by consequences, they remain adaptive without becoming tyrannical.
{3.5.2.6|tie:C127} When consequences are removed (decriminalization without alternatives), reality delivers feedback through increased victimization.

{3.5.3|section} Minimal Pro Natal Policies
{3.5.3.1|idea:C207} A society that does not replace itself has chosen slow death.
{3.5.3.2|tie:C039} Life should continue. This requires new life. A fertility rate below replacement is civilizational failure.
{3.5.3.3|explanation} "Minimal" because the state cannot and should not force reproduction. It can only remove obstacles and reduce costs.
{3.5.3.4|application} Tax policy: Recognizing that children are the future taxpayers, not a lifestyle choice to be penalized.
{3.5.3.5|application} Parental leave: Allowing recovery and bonding without career destruction.
{3.5.3.6|application} Childcare: Not state provision (which crowds out family and community), but removing regulatory barriers to affordable options.
{3.5.3.7|tie:C034} The state supports families; it does not replace them. Parents raise children, not bureaucrats.

{3.5.4|section} Education That Reinforces Responsibility
{3.5.4.1|idea:C208} Education transmits civilization across generations.
{3.5.4.2|tie:C031} Man starts out selfish and savage. Society civilizes him. Education is the primary mechanism.
{3.5.4.3|application} Core content: Literacy, numeracy, history, and civics. The tools to participate in civilization.
{3.5.4.4|application} Responsibility focus: Consequences for actions, deferred gratification, work ethic.
{3.5.4.5|tie:C072} Education should cultivate internal locus of control, not victim identity.
{3.5.4.6|tie:C029} Education should teach that goodness must be learned, not that children are born perfect and corrupted by society.
{3.5.4.7|warning} Education should NOT be activism. Children taking on societal responsibilities inverts tiered responsibility (see 5.2.5).

{3.5.5|section} What the State Should NOT Do
{3.5.5.1|idea:C209} The state must not attempt to provide what only individuals and families can provide.
{3.5.5.2|tie:C025} The state cannot love. It cannot provide meaning. It cannot make someone virtuous.
{3.5.5.3|application} The state should not replace fathers with welfare checks.
{3.5.5.4|application} The state should not replace community with bureaucracy.
{3.5.5.5|application} The state should not replace religion with ideology.
{3.5.5.6|tie:P016} If individuals and families abandon self rule, the state must fill the void with invasiveness. Better that it not be necessary.

{3.5.6|insight} Key Insight: The state is the skeleton, not the flesh. It provides structure; life comes from individuals, families, and communities.
```

```
{2.2|chapter} Seek Excellence
{2.2.1|idea:C080} Humility should not be confused with its common caricatures of low self esteem or passivity, for it is the practice of valuing being correct over having been right.
{2.2.1.1|idea:C081} The opposite is arrogance: the mindset of the ideologue who believes their map of reality is perfect and seeks to bend the territory to fit it.
{2.2.1.2|explanation} Humility is epistemic, not social. It is not about self deprecation; it is about updating beliefs when evidence demands it.
{2.2.1.3|tie:C018} Because knowledge is provisional, humility is the rational response to our limited condition.
{2.2.1.4|example:E065} The scientist who abandons a cherished hypothesis when experiments disprove it demonstrates humility. The scientist who hides contrary data demonstrates arrogance.

{2.2.2|idea:C082} Excellence should not be confused with perfectionism, which is often a mask for fear.
{2.2.2.1|idea:C083} Excellence, by contrast, is a commitment to process: doing the best one can with the knowledge and resources available today, and then striving, with humility, to do it even better tomorrow.
{2.2.2.2|tie:C077} Perfectionism leads to paralysis: nothing is ever good enough to begin. Excellence embraces action with available means.
{2.2.2.3|tie:C078} Competence is the byproduct of action, not the prerequisite for it.

{2.2.3|idea:excellence_domains} Excellence applies to all domains of life.
{2.2.3.1|example:E019} In work: The craftsman who takes pride in quality versus the employee who does minimum to avoid termination.
{2.2.3.2|example:E066} In body: Maintaining physical health through discipline, not for vanity, but for capacity to act.
{2.2.3.3|example:E067} In relationships: Showing up fully, listening attentively, keeping commitments.
{2.2.3.4|example:E068} In thought: Pursuing truth rigorously, not settling for comfortable errors.

{2.2.4|idea:excellence_incremental} Excellence is incremental, not sudden.
{2.2.4.1|explanation} One does not become excellent overnight. It is the accumulation of small improvements over time.
{2.2.4.2|tie:C041} Living well is a vector, not a destination. Excellence is measured by the slope, not the position.
{2.2.4.3|application} The question is not "Am I excellent?" but "Am I more excellent today than yesterday?"

{2.2.5|idea:excellence_vs_mediocrity} The alternative to excellence is mediocrity, which is a form of slow death.
{2.2.5.1|tie:C038} If life should continue WELL, then coasting is insufficient. Good enough is the enemy of good.
{2.2.5.2|explanation} Mediocrity is comfortable in the short term and catastrophic in the long term. Skills atrophy. Relationships decay. Meaning evaporates.

{2.2.6|insight} These two virtues, humility and excellence, are intertwined.
{2.2.6.1|explanation} Humility without excellence is mere passivity. Excellence without humility is arrogant perfectionism.
{2.2.6.2|tie:C007} Humility aligns us with reality (we are fallible). Excellence propels us forward within that reality.
```

### 2.2 Chapter 2.6: Have Faith (Expanded)

**Current State**: Brief treatment
**Required State**: Full defense of faith concept

```
{2.6|chapter} Have Faith
{2.6.0|definition} What "Faith" Means in This Context.
{2.6.0.1|explanation} Faith here is NOT blind belief in the unprovable. It is trust extended beyond immediate verification.
{2.6.0.2|explanation} Every time you plant a seed, you exercise faith that the sun will rise and rain will fall. Every time you teach a child, you exercise faith that they will grow to use what you taught.
{2.6.0.3|distinction} This is MINIMAL faith: the belief that the future is real, that our actions matter, and that investment in what we cannot yet see can bear fruit.
{2.6.0.4|explanation} For many, faith is MORE than this (religious faith in God, providence, afterlife). That is permitted. But the framework requires at least this minimum.

{2.6.1|idea:P017} Faith is required in social and generational symmetry.
{2.6.1.1|idea:C049} As we received from predecessors, we give to successors.
{2.6.1.2|explanation} Your grandparents planted trees whose shade they would never enjoy. You sit in that shade. Will you plant trees for your grandchildren?
{2.6.1.3|tie:C034} Tiered Responsibility extends across time, not just across space. You have duties to the future, not just to the present.

{2.6.2|idea:faith_necessary} Why Faith is Necessary.
{2.6.2.1|tie:C025} Humans are limited. We cannot see the future. We cannot guarantee outcomes.
{2.6.2.2|explanation} Without faith, we would never invest in anything beyond immediate gratification. Why save for retirement? Why raise children? Why build institutions?
{2.6.2.3|tie:C038} If life should continue, someone must invest in that continuation without certainty of return.

{2.6.3|idea:faith_vs_naivete} Faith is Not Naivete.
{2.6.3.1|explanation} Faith is trust extended based on reason and experience, not despite them.
{2.6.3.2|tie:C007} We have faith that the sun will rise because it has risen every day. We have faith that hard work tends to produce results because it has before.
{2.6.3.3|distinction} Naivete ignores evidence. Faith incorporates evidence and extends trust beyond it.

{2.6.4|idea:C038} We work and sacrifice today for a future we may not see.
{2.6.4.1|example:E069} Cathedral builders: Medieval craftsmen worked their entire lives on structures they would never see completed. Faith that the work mattered.
{2.6.4.2|example:E070} Parents: You pour resources into children without knowing what they will become. Faith that the investment matters.

{2.6.5|idea:C039} This is the essence of generational goodness: life should continue and continue well.
{2.6.5.1|tie:C041} We have faith that the future can endure, not just survive, but have joy and meaning.
{2.6.5.2|explanation} Without this faith, why would anyone sacrifice present pleasure for future generations? Nihilism says: eat, drink, for tomorrow we die.

{2.6.6|insight} We believe our efforts matter beyond our immediate gratification.
{2.6.6.1|tie:C007} Faith aligns with reality: The future IS real, even if we don't see it yet.
{2.6.6.2|tie:C015} Truth includes truths about the future. Our actions have consequences that extend beyond our observation.

{2.6.7|idea:faith_compound} Faith in compounding.
{2.6.7.1|explanation} We trust that responsibility, truth, and beauty compound across generations.
{2.6.7.2|example:E071} Institutions: The rule of law, built over centuries, compounds trust. Each generation that honors it strengthens it for the next.
{2.6.7.3|example:E072} Knowledge: Each generation builds on the discoveries of the past. We stand on the shoulders of giants.

{2.6.8|quote:Q002} Will to live manifests in creating and nurturing new life.
{2.6.8.1|tie:C025} We have faith that our children will face reality and choose well, even though we cannot control their choices.
```

### 2.3 Chapter 1.4: What Must Be Good (Expanded)

**Current State**: 6 lines
**Required State**: Full defense of the axiom

```
{1.4|chapter} What Must Be Good
{1.4.0|idea:axiom_necessity} Why We Need an Axiom of Goodness.
{1.4.0.1|explanation} Reality tells us what IS. It does not tell us what OUGHT to be. We need a bridge from fact to value.
{1.4.0.2|explanation} This axiom is not derived; it is chosen. But it is not arbitrary. It is the minimal choice that makes all other choices meaningful.
{1.4.0.3|tie:C151} Survival is the precondition for all value. You cannot pursue any good if you do not exist.

{1.4.1|idea:C038} What we consider good must include that human life should continue and continue well, both generationally and individually.
{1.4.1.1|explanation} This is not a complete definition of good. It is the MINIMUM content that any definition of good must include for a functional society.
{1.4.1.2|tie:C007} This axiom is compatible with reality: it describes what living things actually do (seek to survive and thrive).

{1.4.2|idea:C039} The first part is that life should continue, which speaks to both individual survival and, crucially, to generational survival.
{1.4.2.1|explanation} Individual survival: You should not seek your own death, and others should not seek your death without just cause.
{1.4.2.2|explanation} Generational survival: The human project should extend beyond our individual lives. Children should be born and raised.
{1.4.2.3|tie:C156} This philosophy is human centered. We are human; our axiom concerns human flourishing.

{1.4.3|idea:C040} The second part, that it continue well, elevates this philosophy beyond mere animal survival.
{1.4.3.1|explanation} Animals survive. Humans seek meaning, beauty, truth, and excellence. "Well" includes these higher goods.
{1.4.3.2|tie:C084} Beauty is a sign that life is thriving, not merely persisting.

{1.4.4|idea:C041} Living well is a vector, a direction, not a location, measured by the positive slope between two points in time.
{1.4.4.1|explanation} There is no fixed destination called "The Good Life" that you arrive at and stop. There is only the direction you are moving.
{1.4.4.2|application} The question is not "Have you arrived?" but "Are you improving?"
{1.4.4.3|tie:C083} Excellence is process, not perfection. Living well is trajectory, not position.

{1.4.5|idea:axiom_defense} Defense Against Objections.
{1.4.5.1|distinction:nihilism} Against Nihilism: The nihilist says nothing matters. But the nihilist still eats breakfast. They act as if their survival matters even if they deny it verbally. The axiom aligns with revealed preference.
{1.4.5.2|distinction:buddhism} Against Buddhist Cessation: Buddhism seeks to end the cycle of suffering by ending desire and rebirth. This framework respects that goal for individuals but asserts that a SOCIETY cannot be built on the goal of non existence.
{1.4.5.3|distinction:hedonism} Against Pure Hedonism: Pleasure is good, but pleasure that destroys the capacity for future pleasure (addiction, excess) is not "continuing well." The axiom includes a time dimension that hedonism lacks.

{1.4.6|idea:C042} To evaluate any action, system, or idea, we can ask: On balance, and over the long term, does this contribute to or detract from humans living and continuing well?
{1.4.6.1|explanation} This is the practical test. It does not require metaphysical certainty. It requires empirical observation.
{1.4.6.2|tie:D008} Reason proposes policies. Reality judges outcomes. Policies that reliably lead to death and suffering fail the test.

{1.4.7|insight} Key Insight: This axiom is minimal, not comprehensive. It leaves room for diverse visions of the good life while excluding visions that demand death or suffering.
```

```
{0.1.0|idea:modern_problem} The Problem: A World Without Shared Ground.

{0.1.1.3|idea:C006b} Rather, it is a definition of the necessary axioms required for free people to coexist without killing or subjugating one another.
{0.1.1.4|explanation} These axioms are MINIMAL. They do not tell you how to live your life. They tell you what you must accept to live alongside others who live differently.

{0.1.2|idea:C006c} The Circle of Compatibility.

{0.1.2.3|idea:P020} The Humility Test: Does this worldview possess the humility to allow itself to be critiqued, rather than demanding submission?

{0.1.3.3|explanation} We focus on WHAT works and HOW it works.
{0.1.3.4|explanation} Different people may have different "Whys" (God, Reason, Tradition) for following these rules, and that difference is permitted.
{0.1.3.5|explanation} However, those whose "Why" compels them to destroy the "How" cannot be part of this society.

{0.1.4|idea:not_replacement} What This Book Is NOT.
{0.1.4.1|explanation} This is not a total worldview replacement. It does not tell you what to believe about God, the afterlife, or the meaning of existence.
{0.1.4.2|explanation} This is not a political manifesto. It does not endorse a party or a specific policy platform.
{0.1.4.3|explanation} This is a set of foundational principles that any worldview or policy must be compatible with to function in a free society.

{0.1.5|idea:five_principles_preview} The Five Foundational Principles.
```
