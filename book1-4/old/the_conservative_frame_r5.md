Two people can argue about a proposed law for an hour and never once touch their actual disagreement. The law is a conclusion. Conclusions rest on premises, and when premises differ, arguing over conclusions is talking past each other at best. To be productive together in society, we must first ensure our premises are compatible.

I believe the core premises of society can be drawn out by five questions:

* Is there truth, and can we know it?
* Are humans selfish and limited, or good and unlimited in nature?
* Should human life continue, and should it thrive?
* What should be prioritized?
* What is justice in society?

Labels will not tell you how a person answers them. The United Methodist Church answers differently than the Southern Baptists; the modern Jesuit differently than the Eastern Orthodox; Republicans differently than Democrats; and atheists differ among themselves as much as anyone. Questions of God and ultimate meaning matter, but for the task of living together, these five questions reign supreme.

There is a distinct set of conservative principles that answers all five. With these principles, conservatives can find common ground across disagreements about theism, atheism, and other fundamental beliefs. The same principles expose divides hidden under shared labels: two Christians who part ways over them are radically different people, and one is likely a wolf in sheep's clothing.

## The 5 Principles

* Reality is real, knowable, and consequential. Truth is what corresponds to reality.
* Humans are limited and self-interested.
* Goodness must include human life continuing and human life thriving.
* Act with Self-Anchored Tiered Priority.
* Respond with Procedural Symmetry.

The five are a single piece. Remove one, or distort one, and the rest stop describing the same whole; together they are distinct from, and greater than, their parts. They fall naturally into three groups: how we relate to reality (Reality is Real, Humans are Limited), how we relate to ourselves (Goodness), and how we relate to others (Tiered Priority, Procedural Symmetry).

Stated explicitly, the principles give conservatives a common language and a common measuring stick. Ideologies can be compared against them, and parasitic ideologies recognized before they take root, or identified and removed where they already have. Parasites do not announce themselves; they arrive commingled with healthier ideas, which is why the principles must be applied inside our own churches, our own atheist communities, and our own universities.

The principles also point forward. Applied to society, they direct true progress: not what passes for social progressivism today. This conservatism is not rooted in the past. It is rooted in principles grounded in reality, and what we conserve, we conserve because it is grounded in reality, in life, and in proper relationships to each other.

## Relationship to Reality

Start with the first premise. Reality is real: not a shadow on a wall, but the substance of us and of everything around us. Reality is consequential: one pool ball strikes another and momentum is conserved, minus friction, no matter what anyone wishes. And reality is knowable, because our senses are generally self-consistent. Self-consistency is what makes knowledge possible. My eyes may interpret blue differently than yours; so long as each of our senses distinguishes the differences that are actually there, and does so consistently, each of us can build an accurate model of the world. LLMs (what we call AI today) prove the point from the opposite direction: they take in pixels through a pipeline nothing like human sight, yet with consistent training and resolving residuals they can read a picture and describe it much as a human would. Knowledge does not require identical sensors. It requires consistent ones.

From a knowable reality follows a definition: truth is what corresponds to reality. Not every belief system accepts this, and the refusals are worth naming. Scripturalism, as articulated by Crampton, rejects correspondence outright: "A Christian epistemology maintains that a proposition is true because God thinks it to be true." Ash'arite occasionalism, still common in Sunni Islam in various forms, holds that God (Allah) is the sole and direct cause of every event in the universe; the key word is direct, so that when fire is exposed to cotton, it is God who burns the cotton, not the fire. Postmodernism (Foucault, Derrida) arrives at the same refusal from the secular side, declaring all reality, knowledge, and truth to be social constructions. Three very different traditions, one common move: each severs truth from reality, and each is for that reason incompatible with these 5 principles.

Reason deserves a caution here. Reason is our best tool for working with truth, but you cannot reason your way to reality. Reason that is not grounded in reality is little more than hallucination or imagination. Let reason guide your ideas; let reality keep the final say on truth.

The second premise turns the same honesty inward. We are limited: limited time, limited understanding, limited reach. And we are biologically selfish. Not selfish in the sense that every act is calculated for personal benefit (genuine altruistic impulses are real), but selfish in the sense that self-interest belongs to our nature. Some ideas hold the opposite: that people ought not to be self-interested, and that the self-interest we observe is merely social conditioning to be trained away. Human development and human history tell a different story. Self-interest is protective and fundamental. Work with it, not around it.

Put the two premises together, a demanding reality and a limited human, and specialization follows. The work of bringing order to the world, and of understanding it, exceeds any one person's time and understanding. The farmer who tills, plants, and harvests all day has nothing left for learning to sail and fish. Specialization in turn produces technologies that no one designed, which forces a distinction between discovered and constructed technology. Money is the clear case. Every form of money is constructed: ledger entries, paper, gold, shells. But money itself, a streamlined barter arising wherever people specialize, is discovered. It is what human limits produce on contact with a complex and demanding world. Language shows the same pattern. Each language is constructed and drifts over time, yet language itself, common and limited meaning referring to reality, is discovered and emergent, because cooperation under specialization demands communication. Language does not create reality; it refers to it.

## Relationship to Self

We can describe what is. The harder question is what we should bring about, and the answer to that question is what we call Good. Accounts of the Good vary, but any account compatible with these 5 principles must include human life continuing, and continuing well. A good that ends with us is no good at all. The Christian Bible gives this command twice in the book of beginnings, to Adam and again to Noah: go into the world and multiply. It is not the only Good in Christian belief, but it must be a good Christians take seriously, and an atheist can arrive at the same place by another route and value human life and human thriving.

Notice what this is not: it is not tolerance. Tolerance is not a virtue in any meaningful sense, because you quickly find people who wish you harm, directly or indirectly, and a tolerance that must tolerate them defeats itself. This is sometimes called the paradox of tolerance, and the resolution is plain: tolerance was never a virtue and cannot be one. Actual virtue is a positive vision of what you believe, with clear boundaries marking what is, and what is not, compatible with it. Draw those boundaries with our limits in mind. Seeking truth requires the ability to change our minds about what reality is, so it is in everyone's interest to set the compatibility boundaries wide. Wide boundaries do not mean truth itself is wide, or that there are many realities; absolutely not. The virtue here is humility, knowing our own limits. It was never tolerance.

If life is to continue and continue well, each of us must align with truth and pursue excellence. Excellence is what elevates our craftsmen, our stone workers, our metal workers, our machinists, our factories, and the factories that make factories. Thriving is not an abstraction; it is a recognition of reality that demands excellence in its pursuit.

And thriving is not cold. We must admire and create beauty. Beauty is the conjunction of order, truth, and vitality. A vase is just a vase; but a worn vase, once broken and pieced back together with seams of gold, shows vitality, and a vase of fresh flowers joins order to the vitality of living things. The part of beauty that is subjective is the vitality we each perceive in a thing. In that sense beauty is subjective, and still it is not without bounds.

Creation carries an obligation past the moment of creating. Form a brick from dust and water and you have made new order; that order persists only if the same care continues, the brick cleaned, repaired, kept dry. The ongoing work of keeping order corresponds to the work that created the order, and this correspondence is the substance of ownership and responsibility. Ownership has a social face: whose is it, who may use it, who holds the deed. But a deed without maintenance is ownership in name only. The active face of ownership is responsibility, the act of maintaining that mirrors the act of creating. You cannot create without care and order, and you cannot fully own without continuing them.

The same responsibility reaches our own continuation. Our parents had children; their parents had children; goodness includes having children. We are not atomic individuals, and the life our parents handed us carries an obligation forward: have children, or where that is not physically possible, raise up children. Raise them without trying to control them. Guide and teach, but allow failure when they insist on it; show them consequence, natural or imposed, and let them fail. Some may fail; others will learn and thrive. Have more than one child, and do not hold any of them too tightly.

In the end, the only actions we control are our own. We may bemoan society or politics; the work still begins with ourselves.

## Relationship to Others

We govern ourselves, but we live among others, and the first thing we owe them is dispositional love: a general good will toward the people around us, unless other factors intervene. Dispositional love commits us to no particular act; it is a posture. Action-based love is the second kind, love spent in time, attention, and effort, and it immediately runs into a problem: humans are limited. There is only so much time and energy to spend. Self-Anchored Tiered Priority is how we spend it.

The tiers run outward from the anchor. First care for yourself, your spouse, and your family. Then your neighbors and your community. Then the strangers of your state and country. Last, and still real, the world and beyond. The tiers also account for a fact that softer pictures of society omit: some people actively wish us harm. Enemies are real. Some will come to assault you physically; others work patiently against your interests, seeking to unmake the society you live in and remake it in an image they prefer.

Action-based love follows the tiers. Act lovingly first toward yourself, your spouse, and your family; then toward your neighbors with a lesser but real priority. The test is simple: if your caring actions toward a friend exceed those toward your spouse, stop and rethink how you embody your relationships.

Forgiveness divides along similar lines into two types. Personal release lets go of a hurt, or simply sets it aside, so that you can move on; it asks nothing of the one who wronged you. Social reconciliation asks much: the person who did the wrong must genuinely recognize that it was wrong and commit to avoiding it in the future. Broadly, love is holding on and forgiveness is letting go, and both have their place in long personal and social relationships.

But society is more than personal relationships. Past a limited circle of people (a limit sometimes called Dunbar's Number), society is made of strangers, and strangers need a different instrument. That instrument is Procedural Symmetry: within a given tier, the same procedures offered to every person in it. In a country, every citizen of the citizen tier is bound by the same laws. We act toward others by the same procedures we would want used toward us.

Two rival symmetries make the contrast sharp. Value Symmetry returns acts in kind: if someone chops off your hand, you chop off theirs; if someone hurts your child, you hurt their child. Some Islamic cultures still practice this type of symmetry. Outcome Symmetry, far more common and far more praised today, desires the same outcome for every person and reads any differing outcome as proof of some deeper injustice. It ignores that people differ, that situations differ, and that choices differ; it creates perverse incentives; it fails to protect the innocent from their aggressors; and it breeds corruption.

Procedural symmetry also forces honesty about power, because power is real. An HOA president holds power that can improve lives or burden them. A parent holds real power over a child. I have heard liberalism described as the practice of balancing a bat, where no one willingly picks up the bat of power and uses it to hit people. This is a foolish and dangerous idea. Power is real, and power is necessary. A teacher presides over students precisely in order to teach them what the teacher knows, and teacher and student do not hold equal power; nothing is wrong in that. Procedural symmetry pairs with Tiered Priority: the teacher owes every student the same procedures, while teacher and student stand in different tiers. Parents and children follow the same pattern. A child should have a voice, but that voice does not carry a parent's power; the two stand in different tiers, and parents rightly hold power over their children. That power, bounded by the same procedures for each child, is what the protection and care of children are made of.

## Encouragement

Memorize the five: reality is real, humans are limited, goodness is life and thriving, tiered priority, procedural symmetry. When an idea reaches you, break it down along these axes and see whether it lands fully inside them. Guard yourself, your community, your church, and your ideas with care; the principles are an anchor. Do not let yourself be subverted, or made useful to parasitic individuals and parasitic ideas.

Live well.
