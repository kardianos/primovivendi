To live and work productively together in society, we must share compatible premises about the world. We can argue over specific conclusions (whether to enact this law or adopt that policy), but if our underlying premises differ, we will end up just talking past each other at best.

I believe these questions are some of the core premises of society:

* Is there truth, and can we know it?
* Are humans limited and self-interested, or are they fundamentally good and perfectible through social change?
* Should human life continue, and should it thrive?
* What deserves priority in our obligations?
* What does justice require in society: equal procedures, equal outcomes, or equal value returned?

Different churches, political parties, families, and friend groups give different answers to these questions. The United Methodist Church and the Southern Baptist Convention often diverge here. A modern Jesuit and an Eastern Orthodox Christian may part ways on these premises even while sharing a creed. Republicans and Democrats are divided by them. Atheists are not uniform either. Questions of God and ultimate origins matter, but these five questions reign supreme for the practical task of living together without constant conflict or collapse.

A distinct set of conservative principles answers these questions coherently. These principles allow conservatives of different theological commitments (or none at all) to find common ground. They also expose sharp divides within groups that appear united on the surface: two Christians who split over these principles are not truly aligned, no matter the shared label. One is likely operating from a different frame entirely.

## The Five Principles

The Conservative Frame consists of five interlocking principles:

1. **Reality is real, knowable, and consequential.** Truth is what corresponds to reality.
2. **Humans are limited and self-interested.** These features are protective and fundamental, not defects to be engineered away.
3. **Goodness requires that human life continue and that humans thrive.** What cannot survive cannot be good in the future.
4. **Priority is self-anchored and tiered.** Care and obligation begin with the self and immediate family, then extend to neighbors and community, then nation, then world.
5. **Justice is procedural symmetry, scoped to tiers.** Within each relevant scope or role, the same procedures and rules apply to everyone. This is neither outcome symmetry (equity) nor value symmetry (retaliation in kind).

These five must be held together as a single bundle. Removing or distorting any one principle distorts the others and collapses the framework. They naturally cluster into three relationships: our relationship to reality (principles 1 and 2), our relationship to ourselves (principle 3), and our relationship to others (principles 4 and 5). Each relationship narrows the possibilities available to the next. Treating them as separable produces something coherent but no longer this frame.

By making these principles explicit, we can identify and resist parasitic ideologies that attach themselves to healthier traditions, whether in churches, universities, political movements, or online communities. These principles also point toward genuine progress: not the fashionable "social progress" of the moment, but the kind of ordered improvement that sustains human life and flourishing across generations. This is not a conservatism of nostalgia for the past, but a conservatism rooted in principles that correspond to reality itself.

## Relating to Reality

We begin by acknowledging that reality is real. It is not a shadow, a social construct, or a projection of our minds. It is the ground of our existence and everything we encounter. Reality is consequential: when one billiard ball strikes another, momentum is conserved (minus friction and other forces). Reality is also knowable. Our senses (though imperfect and varying between individuals) provide self-consistent information about the world. Self-consistency is the key: my eyes may register "blue" differently from yours, but if both of us can reliably distinguish blue from red across many observations, we can each construct accurate models of the world.

Large language models illustrate the point from the opposite direction. Their "sensory" input is pixel values processed through entirely different architectures than human vision. Yet when trained consistently, they can describe the content of an image in ways that align with human understanding. What matters is not identical hardware, but consistent mapping from input to a coherent model of reality.

Truth, on this account, is correspondence to reality. Some belief systems reject this explicitly. Scripturalism, as articulated by Gordon Clark and others, holds that a proposition is true because God thinks it, not because it matches any external state of affairs. Ash'arite occasionalism, influential in parts of Sunni Islam, teaches that God is the direct and sole cause of every event; when fire touches cotton, God creates the burning, not any natural causal power in the fire itself. Postmodern thought, following Foucault and Derrida, treats reality, knowledge, and truth as products of power and social construction. Each of these severs truth from correspondence to an independent reality. Once that link is broken, the remaining principles cannot function: there is no stable ground on which to judge whether a policy produces thriving, whether a procedure is fair, or whether a priority is realistic.

Reason alone is not sufficient. Reason unmoored from observation of reality quickly becomes sophisticated hallucination. Reason is a powerful tool for generating hypotheses and spotting inconsistencies, but reality retains the final veto.

When we turn inward, we confront our own limitations. We have finite time, finite attention, finite cognitive capacity, and finite reach. As biological organisms shaped by evolution, we are self-interested by nature. This does not mean every action is coldly calculated for personal gain. It means that self-interest is the default orientation: protective, not pathological. We are not blank slates waiting to be made altruistic through education or social pressure. History and everyday experience confirm this: attempts to engineer self-interest out of human beings have repeatedly produced famine, terror, and economic collapse. Self-interest is a feature to be channeled through good institutions, not a bug to be eradicated.

Human limits also explain why certain social technologies are discovered rather than arbitrarily constructed. Money, for example, emerges wherever people specialize and trade. A farmer has little time to learn deep-sea fishing; a fisherman has little time to grow wheat. Specialization increases productivity but requires a medium of exchange. The specific form (cowrie shells, gold, paper notes, or digital ledger entries) is constructed and can vary. The *need* for some form of money, however, is discovered through the constraints of reality and human limitation. The same is true of language. Each particular language is a human construction that changes over time. The existence of language itself (systems of shared, limited meanings that enable cooperation) is a discovered necessity. Without it, complex specialization collapses.

These distinctions matter. Ideologies that treat money, language, markets, or law as pure social constructs that can be redesigned at will by an enlightened elite will repeatedly run aground on the discovered necessities that human limits impose.

## Relating to Self

We can describe what is. The harder question is what we should bring about. This is the domain of the good.

For any account of the good to be compatible with the five principles, it must include the continuation of human life and the conditions for humans to live well. In the biblical book of Genesis, the command to "be fruitful and multiply" is given both to Adam and to Noah after the flood. This is not the only good in the Christian tradition, but it is one that any version of Christianity compatible with this frame must take seriously. An atheist can reach the same conclusion without theology: if humans do not continue, there is no one left to value anything. If humans continue but live in misery, sickness, and ignorance, the continuation is thin. Goodness therefore requires both survival and flourishing.

This is not a call for tolerance as a supreme virtue. Tolerance, understood as refusing to draw any boundaries, quickly becomes self-destructive. There are people and ideas that wish us harm, directly or by undermining the conditions that make our lives possible. The so-called paradox of tolerance is resolved by recognizing that tolerance is not a virtue at all. Virtue requires a positive vision of what is good and clear, defensible boundaries around what is compatible with that vision. Humility about our own limits keeps those boundaries from becoming tyrannical, but the boundaries themselves are necessary.

For human life to continue and thrive, individuals must cultivate excellence in their own spheres. The craftsman who takes pride in precise work, the machinist who maintains tight tolerances, the factory that produces reliable components, and the factory that builds the machines that build other factories are not optional decorations on survival. They are the accumulated result of aligning human effort with reality's demands. Thriving is demanding; it requires that we become better than we were.

Thriving is not merely cold and efficient. We must also create and appreciate beauty. Beauty arises at the intersection of order, truth, and vitality. A plain vase has order and perhaps truth (it holds water), but little vitality. A vase repaired with gold seams in the Japanese kintsugi tradition shows the history of breakage and restoration; the repair itself becomes part of its life. A vase holding fresh flowers joins human order with the vitality of living plants. Beauty is not boundless or purely subjective. It requires structure that corresponds to reality and carries some resonance of life.

When we shape clay and fire it into a brick, we impose order on raw material. That same order and care must be maintained if the brick is to remain useful: it must be kept dry, protected from roots, repaired when damaged. The ongoing work of maintenance corresponds to the original work of creation. This correspondence is what we call responsibility. Ownership has a legal and social dimension (who holds the deed), but responsibility is the active dimension: the one who created or inherited the thing continues to order and preserve it. Without responsibility, ownership decays into decay. A society full of legal owners who feel no duty to maintain what they own will watch its capital crumble.

The same logic applies to the continuation of humanity itself. Each of us exists because previous generations chose to have and raise children. The responsibility to have children, where physically possible, or to help raise the next generation, is not an optional lifestyle preference. It is the mechanism by which life and the goods it makes possible are passed forward. When raising children, the goal is not control but formation. Guide them, teach them, set boundaries, and then loosen the reins. Allow them to fail in small ways so they can succeed in large ones. Have more than one child. Do not cling so tightly that they cannot become independent adults.

Only our own actions are fully under our control. Political frustration and cultural dismay are easy. Personal responsibility begins at home, in our habits, our work, our families, and our communities.

## Relating to Others

We control ourselves, but we must still live among others. The first requirement is a general dispositional goodwill toward those around us (a default posture of not wishing harm and being willing to extend basic courtesy). This costs little and scales to large numbers. It is the right background assumption for strangers.

A second kind of love is action-based: giving time, attention, resources, and sacrifice. This is costly and does not scale. Human beings have limited time and energy. This is where self-anchored tiered priority becomes essential.

Tiered priority means that our strongest obligations are to ourselves and our immediate family, then to neighbors and local community, then to the broader nation, and only then to the world at large. This ordering is not arbitrary. It reflects real human limits (Dunbar's number suggests we can maintain roughly 150 meaningful social relationships), biological realities (parents are wired to care for their own offspring first), and practical effectiveness (we can actually help those we know well far more than distant strangers). The common modern habit of treating the suffering of someone on the other side of the planet as morally more urgent than the needs of one's own children or neighbors is not a higher ethic; it is an inverted frame that ignores how humans actually function and how goods actually propagate.

Enemies are real. Some will attempt direct physical harm. Others will work methodically to undermine the institutions, borders, and cultural patterns that make our tiered obligations possible, intending to replace them with something they consider better. Recognizing enemies does not require hatred; it requires clear-eyed refusal to grant them access to the inner tiers of obligation they have not earned and do not deserve.

Action-based love and care therefore follow the tiers. You owe your spouse and children more than you owe a friend. If your actions toward a friend exceed your actions toward your spouse, the relationship priorities have become disordered. The same principle applies at every scale.

Forgiveness also comes in two forms. Personal forgiveness is the choice to release resentment and move forward for your own sake; it does not require the other person's participation. Social or relational reconciliation requires the wrongdoer to acknowledge the wrong, accept accountability, and change behavior. The two are often confused, with the demand for the second disguised as a requirement of the first.

Beyond the small circle of people we know personally (Dunbar's number again), society consists largely of strangers. The tool that allows us to live well with strangers is procedural symmetry.

Procedural symmetry means that, within a given scope or role, the same rules, procedures, and standards apply to everyone. In a polity, all citizens are subject to the same criminal law and receive the same procedural protections. A teacher applies the same grading standards and classroom rules to every student. A parent owes each of their children the same duty of care, even though the children differ in temperament and need. This is the institutional form of the Golden Rule: we treat others under the same procedures we would accept for ourselves in that role.

Procedural symmetry must be contrasted with two common alternatives that fail:

Value symmetry (or retributive symmetry) demands that equal harms be returned in kind: an eye for an eye. Some traditional Islamic legal systems retain elements of this. It works in very small, stable groups but escalates violence and cannot handle complex modern societies where harms are not easily measured or reciprocated.

Outcome symmetry (equity) demands that everyone end up in the same condition regardless of effort, talent, choices, or circumstances. Any disparity is treated as proof of injustice requiring correction. This approach ignores human variation, punishes success, subsidizes failure, creates powerful incentives for cheating and rent-seeking, and requires a large coercive apparatus to enforce. A new ruling class inevitably emerges to administer the redistribution, and that class is accountable to no one. Mobility dies. The innocent are not protected; they are sacrificed to the abstraction of equal results.

Procedural symmetry, by contrast, accepts that different people will produce different outcomes when given the same fair process. It protects the innocent by punishing actual violations rather than engineering statistical parity. It scales because it does not require perfect knowledge of every person's situation.

Procedural symmetry is always scoped. A parent has authority over their own children that they do not have over a neighbor's children. That asymmetry is legitimate because it is tied to responsibility. The parent must still treat each of their own children with the same procedural fairness (no arbitrary favoritism). A government has powers over citizens that it does not have over non-citizens; citizens have rights and obligations that non-citizens do not. This is not bigotry. It is the recognition that the scope of a polity is finite and that the duties of citizenship are reciprocal.

Power is real. An HOA board member can approve or deny improvements. A teacher can pass or fail a student. A police officer can arrest. A parent can set curfews. The fashionable claim that power itself is illegitimate (that no one should ever "pick up the bat") is both false and dangerous. Power is necessary for any organized human activity. The question is whether power is legitimate. Power is legitimate when it serves a genuine tiered responsibility and is constrained by procedural symmetry within its proper scope. Parents have power children lack, but only to fulfill the duty of raising those specific children. Teachers have authority students lack, but only to educate according to consistent standards. When power escapes these constraints (when it serves no one's tiered duty or applies different rules to different people), it becomes simple domination.

## Guarding the Frame

Memorize the five principles. When you encounter an idea, a policy, a sermon, or a political movement, test it against all five. Does it treat reality as real and truth as correspondence? Does it account for human limits and self-interest rather than assuming perfectibility? Does it treat the continuation and flourishing of human life as non-negotiable goods? Does it respect self-anchored tiers of obligation? Does it apply procedures symmetrically within the relevant scopes, or does it demand equal outcomes or equal retaliation?

Ideas that fail one or more of these tests are not merely mistaken on details. They operate from a different and incompatible frame. Such ideas often travel as parasites inside traditions that once held the conservative frame (churches, universities, families, nations). They borrow the language of compassion, justice, and responsibility while inverting the substance. The only reliable defense is to hold the five principles explicitly and to notice when any one of them is being softened, redefined, or dropped.

Live according to what you can control. Build excellence in your own life and work. Maintain what you own. Raise the next generation. Extend goodwill to strangers and real care according to your actual tiers. Insist on the same rules for others that you accept for yourself. These are not grand political programs. They are the daily disciplines that allow a people to remain capable of self-government and genuine flourishing.

The Conservative Frame is not a complete theology, nor a full moral system, nor a political platform. It is the minimal set of premises that allows human beings who acknowledge reality and their own limits to live together without devouring their future. Guard it.

Live well.
