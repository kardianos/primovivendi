### Extracted Points (Supported by Conversation)

1. **Blake as Exemplar of Incompatible Visionary Anti-Realism**  
   Blake rejects empirical reality/truth (correspondence to observable world) in favor of visionary imagination as the true "reality" and "human existence itself." He sees reason, law, and procedural order as "mind-forg'd manacles" and celebrates contraries/energy over restraint. This directly violates Principles 1 (Reality is real, knowable, consequential) and 5 (Procedural Symmetry), while clashing with Principle 2 (humans as limited/self-interested) by positing infinite potential through imagination. His initial revolutionary politics and anti-authority stance make him a clear case of the "parasitic ideologies" warned about — one that subverts churches, traditions, and conservative order from within by redefining truth and goodness around subjective vision rather than survival/thriving in reality.

2. **Sartre as Exemplar of Revolutionary Anti-Essentialism**  
   Sartre's "existence precedes essence" + radical freedom + "bad faith" (denying freedom by pretending fixed nature/roles/limits) directly attacks Principle 2 (humans limited/self-interested) and the entire conservative project of conserving functional order. His later existential Marxism (*Critique of Dialectical Reason*) demands revolutionary transcendence of "serial" alienated structures (bourgeois law, tiered institutions, procedural symmetry) rather than working within them. Partial overlap exists on personal responsibility/ownership of choices (aligning with "start with yourself" in tiered priority), but the revolutionary core — willing the freedom of all by overcoming existing "mechanics" — rejects scoped symmetry, tiered priority, and the incremental "what works" empiricism of the Frame. Late Sartre illustrates why revolutionary ideologies are incompatible: they treat proven social patterns as oppressive essence to be broken, not as discovered technology to be conserved and improved.

3. **The User's Pragmatic Incrementalism as Practical Embodiment of the Frame**  
   Your stance ("Prove to me they work in small ways, then greater ways... Outcomes are more important than feelings... Experiments should be bounded and evaluated") is not a compromise but a direct application of the Frame's epistemology (Principle 1 + "Outcomes more important than feelings" + "Strength, Not weakness"). It embodies "Discovered vs. Constructed" (test what emerges and functions) and Scoped Symmetry (test within bounded tiers/roles before scaling). This stands in explicit contrast to Blake/Sartre-style leaps: no "doors of perception" mysticism or "existence precedes essence" rupture — just reality-constrained, survival-oriented iteration. This is why the Frame has "no revolution in a single fiber": it conserves the core vectors that have repeatedly worked (kin selection, Dunbar's number, ownership/responsibility, procedural symmetry within scopes) while allowing tested evolution.

4. **"Bad Faith" and Revolutionary Temptation as Diagnostic Tools**  
   Sartre's concept of bad faith usefully diagnoses resistance to the Frame: people deny human limits, tiered reality, and the consequential nature of survival by pretending existing structures are arbitrary oppression rather than functional adaptations. Blake's "mind-forg'd manacles" does similar work from the visionary side. Both illustrate the "Compassionate Delusion" and "Strategic Adversaries weaponize your own morals" sections — they weaponize compassion/freedom/imagination to erode tiered priority and procedural symmetry from within. The Frame's response: these are not neutral critiques but parasitic redefinitions that fail the survival filter.
