>  1. The Paradox of "Systems" vs. "Irrationality"
> You argue that humans are inherently limited and irrational, and that **Systems** (government, culture, norms) are what allow us to behave rationally.
> *   **The Pushback:** If the "bricks" (humans) are irrational, how can the "building" (the system) be rational? If you don't believe in "Philosopher Kings" (your point: "Don't try to make people philosophers"), who is the architect of these systems?
> *   **The Risk:** You risk a "circular dependency." If the system is what makes us rational, but we are too irrational to design the system, you need a theory of **Evolutionary Selection of Systems.** You should argue that we didn't "reason" our way into these systems; rather, the societies that accidentally stumbled upon these systems survived, and those that didn't, died. (This aligns with your "Discovered Technology" point).

DT: That's a great question, one I should be very careful with when writing. It isn't that people are always irrational, it is that a society should not be made for always rational citizens. It isn't that there aren't philosophers in society, we shouldn't require for a society to function for all/most citizens to become philosophers. We create society through specialization: the bricklayer lays the brick for a building, the roofer installs the roof, the well known wise truth teller builds public policies. This is the problem with democracy and the strength of republic.

> ### 2. Adversary vs. Enemy
> Your definition of "Enemy" is clear: people who defraud, invade, or seek the death of the state. 
> *   **The Pushback:** You need a category for the **"Domestic Adversary."** If everyone who "transparently lies" is an enemy, and enemies have "negative value," a society could quickly spiral into internal purges. In a world of "limited, self-interested" humans, *everyone* lies or misrepresents the truth occasionally to favor their "Tiered Priority" (family/self).
> *   **The Challenge:** How do you distinguish between an "Enemy" (to be excluded/neutralized) and a "Flawed Citizen" (to be corrected via Procedural Symmetry)? Without this distinction, your "Exclusion" principle might become a tool for total polarization.

DT: I think the problem is with your framing. An enemy isn't necessarily "evil" or even "bad". An enemy is someone who puts themselves against my and my own. Against my body, my family, my nation. If that person is also under the law and has the capacity and time to deal with it, the law must handle the enemy, that is defining procedural symmetry. If the law does not have time (a home intruder and there is no time for even first responders) or refuses to handle it, then we must decide what to do, if anything. You prevent it from spiraling by ensuring the rule of law is realistic and covers real enemies and first responders are available and reasonably quick.

> ### 4. The "Kindness vs. Politeness" Trap
> Your take on kindness is perhaps the most controversial and potentially "brittle" part of the book. You argue kindness should be reserved for the inner tier (family) and politeness for the ouBeutatyter (society).
> *   **The Pushback:** This ignores the **"Low-Trust Society"** trap. If I am only "polite" (formal/procedural) to my neighbor but never "kind" (acting beyond what is required), the social fabric becomes brittle. "Kindness" to strangers is often the "oil" that prevents the "gears" of the law from grinding to a halt. 
> *   **The Game Theory counter:** "Tit-for-Tat with Forgiveness" is a more successful game theory strategy than "Pure Tit-for-Tat." If you replace all social kindness with mere politeness, you remove the "buffer" that allows for human error. You might end up with a society that is "perfectly procedural" but emotionally dead, leading to the "Nihilism" you want to avoid.

DT: This works into social take on forgiveness and love. In family, love is action based and forgiveness is personal release. In society love is dispositional and forgiveness social reconciliation. I'm thinking you are confusing things, but it is also the key. We DO want a high trust society. This is where actions shine: a high trust society is one where actions of others can be trusted, a pile of money can be left out in the open without being stolen, art can be put up without defacement.
