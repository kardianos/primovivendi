Brainstorm: Tiered Responsibility, Procedural Symmetry, and Power

---

## THE PROBLEM

You've identified a real tension. Procedural symmetry says "equal procedures for all." But:

- Parents have power over children. Parents don't play favorites (symmetry), but the parent-child relationship is fundamentally asymmetric in power.
- Citizens are under equal law (symmetry), but citizens have rights non-citizens don't. The law treats them differently.
- A judge has authority over a defendant. All judges owe all defendants the same procedures. But the judge-defendant power dynamic is asymmetric.
- You prioritize your family over strangers (tiered). But you're supposed to treat your children equally (symmetry).

So the question: how can procedural symmetry be "equal procedures for all" when power is distributed along tiered lines and is inherently unequal?

The answer: **procedural symmetry is scoped. It operates within a tier and within a role, not universally across all tiers and roles.**

---

## THE KEY INSIGHT: SCOPED SYMMETRY

Procedural symmetry does NOT say "everyone gets the same treatment regardless of relationship, role, or category."

It says: **within a defined scope — a tier, a role, a jurisdiction — the same rules apply to everyone in that scope.**

This is not a weakening of procedural symmetry. It is what makes it work. Without scoping, procedural symmetry becomes universalist absurdity: you'd owe a stranger in another country the same concern as your own child. That's not symmetry; that's the erasure of tiered responsibility.

### Examples of Scoped Symmetry:

**Family tier:**
- Symmetry: all your children get equal parental treatment. No favorites.
- Asymmetry across tiers: you owe your children more than your neighbor's children. This is not "unequal treatment" — it's correct tiered priority.
- Power: you have authority over your children. They don't have equal power. But you're bounded by symmetric obligation to each child.

**Citizen tier:**
- Symmetry: all citizens are subject to the same criminal law, the same procedural rights.
- Asymmetry across tiers: citizens get citizen treatment (safety nets, voting, legal standing). Non-citizens don't. This is borders doing their job.
- Power: government has power over citizens. But that power is bounded by the same procedural rules that protect every citizen equally. The president doesn't get a different criminal code.

**Role-scoped:**
- Symmetry: all judges owe all defendants the same due process. All teachers owe all students the same grading standards.
- Asymmetry across roles: a judge has power a defendant doesn't. A teacher has authority a student doesn't.
- The power is legitimate because the role is defined, the procedures are symmetric within the role, and the role itself serves tiered responsibility (the judge protects the community tier from individual predators).

**Between nations:**
- Symmetry: doesn't apply across borders in the same way. International law isn't real law. Military might is the ultimate arbiter.
- This isn't a failure of symmetry — it's the recognition that the "scope" of procedural symmetry is the polity, not the planet.

## HOW TIERED RESPONSIBILITY AND PROCEDURAL SYMMETRY INTERACT

Think of it as two axes:

```
            PROCEDURAL SYMMETRY
            (within scope)
                 ^
                 |
     +-----------+-----------+
     |           |           |
     |  FAMILY   | COMMUNITY |   TIERED
     |  scope:   | scope:    |   RESPONSIBILITY
     |  equal    | equal     |   (across scopes)
     |  treatment| treatment |
     |  of all   | of all   |   priority
     |  children | citizens  |   ordering
     |           |           |
     +-----------+-----------+
     |           |           |
     |  SELF     |  NATION   |
     |  scope:   | scope:    |
     |  internal | equal law |
     |  coherence| for all   |
     |           | citizens  |
     |           |           |
     +-----------+-----------+
                 |
                 v
```

Tiered responsibility defines the SCOPE (the concentric circles: self, family, community, nation). Procedural symmetry operates WITHIN each scope. The two principles together create a system where:

1. You know WHO you owe what to (tiered responsibility)
2. Within that group, you know the rules are fair (procedural symmetry)
3. Power operates within the scope and is bounded by symmetry within that scope

Remove either:
- Tiered responsibility without symmetry = tribal favoritism. You favor your family but play favorites within it. You favor your nation but apply law unequally to different citizens.
- Symmetry without tiered responsibility = universalism. You treat everyone identically regardless of relationship. Borders dissolve. Parents owe strangers the same as their children.

## THE PARENT-CHILD EXAMPLE, FULLY WORKED

This is the clearest case and worth getting right.

A parent has:
- **Tiered priority**: children come before strangers. The child's well-being is a higher-weighted concern.
- **Power over children**: the parent makes decisions the child cannot. This is legitimate because the child is within the parent's tiered responsibility and the child lacks the capacity for full self-anchoring.
- **Procedural symmetry WITHIN the family**: the parent owes each child equal treatment. Not identical treatment (children differ), but symmetric procedural treatment — equal concern, equal access to the parent's care and judgment, no arbitrary favoritism.
- **Procedural symmetry WITHIN the parent's role**: all parents owe all their children the same standard of care. A parent doesn't get to arbitrarily neglect one child any more than a judge gets to arbitrarily deny one defendant due process.

The power asymmetry (parent > child) is:
- Justified by tiered responsibility (the parent is responsible FOR the child)
- Bounded by procedural symmetry (the parent must treat all children equally)
- Temporary (children grow up — power transfers as capacity develops)
- Accountable (the community/nation tier can intervene if the parent violates the symmetric obligation of care)

This is the pattern for ALL legitimate power:
**Power is legitimate when it serves tiered responsibility AND is bounded by procedural symmetry within its scope.**

## RELATIONSHIP TO THE OTHER PRINCIPLES

This interaction isn't just between principles 4 and 5. It connects to all five:

**Reality & Truth (1):** Humans really are limited and capacity really is distributed unequally. Children really do need parental authority. Some people really are more competent at certain roles. Denying this is reality denial.

**Human Limits (2):** Because humans are limited, power must be scoped. No one person can exercise symmetric concern across all of humanity. The scope must narrow to what a limited being can actually handle. This is why Dunbar's number and kin selection matter — they describe the real boundaries of human concern.

**Goodness/Survival (3):** The whole point of scoped symmetry is to enable survival and flourishing. A society that tries to extend full procedural symmetry to everyone on earth collapses. A family that extends equal concern to strangers' children neglects its own. Survival requires scoping.

**The Interlock:** This is a concrete case of why the principles must be taken together. Principle 4 without principle 5 is tribalism. Principle 5 without principle 4 is universalism. Together, they produce legitimate, bounded, fair order.

- Clean. Two words.
- Loses the concentric-circle signal.

**Concentric Priority**
- Explicitly captures the radiating structure.
- Sounds geometric/academic.

**Radial Responsibility**
- "Radial" = radiating from center outward.
- Precise but unusual. Readers won't immediately grasp it.

**Tiered Priority** (simplified)
- The simplest version. What toc.md uses.
- "Self-anchored" can be explained in the body text rather than the name.

- "Ordered" ties into "ordered liberty" — the framework's subtitle.

- "Scoped" directly signals the interaction with procedural symmetry: priority defines the SCOPE of concern.
- Novel word choice that would need explanation, but once explained, is very precise.

**Bounded Priority**

- Ties to "ordered liberty" theme.

2. "Priority" is more accurate than "responsibility" for this principle. The principle answers the question "what should be prioritized?" — the fourth of your five dividing questions. 3. "Self-anchored" is explanatory material, not name material. It belongs in the body text: "Priority begins with the self and radiates outward. You are the anchor point. If you are not secured, you cannot secure anyone else."
4. The scoping function (how it interacts with procedural symmetry) should be explained in the Interlock section, not crammed into the name.

The full principle entry would then be:

> **Tiered Priority**
> Primary concern radiates outward from self through family, community, and nation. You cannot secure what you have not first anchored. Enemies are real and carry negative weight. The tiers define the SCOPE within which procedural symmetry operates: equal treatment within a tier, differentiated treatment across tiers. Kin selection and Dunbar's number describe the natural boundaries.

## THE KEY PHRASE TO INTRODUCE

The concept that ties tiered priority to procedural symmetry needs a compact expression. Options:

**"Symmetry within scope"**
- Clean. Precise. "Scope" is set by tiered priority. "Symmetry" operates within it.

**"Equal rules within, different rules across"**
- More colloquial. Clear.

**"Fairness within tiers, priority across them"**
- Descriptive but long.

**"Scoped symmetry"**
- Shortest. "Scoped" captures the tiering. "Symmetry" captures the fairness.

I recommend **"scoped symmetry"** as the coined term. It's two words, it's novel, and once you define it (procedural symmetry operates within the scope defined by tiered priority), the reader has a compact handle for the interaction.

Usage: "Procedural symmetry is scoped by tiered priority. Within a scope — citizens under law, children under parental care, students under a teacher — the rules are equal. Across scopes, the rules rightly differ. A parent owes more to their own child than to a stranger's. A nation owes more to its citizens than to foreign nationals. This is not asymmetry; it is scoped symmetry."

## IMPLICATION: THE INTERLOCK MADE CONCRETE

This gives you a specific, tangible way to explain why the principles must be taken together:

| Principle removed | What happens to scoped symmetry |
|---|---|
| Remove Reality (1) | Scopes become arbitrary — you can define "family" or "citizen" however you want, detached from biological or social reality |
| Remove Human Limits (2) | You believe you can extend symmetric concern infinitely — no need for scoping, universalism becomes possible |
| Remove Goodness/Survival (3) | The scopes serve no purpose — priority without a reason to prioritize, survival without the will to continue |
| Remove Tiered Priority (4) | Symmetry goes universal — no boundaries, no borders, no family preference, absurdity |
| Remove Procedural Symmetry (5) | Priority becomes tribal favoritism — you care about your tier but play favorites within it, power is unbounded |

Each principle protects the interaction from a specific failure mode. THIS is the interlock, made concrete.
