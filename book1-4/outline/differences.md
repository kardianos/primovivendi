**A Framework for Procedural Realism**  
**Five Indivisible Principles of Ordered Liberty and Human Flourishing**

These five principles form a single, non-separable system. They begin from the premise that reality is objective and knowable, that humans are finite and self-interested, and that political and social order must therefore be built on fair, equal procedures rather than engineered outcomes or imposed value hierarchies. The principles are:

1. **Reality is real, knowable, and consequential.** Truth is that which corresponds to observable reality, not to abstract ideals, social consensus, or untestable constructs.  
2. **Humans (and consciousness more broadly) are limited and selfish/self-interested.** Any workable order must account for fallibility, partiality, and the permanent fact of self-interest rather than assuming perfectibility.  
3. **What is good must include human survival and human surviving well/thriving.** Moral and political goods are not purely spiritual or ideological; they must demonstrably sustain life, family, community, and ordered prosperity under realistic constraints.  
4. **Self-anchored tiered responsibility.** Primary duty begins with self, then radiates outward—family, neighbors, then the larger nation/state. Enemies are real and carry negative value; resources, loyalty, and effort are finite and must be allocated accordingly.  
5. **Procedural symmetry.** Society and authority relate to every individual through the *same rules and processes*, applied equally regardless of identity, belief, or status. Outcome symmetry (forced equality of results) and value symmetry (all lifestyles or beliefs are interchangeable) are explicitly rejected.

The five cannot be mixed and matched; they stand or fall together.

### Thinkers and Traditions That Align Fully with the Framework

The following fit squarely inside the five principles as an integrated whole. Disagreements on application, emphasis, or specific policies remain possible—and productive—because all parties share the same epistemological ground (reality is real and knowable) and the same procedural rules of engagement.

- **Scottish Enlightenment Realism and Classic American Liberalism** (Thomas Reid, James Beattie, James Oswald, John Locke, James Wilson, Thomas Jefferson, James Madison)  
  These traditions treat reality as objective and accessible to common-sense reason (principle 1). They design limited government precisely because humans are limited and self-interested (principle 2), aim for ordered liberty that enables ordinary people to survive and thrive (principle 3), locate primary duties in the individual, family, and local community before the state (principle 4), and insist on equal rules under law while accepting naturally unequal outcomes (principle 5).

- **Edmund Burke and Russell Kirk**  
  Burke’s defense of prescription, prudence, and organic order, and Kirk’s canon of the “permanent things,” are built on the same realist anthropology and procedural restraint. Both reject abstract rationalism and utopian leveling. They locate moral order in correspondence to human nature and inherited custom, not in imposed outcomes or value flattening. Their vision is explicitly tiered, anti-utopian, and procedurally symmetric.

- **Heritage Foundation** (as an institution)  
  Heritage’s long record of originalist constitutionalism, limited-government advocacy, federalism, and empirical policy work aligns with every principle. It defends equal application of law, personal and family responsibility, and realistic constraints on power while treating ideological opponents as genuine threats to ordered liberty.

- **Peter Thiel** (likely compatible)  
  Thiel’s realism about human limits, technological stagnation, and elite failure, combined with his emphasis on competence, contracts, and decisive authority that still operates under consistent rules for all participants, fits the package. His critique of mass democracy is a critique of dysfunctional *mechanisms*, not of the requirement that the same rules apply equally to every individual. He seeks thriving through innovation and excellence, not through outcome or value symmetry.

Conversation with any of these figures or traditions is possible and potentially fruitful. Even sharp disagreements about the precise content of reality, the best policies, or the exact boundaries of authority remain *within* a shared framework: truth is correspondence to reality, procedures must be the same for all, and human limits are permanent. We could live together, argue productively, and maintain a common polity.

### Thinkers and Traditions That Fall Outside (and Why Dialogue Breaks)

The chart’s Leftist river (Rousseau, Hegel, Marx, Critical Theory, Woke/DEI/CRT/Queer Theory, American Progressivism, etc.) and most of the Post-Liberalism cluster (Stephen Wolfe, Curtis Yarvin/Mencius Moldbug, Charles Haywood, Aaron MacIntyre, etc.) diverge on at least one—and usually several—principles. They ultimately reject procedural symmetry in favor of engineered outcomes or value hierarchies, and they redefine truth itself.

- They treat reality as malleable (socially constructed by power or ideologically constructed by class/identity) or as subordinate to divine/scriptural fiat (pure Biblical Scripturalism or theonomic visions that override natural reason and evidence).  
- They therefore cannot share the same starting point for argument: truth is no longer correspondence but constructivism (social or “divine”).  
- This makes foundational conversation fruitless. One side seeks fair, equal procedures among limited, self-interested humans; the other seeks substantive transformation or confessional uniformity that assigns different rules, rights, or statuses by group, belief, or favored identity.

**Note on Christianity**  
Christians can and do fit comfortably within the five principles—provided they follow the historic “two books of God” approach (Scripture + Nature/General Revelation, as in Augustine, Aquinas, or the Scottish realists). Special revelation is integrated with, and tested against, observable reality and reason. Pure Scripturalism or theonomic constructivism that overrides empirical reality and procedural symmetry falls outside, just as secular social constructivism does.

**Note on Islam**  
A similar distinction applies in Islamic thought. Muslims can align with the five principles when they integrate reason and observable reality with revelation in a “two books” manner—treating the natural world and human reason as genuine sources of knowledge that complement (and are not wholly overridden by) scripture. Historical rationalist streams, such as elements of Mu'tazilite thought, emphasized reason’s role in ethics and knowledge, allowing potential compatibility with correspondence truth, limited human nature, and procedural approaches in limited contexts.

However, dominant Ash'arite occasionalism (exemplified by al-Ghazali) and related theological currents treat God as the sole true cause, with natural causality reduced to divine habit or constant recreation of events. This subordinates observable reality and secondary causation to divine will, making truth more a matter of revelatory or occasionalist construct than consistent correspondence to an independent, knowable order. When political application follows—such as classical Sharia frameworks that assign different legal statuses, rights, or procedures based on belief (e.g., dhimmi rules, apostasy laws, or confessional precedence)—it breaks procedural symmetry (#5) and often strains the other principles by prioritizing divine command structures over empirical realism about limited humans and tiered duties. Pure occasionalist or scripturalist approaches thus fall outside the framework, rendering foundational dialogue difficult for the same reasons as secular constructivism: the nature of truth and reality itself diverges.

In short, the Scottish Enlightenment realists, American Liberals, Burke, Kirk, Heritage, and Thiel represent the realistic, procedurally symmetric path. The others want a fundamentally different order built on different foundations. The former allows coexistence and productive disagreement; the latter does not.
