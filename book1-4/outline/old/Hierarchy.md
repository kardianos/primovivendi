**A Framework for Procedural Realism**  
**Hierarchy Within the Five Indivisible Principles**

Hierarchy is not optional; it is a necessary consequence of the five principles taken as a single, non-separable system. Because humans are limited and self-interested (principle 2), equal rules applied to unequal abilities, efforts, and choices will reliably produce unequal outcomes. Procedural symmetry (principle 5) explicitly permits and protects this natural hierarchy: the same fair processes apply to everyone, but no forced equality of results or values is imposed. The framework therefore demands that hierarchy be *legitimate, accountable, and reciprocal*—anchored in reality (principle 1), oriented toward human thriving (principle 3), and lived out through self-anchored tiered responsibility (principle 4).

The principles do not prescribe any particular mechanism for selecting leaders (election, appointment, inheritance, or merit-based promotion). They only require that once hierarchy exists, both those who hold power and those who do not relate to one another through the *same rules and procedures*. This produces a stable, ordered liberty rather than tyranny from above or chaos from below.

### From the Perspective of Those in Power: Responsible Stewardship (the mirror of Noblesse Oblige)

Those elevated by talent, circumstance, inheritance, election, or appointment bear heightened duties precisely because they possess greater capacity and visibility. Their obligations flow directly from the principles:

- **Reality and truth (1):** They must govern on the basis of observable human nature and empirical consequences, not abstract ideologies or personal whims. Decisions are judged by correspondence to reality, not by narrative or intention.
- **Limited humans (2):** Power must be exercised with humility and self-restraint. Leaders acknowledge their own self-interest and fallibility; they therefore design systems that constrain *all* power—including their own—through checks, transparency, and fixed rules.
- **Thriving (3):** Authority exists to enable human survival and flourishing, not to remake human nature. Policies and decisions must demonstrably sustain families, communities, and ordered prosperity under realistic constraints.
- **Tiered responsibility (4):** Leaders owe primary loyalty to the polity they serve (nation, institution, or community) and must protect it from genuine enemies. They may not sacrifice the whole for abstract universalism or personal gain.
- **Procedural symmetry (5):** The same rules apply to the powerful as to everyone else. They receive no special exemptions, no immunity from law, and no right to impose outcome or value symmetry. Legitimate hierarchy is earned and maintained by fair process, not by fiat or group identity.

In practice this is the classic duty of stewardship: protect the innocent, punish the guilty, preserve inherited order where it works, reform only where evidence demands, and use elevated position for the common good rather than exploitation. Failure to meet these duties delegitimizes the hierarchy and invites corrective mechanisms (impeachment, recall, replacement, or, in extremis, lawful resistance) that themselves remain procedurally symmetric.

### From the Perspective of Those Not in Power: Reciprocal Civic Duty (the mirror of responsible followership)

Citizens, subordinates, and the governed also bear active, non-servile obligations. Their duties are the necessary counterpart that makes hierarchy sustainable:

- **Reality and truth (1):** They must assess leaders and institutions by observable results and correspondence to reality, not by slogans or emotional appeals. Blind obedience or reflexive resentment both violate realism.
- **Limited humans (2):** They accept their own partiality and self-interest while recognizing that some hierarchy is inevitable and beneficial. They do not demand perfect leaders or perfect equality of outcomes.
- **Thriving (3):** They contribute productively in their own station—through work, family, community participation, and honest self-improvement—because personal and local flourishing under fair rules is the foundation of broader prosperity.
- **Tiered responsibility (4):** Primary duty remains self, family, neighbors, and local community. Loyalty to the larger polity is real but secondary and conditional on legitimate authority. Enemies of the order are recognized as such; citizens do not extend equal concern to those who actively undermine the shared framework.
- **Procedural symmetry (5):** They obey the same rules that bind everyone, including those above them. They extend deference and cooperation to legitimate hierarchy while retaining the right (and duty) to criticize, petition, vote, or lawfully resist when procedures are violated. They reject both servility and egalitarian leveling; they fulfill their station virtuously without envy of natural unequal outcomes.

This reciprocal duty is not servility—it is the active virtue of “good followership.” It sustains the system by rewarding legitimate authority with loyalty and productivity while providing built-in accountability when stewards fail.

### Integration and Stability

Because the five principles are indivisible, hierarchy cannot be justified by outcome symmetry (“we need elites to force equality”) or value symmetry (“all beliefs deserve equal public standing”). It is justified only by correspondence to limited human nature and by equal rules that allow unequal results. When both rulers and ruled honor their respective obligations, the result is ordered liberty: legitimate authority without tyranny, responsible participation without chaos, and human thriving without utopian engineering.

This framework allows Christians (operating via the “two books” of Scripture and Nature), Muslims (operating via reason-integrated streams rather than pure occasionalism), and secular realists to coexist productively. All can argue vigorously about specific policies or the precise content of reality while sharing the same procedural ground and the same recognition that hierarchy is both necessary and accountable. The alternative—rejecting procedural symmetry for confessional or ideological uniformity—breaks the system and renders foundational dialogue impossible.

In short, the five principles do not abolish hierarchy; they civilize and constrain it. They demand responsible stewardship from above and responsible participation from below, under the same rules for all. This is the realistic path to enduring social order.
