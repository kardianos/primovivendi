# Provenance regarding David Hume
Primary sources include *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739), *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748), and *An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751). Hume is a skeptic who grounds morality in utility and sentiment.

## 5-Principle Compatibility Summary

| Principle | Rating | Reason |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| P1 (Reality) | **Incompatible** | Denies knowledge of causation; we see conjunction, not necessary connection; skepticism undermines knowability |
| P2 (Human Nature) | **Compatible** | "Reason is slave of passions"; humans are partial to self; "avidity" is universal; constrained vision |
| P3 (Tiered Responsibility) | **Compatible** | Sympathy fades with distance; natural obligation to children > parents > brothers; tiered by proximity |
| P4 (Goodness) | **Compatible** | "Public utility is sole origin of justice"; whatever contributes to happiness recommends itself; proto-utilitarian |
| P5 (Procedural Symmetry) | **Compatible** | "Law always limits every power"; rules of justice are "steadfast and immutable"; pragmatic symmetry |
| Humility Test | **Passes** | Skepticism is inherently humble; no certainty justifies persecution; "proportion belief to evidence" |

## Reality is Real, Knowable, and Consequential (Incompatible: Skepticism)
"All our valid reasoning concerning matter of fact and existence is founded on the relation of Cause and Effect... [but] the knowledge of this relation is not, in any instance, attained by reasonings a priori; but arises entirely from experience." (*Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*, Section IV).
"We have no other notion of cause and effect, but that of certain objects, which have been always conjoined together... we cannot penetrate into the reason of the conjunction." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).
"The contrary of every matter of fact is still possible; because it can never imply a contradiction." (*Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*).
"Nature will always maintain her rights, and prevail in the end over any abstract reasoning whatsoever." (*Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*).

## Humans are Limited and Selfish
"Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions, and can never pretend to any other office than to serve and obey them." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).
"Men are not blamed for having such partiality [self-interest]; they are blamed for not restraining it within existing limits." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).
"In general, it may be affirmed that there is no such passion in human minds, as the love of mankind, merely as such." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).
"Avidity alone, of acquiring goods and possessions for ourselves and our nearest friends, is insatiable, perpetual, universal, and directly destructive of society." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).

## Tiered Self-Anchored Responsibility
"Sympathy with persons is much fainter than our concern for ourselves, and sympathy with persons remote from us is much fainter than that with persons near and contiguous." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).
"The convention for the distinction of property... is not derived from any internal impression [of natural right]... it is an artificial virtue." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).
"A man's natural obligation to his children is stronger than to his parents; that to his parents stronger than to his brothers." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).
"We are naturally partial to ourselves, and to our friends; but are capable of learning the advantages of a more equitable conduct." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).

## Goodness is to Live and Live Well
"Public utility is the sole origin of justice, and... reflections on the beneficial consequences of this virtue are the sole foundation of its merit." (*Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals*).
"Whatever is valuable in any kind... arises from the useful or agreeable." (*Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals*).
"The peace and security of human society." (*Treatise of Human Nature*, regarding the purpose of Justice).
"Everything which contributes to the happiness of society recommends itself directly to our approbation." (*Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals*).

## Procedural Symmetry
"The law always limits every power it gives." (*History of England*).
"Political writers have established it as a maxim, that, in contriving any system of government... every man ought to be supposed a knave, and to have no other end, in all his actions, than private interest." (*Essays, Moral, Political, and Literary*).
"General flexibility of laws... is a necessary inconvenience." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).
"The rules of justice are steadfast and immutable." (*Treatise of Human Nature*).

## Humility (The Criticism Test)
"In all investigations of this nature, we must proceed with trials and experiments." (*Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals*).
"A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence." (*Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*).
"Be a philosopher; but, amidst all your philosophy, be still a man." (*Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*).
"The most perfect philosophy of the natural kind only staves off our ignorance a little longer." (*Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*).

## 5-Principle Analysis

**P1 (Reality)**: **Incompatible (philosophical), Compatible (practical).** Hume denies we can KNOW causation—we only observe constant conjunction. "We cannot penetrate into the reason of the conjunction." This undermines P1's claim that reality is knowable. HOWEVER, Hume is a practical realist: "Nature will always maintain her rights." He plays backgammon and lives as if causation is real. The incompatibility is theoretical; practice aligns.

**P2 (Human Nature)**: Compatible. "Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions." "Avidity of acquiring goods for ourselves and our nearest friends is insatiable, perpetual, universal." "There is no such passion as the love of mankind, merely as such." This is P2 fully articulated: humans are selfish, partial, driven by passion rather than reason.

**P3 (Tiered Responsibility)**: Compatible. "Sympathy with persons remote from us is much fainter than with persons near and contiguous." "A man's natural obligation to his children is stronger than to his parents; that to his parents stronger than to his brothers." This is the gradient of P3: responsibility radiates outward with decreasing intensity.

**P4 (Goodness)**: Compatible. "Public utility is the sole origin of justice." "Whatever is valuable arises from the useful or agreeable." "Everything which contributes to the happiness of society recommends itself directly to our approbation." Hume grounds goodness in human flourishing, not metaphysical duty. This aligns with P4.

**P5 (Procedural Symmetry)**: Compatible. "The law always limits every power." "Rules of justice are steadfast and immutable." Hume recognizes that fair rules must be general and apply equally, not because of metaphysical truth, but because that's what WORKS for social cooperation. This is pragmatic P5.

**Humility Test**: Passes. "A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence." "The most perfect philosophy only staves off our ignorance a little longer." Skepticism is inherently humble—no certainty could justify persecution. "Be a philosopher, but be still a man." Hume's system cannot generate the certainty needed to suppress critique.
