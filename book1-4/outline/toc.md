## Expanded point: SCOPED SYMMETRY

Procedural symmetry does NOT say "everyone gets the same treatment regardless of relationship, role, or category."

It says: **within a defined scope — a tier, a role, a jurisdiction — the same rules apply to everyone in that scope.**

This is not a weakening of procedural symmetry. It is what makes it work. Without scoping, procedural symmetry becomes universalist absurdity: you'd owe a stranger in another country the same concern as your own child. That's not symmetry; that's the erasure of tiered responsibility.

### Examples of Scoped Symmetry:

**Family tier:**
- Symmetry: all your children get equal parental treatment. No favorites.
- Asymmetry across tiers: you owe your children more than your neighbor's children. This is not "unequal treatment" — it's correct tiered priority.
- Power: you have authority over your children. They don't have equal power. But you're bounded by symmetric obligation to each child.

**Citizen tier:**
- Symmetry: all citizens are subject to the same criminal law, the same procedural rights.
- Asymmetry across tiers: citizens get citizen treatment (safety nets, voting, legal standing). Non-citizens don't. This is borders doing their job.
- Power: government has power over citizens. But that power is bounded by the same procedural rules that protect every citizen equally. The president doesn't get a different criminal code.

**Role-scoped:**
- Symmetry: all judges owe all defendants the same due process. All teachers owe all students the same grading standards.
- Asymmetry across roles: a judge has power a defendant doesn't. A teacher has authority a student doesn't.
- The power is legitimate because the role is defined, the procedures are symmetric within the role, and the role itself serves tiered responsibility (the judge protects the community tier from individual predators).

**Between nations:**
- Symmetry: doesn't apply across borders in the same way. International law isn't real law. Military might is the ultimate arbiter.
- This isn't a failure of symmetry — it's the recognition that the "scope" of procedural symmetry is the polity, not the planet.

### Parent Child Relationship

This is the clearest case and worth getting right.

A parent has:
- **Tiered priority**: children come before strangers. The child's well-being is a higher-weighted concern.
- **Power over children**: the parent makes decisions the child cannot. This is legitimate because the child is within the parent's tiered responsibility and the child lacks the capacity for full self-anchoring.
- **Procedural symmetry WITHIN the family**: the parent owes each child equal treatment. Not identical treatment (children differ), but symmetric procedural treatment — equal concern, equal access to the parent's care and judgment, no arbitrary favoritism.
- **Procedural symmetry WITHIN the parent's role**: all parents owe all their children the same standard of care. A parent doesn't get to arbitrarily neglect one child any more than a judge gets to arbitrarily deny one defendant due process.

The power asymmetry (parent > child) is:
- Justified by tiered responsibility (the parent is responsible FOR the child)
- Bounded by procedural symmetry (the parent must treat all children equally)
- Temporary (children grow up — power transfers as capacity develops)
- Accountable (the community/nation tier can intervene if the parent violates the symmetric obligation of care)

This is the pattern for ALL legitimate power:
**Power is legitimate when it serves tiered responsibility AND is bounded by procedural symmetry within its scope.**

### RELATIONSHIP TO THE OTHER PRINCIPLES

This interaction isn't just between principles 4 and 5. It connects to all five:

**Reality & Truth (1):** Humans really are limited and capacity really is distributed unequally. Children really do need parental authority. Some people really are more competent at certain roles. Denying this is reality denial.

**Human Limits (2):** Because humans are limited, power must be scoped. No one person can exercise symmetric concern across all of humanity. The scope must narrow to what a limited being can actually handle. This is why Dunbar's number and kin selection matter — they describe the real boundaries of human concern.

**Goodness/Survival (3):** The whole point of scoped symmetry is to enable survival and flourishing. A society that tries to extend full procedural symmetry to everyone on earth collapses. A family that extends equal concern to strangers' children neglects its own. Survival requires scoping.

**The Interlock:** This is a concrete case of why the principles must be taken together. Principle 4 without principle 5 is tribalism. Principle 5 without principle 4 is universalism. Together, they produce legitimate, bounded, fair order.

## Precepts Matrix Tool

**How to use it:**  
When facing any decision—big or small—run the situation through all six filters **in order**. All must align. If any filter fails or feels forced, pause, adjust the choice, or reject the path. This forces the entire bundle (Survival as Root + the five foundations) to work together so none can be cherry-picked or used in isolation.  

Keep this outline handy. Use it weekly for habits, major life choices, family discussions, or even policy opinions. The scenarios below show exactly how the matrix works in practice, with full examples and counter-examples woven in.

**Quick mental version (memorize this):**  
- 1. Survival filter? (Will this propagate *flourishing* life?)  
- 2. Reality? (Facts, not wishes)  
- 3. Limits? (Oxygen mask — self first)  
- 4. Tiered responsibility? (Core before outer rings)  
- 5. What must be good? (Excellence, beauty, joy, ownership, children?)  
- 6. Symmetry? (Would I accept this if roles reversed?)  

Run the matrix often. When all six align, you are living the bundle. When they do not, you are choosing a path that ultimately fails to survive. This turns the abstract foundations into a daily, practical discipline.

**Scenario 1: Cleaning and organizing your home**  
- Survival (Flourishing Propagation): Increases long-term platform for family health, joy, and raising the next generation well.  
- Reality Check: Dirt, clutter, and chaos have real negative health, mental, and social consequences.  
- Human Limits Check: You cannot magically outsource everything or live in chaos without cost; start with your own effort.  
- Tiered Responsibility: Self and immediate family first (oxygen mask) — tidy your space before demanding help from others.  
- What Must Be Good: Creates beauty, order, ownership, and a platform for joy (dancing, friends, games, hospitality).  
- Procedural Symmetry: You do not demand others clean your house while neglecting your own; the rule applies equally.  
- Balanced Decision: Take ownership: clean regularly. This builds a stable base for flourishing life and future generations.  
- Counter-Example (What Breaks When Any Filter Is Ignored): Ignoring reality + responsibility → live in filth “because cleaning is oppressive” → health decline, depression, inability to host or raise children. Survival quality collapses.

**Scenario 2: Deciding whether to have (more) children**  
- Survival (Flourishing Propagation): Directly propagates your lineage, culture, and values in a high-quality way that is worth continuing.  
- Reality Check: Biological clock, financial realities, parenting demands, and child outcomes are concrete facts.  
- Human Limits Check: You cannot love children into existence without personal capacity; limits on time, energy, and money are real.  
- Tiered Responsibility: Secure self and marriage first, then children, before wider community or global demands.  
- What Must Be Good: Children are the ultimate good — love, meaning, joy, continuity, and the only way survival has purpose.  
- Procedural Symmetry: You accept the same rules and expectations you place on others regarding family formation and support.  
- Balanced Decision: Have children only when you can responsibly anchor them. Prioritize quality + responsibility over raw quantity.  
- Counter-Example (What Breaks When Any Filter Is Ignored): Raw survival only (ignore limits + “what must be good”) → have many children in chaos → poverty, resentment, poor outcomes for the next generation. Or “right to exist” victim framing: demand society subsidize endless children without personal ownership → collapse of tiers and symmetry.

**Scenario 3: Forgiving someone who betrayed you (friend or family)**

- Reality Check: Honestly assess what actually happened, the evidence, and the realistic likelihood of genuine change.

- Tiered Responsibility: Family/community tier takes clear precedence over abstract “forgive everyone” or distant strangers.  
- What Must Be Good: Combines love (holding on to the relationship) + forgiveness (letting go of the debt) to restore trust, joy, and platforms for more life.  
- Procedural Symmetry: Would you want the exact same mercy, process, and accountability if the roles were reversed?  
- Balanced Decision: Forgive where reality and accountability allow, restore where possible, but maintain firm boundaries. Rebuild the relationship only if it strengthens flourishing survival.
