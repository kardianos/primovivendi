* Introduction
  * To be productive in society, we must discuss the premise, not conclusions.
  * Once we understand each other's premises, including where we believe we should be going, we can talk about layers on top of that.
  * I believe that these are the core premises that fundamentally divide things for societies:
    - Is there truth and can we know it?
    - Are humans selfish/limited or good/changeable?
    - Should human life continue, should human life thrive?
    - What should be prioritized?
    - What is justice in society? Outcome symmetry? Procedural symmetry? Value Symmetry?
  * These premises are present in religion, politics, and society.
  * Here we introduce 5 principles that work together and provide a specific set of premises that work together.
    - Everyone either explicitly or implicitly have these.
    - We recommend having a premise that benefit yourself, others, and are in alignment to reality.
  * To retain the meaning, all 5 principles must be active and be interpreted and used consistent with the other principles at the same time.
  * A principle must not be applied in isolation of the other.
  * This has more to do with politics then philosophy.
  * This does not replace or subsume religion or atheism.
  * This is a positive vision for defining systems that can work together and by doing so, define what cannot work with these principles.
  * This is not a system of virtue, honor, or morality; instead we seek how to live.
* Foundations
  * Reality & Truth: reality is real, knowable, and consequential. Truth is what corresponds to reality.
  * Human Nature: limited, self-interested
    - productive when properly ordered
    - Don't try to make people angle.
    - Don't try to make people philosophers.
    - Understand people will make irrational decisions.
  * Tiered Self-Anchored Priority
    - Prioritize yourself and family, then neighbors, then community, then nation, then world.
    - Do not drop any tiers off.
    - Enemies are real and have negative value.
    - Kin Selection
    - Dunbar's Number
  * Goodness - Surviving and Flourishing
    - Goodness must include humans living, and living well.
    - Only what survives and thrives can be good in the future.
    - We must allow failure in order for goodness to survive and thrive.
  * Procedural Symmetry
    - Equal procedures applied to all.
    - This is what seeking truth is.
    - Not outcome symmetry. Not value symmetry.
  * All or Nothing: All 5 principles, removing, or distorting one, distorts them all.

    - Love is holding on; Forgiveness is letting go. Both useful for long-term personal and social relationships.

    - Will to live generationally.
    - Generational Symmetry, derived from what is Good (surviving) and procedural symmetry.
    - Not having children is a failure to survive generationally. An end to everyone who has gone before you.

  * Action begins with you. Integrate these references directly into your life.
* Society
  * Rule of Law
    - Truth
    - Procedural Symmetry: all are subject to the same law.
    - Does NOT mean rule by bureaucrats.
    - The law exists only as far as it is enforced.
    - Unequal enforcement is de-facto unequal law.
    - Humans are limited: The law must be knowable: not too large, not too complicated.
    - Leniency towards criminals is violence to the innocent.
    - It is clear you can change the nature and course of a country if you remove the small percent of those who are violent.
  * Freedom of Speech
    - To Align with Truth, society must be able to discuss ideas.
    - Without being able to discuss ideas society cannot correct errors, limits flourishing.
    - Humans are limited.
  * Borders and Common Defense
    - Borders allow safety nets provided for by by citizens, for citizens, to limit abuse. This is procedural symmetry and tiered priority.
    - Borders are protection against enemies.
    - Allows different laws and customs without universal acceptance. Borders and laws correspond.
    - There is no such thing as international law. The only real international law is military might.
  * Power Is Real and Necessary
    - Those in power must wield real power to enforce law, defend borders, and maintain order.
    - Power is good when it remains bounded by the same law as everyone else.
  * Unity & Exclusion
    - Unity implies exclusion. To be unified in something, means practically there are other states which you don't have.
    - Unity can be good so long as the ideas and practices are good.
    - Unity is useful to do bigger things than an individual: society is the sum of the vectors.
    - Exclusion isn't a weapon, but you must recognize truly fundamental differences and when required, exclude them.
  * Survival, ultimately, requires force. Enemies are real. This is true at the individual and nation level.
    - The rule of law (procedural symmetry) drastically reduces the need for blunt force and clarifies expectations for everyone.
  * Ownership is real and necessary. Responsibility cannot exist outside of ownership.
    - Markets and currency are not a philosophy: they are emergent from specialization.
    - Failure must be allowed for true ownership.
  * Education
    - We must teach new generations the skills to replicate and improve technology.
    - Society itself is a form of technology, we must not lie about it's structure or basis.
    - Truth, Beauty, and excellence must be taught. Humans are limited and selfish.
* Deeper Grounding
  * Reason is not enough.
    - Reason that is not grounded, is just hallucination or imagination.
    - Reason can guides our ideas.
    - Reality has final say.
  * The Importance of Systems
    - We should seek truth, but humans are limited, selfish, often irrational.
    - Systems enable faulty humans into things beyond them.
    - Writing consistently in a diary.
    - The system we call Science ideally is a system that separates out irrational ideas into ideas that can be demonstrated over time and place. This doesn't imply an institution of science, but the system and any person or small group can practice.
    - The system we call Government ideally prevents our individual selfishness or irrationality from governing, but a wiser form. This doesn't imply any specific form of government, nor does it imply all government is good. But a system of justice and common defense is superior to individuals.
  * Goodness that includes survival in completely linked to Reality is real.
  * Discovered Technology
    - Money: any group of people who have different needs at different times and produce different things, all come up with some form of money.
    - Language: To be valuable, we must have some agreement on meaning.
  * Division of Power
    - Reality is complex and large. Humans are limited and small. Society must specialize to thrive.
    - A political statesmen or even an HOA board member, or CEO, is a type of specialization. They take care of the details. If they do a poor job, they can be replaced.
    - Power is real, and the division of power is real too. 
    - Power is needed remove murderers from the innocent. Power can be abused, but the lesson isn't that power is evil, but either the system was insufficient or those people who held power were corrupt or without sufficient honor, goodness, or proper direction.
    - Parents exercise power and authority over their children to direct them in life and keep them safe. The lesson from when a parent hurts their children isn't power or parents with power is bad, but that those parents were bad.
    - Teachers exercising authority in the classroom, instructing their students is not bad. It is appropriate division of power.
    - At times power is divided between authority and general public. Other times it is divided generationally, for those children aren't withheld power forever, but until they grow up.
    - This is a lesson of procedural symmetry and the limited nature of humans.
  * The Principles are Broad
    - This framework is intended to be broad: use it to judge past societies and present ones.
    - There are better and worse ways of living as individuals and as societies.
    - But so long as we align with these principles, we can improve and continue into the future.
* Distinctions
  * Procedural Symmetry, not Outcome Symmetry.
    - In critical theory, outcome symmetry is called equity.
    - Outcome symmetry breeds corruption.
      - Humans vary in talent, effort, and honesty.
      - Central rules can't capture local realities.
      - People respond to incentives. If the system punishes success or rewards failure, they will fail or cheat.
    - Outcome symmetry entrenches classism.
      - Someone must pay for the symmetry - the human 80/20 rule still exists.
      - A new ruling class always emerges.
      - Corruption as a class membership test.
      - Suppression of mobility and class becomes permanent.
  * Procedural Symmetry, not Value Symmetry.
  * Strength, Not weakness.
    - There is no "right to exist".
  * Responsibility, Not Sacrifice.
    - Responsibility can include sacrifice, especially for inner tiers like for family.
    - Sacrifice by itself is waste and not a virtue.
    - Responsibility, not unbounded action love.
  * Outcomes are more important than feelings.
    - Feeling good about something in society is not a good reason to do something.
    - Proof of good outcomes is a reason to do something.
    - Experiments should be bounded and evaluated.
  * Discovered vs Constructed
    - An individual word can be constructed in society, but the core need for stable communication is discovered.
    - The type of money (shells, paper, electronic ledger) is constructed, but money itself is discovered.
  * Not virtues:
    - Tolerance - this is logically insane to have tolerance as a fundamental virtue, as the least tolerant will naturally thrive in such a society. This is born out in both reality and in game theory.
    - Diversity - Different people and different opinions are fine and often healthy, but it isn't a virtue.
  * Kindness vs Politeness
    - Kindness within family. Politeness in society. Not kindness in society.
    - Kindness in family is a form of love.
    - Kindness is distinct from politeness.
    - In large societies, kindness as the norm or policy can subsidize irresponsible or criminal.
    - Enemies exist and kindness extended to them rewards predation and selects against excellence, responsibility, and survival. In game theory in an open system the least-kind strategy eventually dominates.
    - Social kindness is an error that should be reserved for close family.
  * Life, Not Nihilism.
    - Propagation of a full life, not just numbers.
