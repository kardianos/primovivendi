# Outline of Core Concepts
## Tiered Procedural Symmetry (TPS)
### Common Principles Toward a Reality-Based Life
**Author:** Daniel Theophanes

---

## Introduction

**Tiered Procedural Symmetry** is a worldview focused on a productive way to live and live together, grounded in the following foundational principles:

1. **Reality is real, consequential, and knowable.** Truth is what corresponds to reality.
2. **Humans are limited and selfish.** We must design systems that work within our limitations.
3. **Responsibility is tiered.** It begins with ourselves and family, radiating outward in decreasing measure to community, region, and nation.
4. **Goodness is defined axiomatically.** Human life should continue and continue well.
5. **Procedural Symmetry is foundational.** We bind ourselves to the same procedures we demand of others.

**Key Distinction:** TPS is not a new religion or blended philosophy. It respects that the "why" behind different worldviews may be irreconcilable, but operates on the principle that incompatible beliefs do not prevent cooperation on the practical "how" of building a society.

# Part I: Core Principles

## Chapter 1: Reality is Real, Truth is Knowable

### The First Axiom
**Reality is real, knowable, and consequential.**

### Key Concepts

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Primacy of Existence** | Existence has primacy over consciousness. We must exist before we can perceive. Consciousness apprehends reality; it does not create it. |
| **Independence of Reality** | Reality operates independently of hopes, feelings, and social status. A dropped ball falls regardless of your thoughts. |
| **Consequences** | Reality provides feedback, often brutally. Societies can declare fire is cold, but the first child who touches a flame will still be burned. |
| **Discovered Solutions** | Social systems like money and prohibitions against murder are not arbitrary constructs but discovered solutions to real-world constraints. |
| **Stable Definitions** | Words and definitions must remain stable for effective communication. Arbitrarily redefining words degrades shared understanding. |
| **Reason's Role** | Reason reduces the number of things we must test, but reason untethered from reality is hallucination. |

### Characteristics of Knowing
- **Accuracy:** How close your understanding is to reality
- **Precision:** How much detail it can describe
- **Single Reality:** There is only one reality, shared by everyone

## Chapter 2: Truth, Knowing What Is

### The Definition of Truth
**Truth is what corresponds to reality.**

### Key Principles

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **No Relative Truth** | There is no "your truth" or "my truth." What is true is what corresponds to reality. |
| **Provisional Knowledge** | Our knowledge is always provisional and subject to revision. Fallibility does not negate the possibility of knowledge. |
| **Self-Consistent Senses** | Senses must be generally consistent with themselves over time. |
| **Difference Detection** | Senses must be able to detect differences between different inputs. |

### Requirements for Truth-Seeking
- **Not shared subjectivity** — but self-consistent senses that yield consistent, correlatable data
- **Objective and discovered, not created**
- **Consequential** — Truth reflects reality, so it shares reality's consequences

### The Lysenko Example
Trofim Lysenko's rejection of genetics in Soviet agriculture demonstrates that ideas have consequences because ideas are claims about what is true. His false doctrines contributed to famines killing millions.

### Cautions for Truth-Seeking
1. **Models can work for wrong reasons** (e.g., Ptolemaic astronomy)
2. **Truth is indifferent to feelings** — value truth even when uncomfortable
3. **Apply equal scrutiny** — The same critical standards must apply to all ideas

### The Constrained Vision of Humanity

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Finite Time** | Forces choices and trade-offs |
| **Finite Bodies/Minds** | Require rest, succumb to biases |

### Contrasting Visions

| Vision | Belief | Consequence |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Constrained Vision** | Human flaws are inherent; wisdom lies in designing systems within limitations | Pragmatic approach to governance |
| **Unconstrained Vision** | Human flaws are products of corrupt external systems; humans can be perfected | Utopian dreams end in totalitarian nightmare |

### Human Selfishness (Descriptive, Not Prescriptive)
- Human selfishness means an individual's primary driver is their own survival and well-being
- This is not a moral judgment but a descriptive claim about how we are wired
- **Kin Selection:** Concerns naturally radiate outward from self

### The Noble Savage vs. Reality
- **Rousseau's View:** Humans are born good and pure, corrupted by society
- **TPS View:** Humans start selfish and savage; society (laws, traditions, institutions) civilizes us
- **Key Insight:** Goodness must be taught; virtue is learned over a lifetime

## Chapter 4: Tiered, Self-Anchored Responsibility

### Definition of Responsibility
**Responsibility is the mindset of creating and maintaining.**

### Key Principles

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Self-Anchored** | Responsibility begins with ourselves. Our responsibility for ourselves is greatest. |
| **Radiating Outward** | Concentric circles of decreasing intensity: Self → Spouse/Children → Extended Family → Neighbors/Community → Countrymen → Humanity |
| **Not Arbitrary** | The gradient reflects hard limits of time, knowledge, loyalty, and symmetric obligation |

### Examples of Responsibility
- Building/maintaining a house (not destroying foundations or load-bearing walls)
- Growing food (cultivating soil, not salting earth)
- Raising children (time, energy, will to care)
- Managing a team (owning what the team produces)

### Recognizing Enemies
- **Definition:** Those who seek to abolish or kill your nation/family
- **The Moriori Example:** The Moriori people's principled pacifism led to their destruction when invaded by Māori tribes in 1835
- **Lesson:** Failing to recognize existential enemies is dangerous and suicidal

### Rejected Concepts
- **Universalism:** Giving each person the same consideration (prideful, not humble)
- **Radical Altruism:** Caring for "everyone on earth" or "all strangers on different continents"

## Chapter 5: What Must be Good

### The Axiom of Goodness
**Human life should continue and continue well, both generationally and individually.**

### Two Components

| Component | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **"Continue"** | Individual survival AND generational survival (having and raising the next generation) |
| **"Continue Well"** | Not mere existence but flourishing — quality, health, prosperity, purpose |

### Living Well as a Vector
- Living well is not a destination or utopia
- It is a **direction**, not a location
- Measured by the positive slope between two points in time
- **Example:** Sweeping a dirty floor → clean floor (imposing order on chaos)

### Objective Goodness
- Because reality is consequential and we have a fixed criterion, we can make objective statements about what is good
- **Example:** "It is better to rest one's hand on a soft pillow than on a hot flame" is objectively true, not subjective opinion

### Evaluating Actions
**Test:** On balance, and over the long term, does this contribute to or detract from humans living and continuing well?

## Chapter 6: The Unifying Principle of Procedural Symmetry

### Definition
**Procedural Symmetry** is the principle of applying the same rules and methods of evaluation consistently to all subjects within a given domain. The process for judging remains the same whether it applies to you or to others.

### Seven Domains of Application

| Domain | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| **Social** | Rule of Law (identical legal process for all); Freedom of Speech (procedure available to all) |
| **Truth-Seeking** | Same critical evaluation methods for all claims, regardless of origin or bias |
| **Logic** | Formal validity — argument structure independent of content (e.g., modus ponens) |
| **Behavior** | Test rules by universal application — "What if everyone followed this rule?" |
| **Generational** | Reciprocal duty: receive care from predecessors → provide care to successors |
| **Conflict** | Future survival and peace as goals; strong response to violations of sovereignty |
| **Selection** | Single standard uniformly applied (e.g., standardized tests with single threshold) |

### Inferior Alternatives

| Alternative | Core Belief | Problem |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| **Value Symmetry** ("Eye for an eye") | Fairness = equal retribution | Backward-looking; breeds rigid, dogmatic systems |
| **Outcome Symmetry** ("Equal Outcomes") | Fairness = identical results for all | Requires unequal application of rules; functions as secular religion |

### Tiered Procedures
Procedural Symmetry may be tier-based (different procedures for different tiers of relationship) but must never be identity-based.

Procedural Symmetry demands not moral passivity, but the courage to judge fairly.

### Key Concepts

| Concept | Description |

### Mercy vs. Permissiveness

| | Definition |
|---|------------|
| **Mercy** | Conscious choice to withhold a just consequence AFTER a wrong is clearly identified |
| **Permissiveness** | Failure to identify the wrong in the first place |

### Scope of Judgment (Following Tiered Responsibility)

2. Within our family
3. Community and nation under shared rule of law
4. NOT: Stranger's private life in a distant land (hubris)

### Rule of Law
The formalized system of judgment for a society, applied symmetrically to those within its jurisdiction.

## Chapter 8: Consequences of Generational Obligation

### Definition
**Generational Obligation** is Procedural Symmetry applied across time: *We must do for the next generation what we wish the previous generation had done for us.*

### The Chain Metaphor
- We are not isolated atoms but **links in a great chain**
- A link has two responsibilities:
  1. Faithfully receive strength from the past
  2. Reliably transmit strength to the future

### The Scale of Inheritance
We inherit far more than genetic code:
- Language (developed over thousands of years)
- Scientific knowledge and technologies
- Moral inheritance (traditions, stories, accumulated wisdom)
- Political institutions (Rule of Law, constitutional government)

### The Broken Link
A generation that consumes inheritance without transmitting it betrays both past and future.

### Two Core Duties

| Duty | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Biological/Demographic Imperative** | To have children — the ultimate act of hope; rejection of anti-natalism |
| **Raising Children** | Transforming dependent infants into competent, virtuous adults; transmitting skills and ethical/cultural inheritance |

### Responsible Conservation
The goal is not stagnation but passing on foundational principles and tools that allow the next generation to flourish and innovate. This is "responsible conservation, not revolutionary destruction."

## Chapter 9: Love, Enemies, and the Golden Bridge of Forgiveness

### Two Forms of Love

| Form | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Dispositional Love** | General goodwill and non-malice toward all people within society; baseline respect for humanity |
| **Action-Based Love** | Demonstrable, prioritized investment of limited resources (time, energy, attention, money) for another's well-being |

**Critical Rule:** Never offer action-based love to a friend/stranger greater than what you owe your spouse or family.

### Enemies Are Real
An enemy is someone who, through actions and stated goals, demonstrates fundamental opposition to your flourishing.

**Types:**
- Those who directly threaten survival
- Those who subvert foundational principles (procedural symmetry, Rule of Law)
- Complete narcissists who act as destructive forces

**The Failure of Universalism:** Naively projecting desire for peace onto those explicitly based on conquest or destruction.

### Two Forms of Forgiveness

| Form | Description | Purpose |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Personal Forgiveness** | Internal act of letting go resentment/desire for revenge | For oneself; prerequisite for living well |
| **Social Reconciliation** | External act of restoring trust and cooperation | Requires full accountability + demonstrated sustained change |

**The Golden Bridge:** Strategic choice of reconciliation offered only after strict conditions are met.

## Chapter 10: Taking Responsibility

### Responsibility vs. Blame

| Concept | Focus |
|---------|-------|
| **Blame** | Assigning a past error (backward-looking) |
| **Responsibility** | Committing to a future outcome (forward-looking) |

**Key Question:** Not "Whose fault is this?" but "What can I do to fix this?"

### Two Halves of Responsibility
1. **Taking Ownership:** The mental act — "This is my problem now"
2. **Taking Action:** Bringing about a better result

### Examples
- Boy who breaks a window: Admission of fault ≠ responsibility; responsibility = paying for repair, cleaning up glass
- Father who creates a child: Biological admission ≠ responsibility; responsibility = providing resources, time, protection, love

### Locus of Control

| Type | Belief | Result |
|------|--------|--------|
| **Internal Locus** | Primary cause of own successes/failures | Learning, adapting, growing |
| **External Locus** | Life determined by luck, system, others | Helplessness, resentment |

### Responsibility Extends Beyond Fault
You can take responsibility for problems you didn't cause (e.g., picking up trash in a neighborhood park).

### Freedom Through Responsibility
Taking ownership of choices, work, and commitments makes you the author of your own story.

### Action-Oriented Life
**Smallest Viable Action:** Identify the smallest action that moves you in the right direction and do it immediately. Action creates its own momentum.

## Chapter 11: Humility and the Pursuit of Excellence

The quiet acknowledgment that "I might be wrong."

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|

### Humility in Practice

- Courage to admit "I was wrong"
- Willingness to listen and consider others may know something we don't
- Beginning inquiry with "I don't know"

### The Pursuit of Excellence
Once you know what is good to do, you have a duty to do it well.

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|

**Excellence without humility** → arrogant and brittle

## Chapter 12: Beauty, The Polish of Responsibility

### Definition of Beauty
**Beauty is the polished form after someone bears ownership and responsibility.**

It is the aesthetic signature of virtue in action — what happens when a responsible agent brings a piece of the world into benevolent, flourishing order.

### Rejected Views

| View | Problem |
|------|---------|
| "Beauty is in the eye of the beholder" | Shallow; ignores cross-cultural/historical consistency in what humans find beautiful |
| Beauty as "social construct" | Cannot account for visceral, pre-rational thrill of true beauty |

### TPS Grounding
Beauty is not the highest good itself, but the **aesthetic radiance of the good made manifest** — a sign that life is thriving.

### Examples of Beauty as Responsibility

| Domain | Beautiful = |
|--------|-------------|

Ugliness is the aesthetic of irresponsibility — visible signs of decay, of life diminishing rather than flourishing.

Creating beauty in your sphere of influence is a direct contribution to goodness.

# Part III: Contrasting Tiered Procedural Symmetry

## Chapter 13: Contrasting Symmetry

### Three Forms of Symmetry/Justice

| Form | Core Belief | Problem |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Value Symmetry** | Fairness = equal retribution ("eye for an eye") | Backward-looking; settles past scores; subjective application; cycles of vendetta |
| **Outcome Symmetry** | Fairness = identical results for all ("equity") | Requires unequal treatment; group identity over individual action; central authority intervention |
| **Procedural Symmetry** | Fairness = same rules for all | Works with human nature; encourages productivity; compatible with free, prosperous society |

### Critical Theory Framework
Outcome Symmetry draws from Critical Theory, which includes:
- Critical Race Theory, Critical Legal Studies, Feminist Theory, Queer Theory
- Postcolonial Theory, Critical Disability Theory, Fat Studies, etc.

### The Core Error of Outcome Symmetry
**Misinterpreting difference as injustice.**

| View | Injustice Exists When... |
|------|-------------------------|
| **Procedural Symmetry** | Different sets of laws for wealthy vs. poor |
| **Outcome Symmetry** | Laws don't favor the "oppressed" against the "oppressor" |

### Practical Implications

| Setting | Value Symmetry | Outcome Symmetry | Procedural Symmetry |
|---------|---------------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Courtroom** | Exact equal retribution | Blame socioeconomic forces | Did accused violate a clear law, determined by fair procedure? |
| **Classroom** | — | Eliminate advanced classes to force equal outcomes | Same high standards for all; allow each to achieve their level of excellence |

## Chapter 14: The Boundary of Reason

TPS must reject philosophies that deny reality's foundation. The difference is not degree but kind — irreconcilable.

### Two Non-Negotiable Principles for Debate

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|

| **External Consistency** | Standard for validating claims against reality must apply to all ideas |

**Without these, participants are not debating — they are performing separate monologues.**

### The Sealed System of Constructivism

| Rejection | Method |
|-----------|--------|
| **External Consistency** | Adopts all-encompassing, unfalsifiable interpretive lens; forces all data through single lens; incapable of correction by reality |

### Example: Contradictory Standards
- Hurtful speech from "oppressor" group → labeled "violence"

### Word Redefinition
The belief that all social meaning is "social construct" allows hijacking moral authority of respected words while replacing meaning with political one.

### The Prison of False Equivalence
Minor social slights and catastrophic injustices placed on same continuum of "harm."

### TPS Response
**Refuse to enter the simulation.** Pull conversation back to reality and first principles. This is not retreat from engagement — it's refusal to participate in a rigged game.

## Chapter 15: Discovered vs. Constructed

### The Analytical Tool: Convention vs. Consequence

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Convention** | Specific, often arbitrary form an institution takes (product of culture, history, circumstance) |
| **Consequence** | Underlying, non-arbitrary function discovered as emergent solution to a problem imposed by reality |

### Examples

| Domain | Convention | Consequence |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| **Driving** | Right side (US) vs. left side (UK) | Need for shared, predictable rules to prevent collisions |
| **Language** | Specific words ("cat" vs. "gato") | Need for stable, shared labels for complex cooperation |
| **Money** | Shells, gold, paper, digital entries | Solution to "double coincidence of wants" in barter |
| **Family** | Specific roles/structures | Biological reality of sexual reproduction and helpless offspring requiring long-term investment |

### The Fundamental Error
Mistaking the arbitrary nature of conventions for the underlying function being arbitrary.

### The TPS Method
When seeing a social convention, ask: *"What real-world problem does this solve, and how well does it help people live and continue well?"*

### Competence vs. "Different Ways of Knowing"
Reality provides feedback on which methods work. Competence is the demonstrable ability to achieve desired outcomes in the real world.

### Misplaced Loyalty in Public Policy
- **Crime:** Empathy for criminal's background over community safety
- **Public Order:** Empathy for addict over community well-being
- **National Borders:** Abstract empathy over national security

**Error:** Empathy detached from tiered responsibility becomes destructive.

## Chapter 17: The Rule of Law, Not of Men

### Two Alternatives

| System | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Rule by Man** | Power arbitrary; judgments by personal will; guarantees abuse, corruption, unpredictability |
| **Rule of Law** | Stable, public, generally applicable laws; law applies to everyone, including rulers; predictable framework |

### Rule of Law as Institutional Procedural Symmetry
The government is subject to law, not above it. This provides predictability for planning lives, businesses, and commitments.

### Distinctions Must Be Objective
Rule of Law doesn't mean treating every person identically. Distinctions must be grounded in objective, real-world differences relevant to law's just purpose.

### Selective Application = Rule by Man
When prosecutors pursue one political party for crimes while ignoring the same crimes from their own party, this IS Rule by Man.

### Empathy vs. Mercy

| Concept | Application |
|---------|-------------|
| **Empathy** | Powerful in close relationships; dangerous as guide for national policy |
| **Mercy** | Conscious judgment to waive justly-applied consequence AFTER verdict; structured exception that reinforces legitimacy |

### The Complete Framework
- Tiered responsibility without procedural symmetry → corrupt tribalism
- Judgment without humility → tyranny
- Rule of Law, protected by principled judgment → complete, interlocking framework for reason, responsibility, realism
